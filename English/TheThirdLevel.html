Theme & Sub-Theme

Theme: Escapism and the Intersection of Time and Reality

The central theme of "The Third Level" is the innate human desire to escape the harsh realities of modern life—its "insecurity, fear, war, worry and all the rest of it." The story explores this through the protagonist, Charley, who seeks refuge in a romanticized, peaceful past. The "third level" at Grand Central Station serves as a portal, blurring the lines between reality, imagination, and time, questioning whether it's a literal place or a psychological "waking-dream wish fulfillment."

Sub-Themes:

  • Nostalgia and the Lure of the Past: The story highlights a deep nostalgia for a simpler, more peaceful time, represented by Galesburg, Illinois, in 1894—a world before the two World Wars.
  • Fantasy vs. Reality: The narrative deliberately leaves the existence of the third level ambiguous, forcing the reader to question whether Charley's experience is real or a product of his unhappy mind.
  • Modern-day Anxiety: The story is a commentary on the stress and anxiety of contemporary urban life, which pushes individuals to seek out "temporary refuges from reality" like hobbies or, in Charley's case, an entire alternate timeline.

Plot Summary

  1. Exposition: Charley, a 31-year-old New Yorker, claims to have been on the "third level" of Grand Central Station, even though officials and timetables state there are only two. He discusses this with his psychiatrist friend, Sam, who dismisses it as a form of escapism from the anxieties of the modern world, pointing to Charley's stamp collecting as another example.
  2. Rising Action: Charley recounts the experience. While rushing home one night, he gets lost in Grand Central's maze-like corridors and finds himself on a different, old-fashioned level. Everything—the gaslights, the spittoons, people's clothing, the locomotive—seems to be from the 1890s. He confirms the date is June 11, 1894, from a newspaper called The World.
  3. Climax: Filled with a desire to escape to the idyllic town of Galesburg, Illinois, Charley tries to buy two tickets for himself and his wife, Louisa. However, the clerk rejects his modern currency, calling it "fake," and Charley has to flee to avoid getting arrested.
  4. Falling Action: Charley withdraws his savings and exchanges them for old-style currency, but despite many attempts, he can never find the corridor to the third level again. His wife becomes very worried. Sometime later, his psychiatrist friend, Sam Weiner, suddenly disappears.
  5. Resolution: One night, while going through his stamp collection, Charley finds a "first-day cover" that shouldn't be there. It was mailed to his grandfather in Galesburg on July 18, 1894. Inside, he finds a letter from Sam, who writes that he has found the third level and is now living happily in Galesburg. The letter urges Charley and Louisa to keep looking for it. Charley later finds out that Sam had bought $800 worth of old-style currency, confirming his friend had made the journey.

Key Points

  • Charley: The protagonist and narrator, an ordinary man who feels overwhelmed by the modern world and yearns for a simpler past.
  • The Third Level: A physical and metaphorical gateway to the past. It represents a space where one can escape the anxieties of the present. Its existence remains ambiguous, blending fantasy with reality.
    • * Galesburg, Illinois, 1894: The idyllic destination Charley seeks. It symbolizes peace, tranquility, and a pre-war world free from modern stress.
  • Sam Weiner: Charley's psychiatrist friend who initially provides a rational, psychological explanation for the third level but ultimately becomes a believer and finds his own escape there. His journey validates Charley's experience.
  • Stamp Collecting (Philately): Introduced as a "temporary refuge from reality," it ironically becomes the source of tangible proof (the first-day cover from Sam) that the escape is real.

NCERT Textbook Solutions (Reading with Insight)

1. Do you think that the third level was a medium of escape for Charley? Why?
Yes, the third level was undoubtedly a medium of escape for Charley. This is supported by his own psychiatrist, Sam, who describes it as a "waking-dream wish fulfillment." Charley himself admits that the modern world is full of "insecurity, fear, war, worry," and his detailed, romanticized description of Galesburg in 1894 reveals his deep desire to escape to a simpler, more peaceful time. The third level provides a direct, physical pathway to this idealized past, making it the ultimate escape from the pressures of his present reality.
2. What do you infer from Sam’s letter to Charley?
Sam's letter serves as the story's climax and blurs the line between fantasy and reality. From the letter, we can infer that:
  • Sam, who initially dismissed the third level as a figment of Charley's imagination, was himself deeply unhappy with modern life.
  • He was so captivated by Charley's description of Galesburg that he actively sought out the third level himself.
  • He succeeded in finding it and traveled back to 1894, where he found the peace and contentment he was looking for.
  • The letter acts as validation for Charley, proving that his experience was real and not just a hallucination. It encourages Charley to continue his own search for escape.
3. ‘The modern world is full of insecurity, fear, war, worry and stress.’ What are the ways in which we attempt to overcome them?
People attempt to overcome the stresses of the modern world in various ways, often seeking a "temporary refuge from reality" as Charley does. Some common methods include:
  • Hobbies and Pastimes: Engaging in activities like stamp collecting, gardening, reading, painting, or listening to music provides a mental break from daily anxieties.
  • Entertainment: Watching movies, TV shows, and playing video games allows people to immerse themselves in different worlds and narratives.
  • Travel and Nature: Visiting new places or spending time in nature helps people disconnect from their stressful routines and gain new perspectives.
  • Nostalgia: Revisiting old music, films, or places from one's past can evoke feelings of comfort and security.
  • Mindfulness and Spirituality: Practices like meditation, yoga, or engaging in religious activities help manage stress and find inner peace.
4. Do you see an intersection of time and space in the story?
Yes, the intersection of time and space is the central premise of the story. The "third level" is a physical space within Grand Central Station (a specific point in space) that also functions as a portal to a different point in time (1894). Charley physically walks through a corridor in the present and emerges into the past. Sam's letter is another example: it is a physical object from the past (postmarked 1894) that travels through time to reach Charley in the present, bridging the temporal gap. The story suggests that time is not strictly linear and that different temporal planes can intersect in certain physical locations.
5. Apparent illogicality sometimes turns out to be a futuristic projection? Discuss.
This statement suggests that concepts that seem impossible or illogical today might be understood or achieved in the future through scientific advancement. The history of science is filled with examples: the idea of flying machines, instant global communication, or travelling to the moon were once considered pure fantasy.

In "The Third Level," the idea of time travel through a portal seems illogical by our current understanding of physics. However, the story can be seen as a "futuristic projection" or a work of science fiction that explores this possibility. It uses this "illogicality" not to predict technology, but to explore a deep psychological truth about the human desire to escape. The story posits that perhaps the boundaries of time and reality are more fluid than we believe, a concept that futuristic and speculative fiction often explores. What appears as a psychological delusion to Sam the psychiatrist turns out to be a physical reality he himself experiences, suggesting our rational frameworks may be limited.
6. Philately helps keep the past alive. Discuss other ways in which this is done...
Philately (stamp collecting) keeps the past alive by preserving small, dated artifacts that reflect the history, art, and politics of their time. Other ways to keep the past alive include:
  • Museums and Archives: They preserve and display artifacts, documents, and artworks from different eras.
  • Literature and Storytelling: Stories, whether historical fiction or oral traditions, pass down cultural memory and experiences through generations.
  • Photography and Film: Visual media provide a direct window into the past, capturing moments, people, and places.
  • Historical Monuments and Architecture: Buildings and monuments stand as physical testaments to the past.
The human tendency to constantly move between past, present, and future is natural. We learn from the past (through memory and history), live in the present (our immediate reality), and plan for the future (through hopes and ambitions). This mental time-travel allows us to build on experience, find meaning, and navigate our lives.
7. You have read ‘Adventure’ by Jayant Narlikar... Compare the interweaving of fantasy and reality in the two stories.
Both "The Third Level" and "Adventure" masterfully interweave fantasy and reality, leaving the reader to question the nature of the protagonist's experience.
  • Nature of the Shift: In "The Third Level," the shift is into the past (1894) through a supposed physical portal. The fantasy is rooted in nostalgia and psychological escape. In "Adventure," Professor Gaitonde shifts into a parallel or alternate present, a world where history (specifically the Battle of Panipat) unfolded differently. This fantasy is rooted in scientific concepts like catastrophe theory and alternate realities.
  • The Protagonist's Role: Charley actively seeks his fantasy world, driven by a desire to escape. Professor Gaitonde's shift is accidental and intellectual; he is a historian trying to understand the discrepancy he observes.
  • The "Proof": Both stories provide a piece of tangible "proof" that blurs the line between fantasy and reality. In "The Third Level," it is Sam's letter from 1894. In "Adventure," it is the torn-off page from the alternate-history book that Professor Gaitonde finds in his pocket upon his return. This physical evidence in both stories prevents the experience from being dismissed as a mere dream.
While "The Third Level" is a psychological and emotional exploration of escapism, "Adventure" is a more intellectual and scientific exploration of the nature of reality itself. However, both use the "interweaving" to challenge our conventional understanding of time and existence.

Extra Questions & Answers

Short Answer Questions

1. What did Charley's psychiatrist friend, Sam, think about the third level?
Sam believed that the third level was not a real place but a "waking-dream wish fulfillment." He diagnosed it as a form of escape for Charley from the unhappiness, stress, and insecurity of the modern world.
2. Describe the appearance of the third level at Grand Central Station.
The third level looked like a scene from the 1890s. It was smaller than the second level, had fewer ticket windows, and the information booth was made of old wood. The lighting was dim from open-flame gaslights, there were brass spittoons, and people were dressed in old-fashioned clothes with sideburns and fancy mustaches.
3. How did Charley confirm that he had travelled back in time?
Charley confirmed the time period by looking at a stack of newspapers at a newsboy's feet. The newspaper was The World, which hadn't been published for years, and the lead story was about President Cleveland. The date on the front page was June 11, 1894.
4. Why couldn't Charley buy tickets to Galesburg?
Charley couldn't buy tickets because he offered modern currency to the clerk. The clerk at the 1894 ticket counter did not recognize the bills and thought Charley was trying to "skin" him (cheat him). The money in 1894 was old-style, different in size and appearance.
5. What is a 'first-day cover'? How did one provide proof of the third level?
A 'first-day cover' is an envelope with a newly issued stamp, postmarked on the very first day of its sale, and mailed to oneself as a collector's item. The one Charley found provided proof because it was mailed from Galesburg, Illinois, on July 18, 1894, and contained a letter from his friend Sam, confirming he had found the third level.
6. Who was Sam Weiner and why is his disappearance significant?
Sam Weiner was Charley's psychiatrist friend. His disappearance is significant because it suggests that he, the man of logic and science who had initially dismissed the third level, came to believe in it and successfully used it to escape to 1894 himself.
7. Why did Charley's grandfather not need any 'refuge from reality'?
Charley believed his grandfather didn't need a refuge from reality because, in his day (the 1890s), "things were pretty nice and peaceful." It was a time before the World Wars and the intense stresses of modern urban life, so there was nothing significant to escape from.
8. What does Galesburg, Illinois, symbolize for Charley?
Galesburg symbolizes an idyllic, peaceful, and secure world for Charley. It represents his ideal of a pre-modern life with "big old frame houses, huge lawns, and tremendous trees," where people had ample time and lived without fear and worry. It is the ultimate destination of his escapist fantasy.

Long Answer Questions

1. The story "The Third Level" is a fascinating blend of realism and fantasy. Discuss how Jack Finney creates a believable narrative despite its fantastical premise.
Jack Finney masterfully blends realism and fantasy in "The Third Level" to create a compelling and believable narrative. He grounds his fantastical premise in a meticulously detailed and realistic setting, making the unbelievable seem plausible.

The story begins with a very real and relatable conflict: the stress and anxiety of modern urban life. Charley is presented not as a fanciful dreamer but as an "ordinary guy," whose feelings of being overwhelmed are universal. His visit to a psychiatrist is a realistic response to a strange experience. The setting of Grand Central Station is a real, iconic, and notoriously complex place, and Charley's description of getting lost in its corridors is something many commuters can relate to. This realism provides a solid foundation for the fantasy to emerge.

When Charley discovers the third level, Finney uses precise, sensory details to make the past come alive: the "open-flame gaslights," the "brass spittoons," the style of clothing, and the "Currier & Ives locomotive." These are not vague descriptions but concrete historical details that lend authenticity to the scene. The crucial detail of the newspaper, The World, with its specific date (June 11, 1894) and mention of President Cleveland, anchors the fantasy in historical fact.

Finally, the narrative cleverly uses the rational voice of Sam, the psychiatrist, to voice the reader's own skepticism, only to have him become a convert. Sam's eventual journey to the third level, confirmed by the tangible evidence of the first-day cover and the money he exchanged, serves as the ultimate bridge between fantasy and reality. It suggests that Charley's experience was not just a hallucination but a shared, accessible reality, making the entire premise both intriguing and believable.
2. "The Third Level" explores the theme of escapism. Do you think the story condemns or condones Charley's desire to escape? Justify your answer.
"The Third Level" presents a nuanced perspective on escapism, neither overtly condemning nor condoning it, but rather exploring it with empathy and understanding. The story suggests that the desire to escape is a natural human response to the overwhelming pressures of the modern world.

On one hand, the story presents escapism through a rational, modern lens via the psychiatrist, Sam, who initially labels it a "waking-dream wish fulfillment" and a symptom of unhappiness. This view might be seen as a mild condemnation, suggesting that Charley is not dealing with reality. Charley's failure to find the third level again after his first visit could also be interpreted as a sign that such escapes are fleeting and unsustainable.

However, the story's emotional core seems to condone, or at least sympathize with, Charley's desire. The world Charley seeks to escape to—Galesburg in 1894—is painted as a genuinely beautiful, peaceful, and desirable place. The modern world, in contrast, is described as full of "insecurity, fear, war, worry and stress." The narrative makes the reader want Charley to succeed. The ultimate twist, where the cynical psychiatrist Sam himself finds and embraces this escape, is the strongest argument in favor of it. Sam's letter is not a warning but an enthusiastic invitation. He describes the third level as "worth it," implying that the escape has brought him genuine happiness and peace. By allowing the escape to be real and fulfilling for Sam, the story validates the desire to flee a harsh reality in search of a better world, suggesting that sometimes, the "refuge from reality" is the only sane choice.
