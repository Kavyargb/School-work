Theme & Sub-Theme

Theme: Linguistic Chauvinism and Cultural Identity

The core theme of "The Last Lesson" is the pain inflicted upon a community when its language and culture are suppressed by a colonizing power. The story explores the idea that people often don't appreciate the value of their heritage—in this case, the French language—until they are faced with the prospect of losing it forever. It powerfully illustrates that language is not merely a tool for communication but the very essence of a culture's identity and a symbol of its freedom.

Sub-Themes:

  • Regret and Procrastination: A significant sub-theme is the regret that comes from taking things for granted. Franz, his parents, and the villagers all feel a deep sense of remorse for not valuing their language and education when they had the chance.
  • Patriotism and Resistance: The story showcases a quiet, dignified form of patriotism. M. Hamel's final lesson and his act of writing "Vive La France!" are powerful acts of defiance against the oppressive order.
  • The Role of Education: The story highlights the transformative power of education and the profound impact a dedicated teacher can have. The classroom becomes a symbol for the preservation of culture itself.

Plot Summary

The story, set during the Franco-Prussian War (1870-71), is narrated by a young boy named Franz from the French region of Alsace.

  1. Exposition: Franz is late for school and fears a scolding from his teacher, M. Hamel, especially because he hasn't studied the rules for participles. He notices the day is unusually warm and bright, and he passes the town hall where a crowd is gathered before the bulletin board, a source of bad news for the past two years.
  2. Rising Action: Upon reaching the school, Franz is surprised by the profound silence, unlike the usual noisy bustle. He enters the classroom to find his classmates already seated, the village elders occupying the back benches, and M. Hamel dressed in his finest clothes, which he wore only on special occasions. M. Hamel kindly lets Franz take his seat and then makes a shocking announcement: an order has come from Berlin that only German will be taught in the schools of Alsace and Lorraine, and this is to be their last French lesson.
  3. Climax: The announcement acts as a "thunderclap" for Franz. He is suddenly filled with immense regret for having neglected his lessons. His books, once a burden, now seem like old friends he cannot part with. He sees M. Hamel in a new light, understanding his strictness and appreciating his forty years of faithful service. M. Hamel delivers a passionate, final lesson on the beauty and importance of the French language, calling it the "key to their prison." The entire classroom is filled with a somber, emotional atmosphere as everyone, young and old, tries to soak in the final moments of their linguistic heritage.
  4. Falling Action: As the church clock strikes twelve, the sound of the Prussian soldiers' trumpets is heard. M. Hamel, pale and overwhelmed with emotion, tries to speak but is choked up.
  5. Resolution: Unable to utter a word, M. Hamel turns to the blackboard and, with all his might, writes in large letters: "Vive La France!" ("Long Live France!"). He then leans against the wall and dismisses the class with a silent gesture of his hand.

Key Points

  • Setting: The story is set in Alsace, France, during the Franco-Prussian War, after the region was annexed by Prussia (led by Germany).
  • The Order from Berlin: The central conflict is triggered by an official order to replace French with German as the medium of instruction in schools.
  • Franz's Transformation: The protagonist, Franz, transforms from a carefree, lesson-shirking boy into a sensitive, regretful student who suddenly understands the value of his language and culture.
  • M. Hamel's Patriotism: The teacher, M. Hamel, emerges as a patriotic figure. His final lesson is not just about grammar but about national identity, pride, and the importance of holding on to one's language as a form of resistance.
  • The Villagers' Presence: The attendance of the village elders symbolizes the collective regret of the community for not prioritizing their language and education. It is their way of paying respect to their teacher and their lost country.
  • Symbolism of Language: The French language is symbolized as the "key to the prison" of enslavement. It represents the soul of the nation and the hope for future freedom.

NCERT Textbook Solutions

1. Infer their meaning from the context (p. 2)

  • in great dread of: To be in extreme fear or apprehension of something. Franz was terrified of the scolding he expected from M. Hamel.
  • counted on: To rely or depend on something. Franz had hoped to use the usual noise and commotion to sneak into his seat unnoticed.
  • thumbed at the edges: Refers to a book whose pages are worn out and dirty at the edges from being handled frequently over a long time. This described the old primer Hauser brought.
  • in unison: All together, at the same time. The students usually chanted their lessons loudly and in unison.
  • a great bustle: A lot of noisy activity and movement. This was the typical sound of the school when it began.
  • reproach ourselves with: To blame or criticize oneself for a fault. M. Hamel said that everyone, including himself, had reasons to feel guilty about neglecting the French language.

2. Think as you read (p. 7)

1. What was Franz expected to be prepared with for school that day?
Franz was expected to be prepared with the rules for participles, as his teacher, M. Hamel, had announced that he would be questioning the students on this topic.
2. What did Franz notice that was unusual about the school that day?
Franz noticed several unusual things:
  • The school was extremely quiet, like a Sunday morning, with no sound of opening/closing desks or lessons being repeated.
  • His teacher, M. Hamel, was wearing his special ceremonial clothes (a beautiful green coat, frilled shirt, and embroidered silk cap), which he only wore on prize days and inspections.
  • The back benches of the classroom, usually empty, were occupied by the village elders, including old Hauser, the former mayor, and the former postmaster.
3. What had been put up on the bulletin-board?
The bulletin board had an order from Berlin which stated that from the next day, only German would be taught in the schools of Alsace and Lorraine. This was the "bad news" that had drawn the crowd.

3. Think as you read (p. 8)

1. What changes did the order from Berlin cause in school that day?
The order from Berlin brought about profound changes:
  • The usual noisy atmosphere was replaced by a solemn, serious silence.
  • M. Hamel, instead of being cranky, was patient, gentle, and emotional. He taught his final lesson with immense dedication.
  • The students, including Franz, became very attentive and understood the lesson perfectly, realizing its importance for the first time.
  • The village elders came to the school to attend the last lesson, showing their respect for their language and their teacher, and regretting their own neglect.
2. How did Franz's feelings about M. Hamel and school change?
Franz's feelings underwent a complete transformation. Initially, he feared M. Hamel and disliked his "cranky" nature and iron ruler. He considered his books a "nuisance." However, upon hearing that it was the last French lesson, his feelings changed to shock, regret, and sympathy. He began to see M. Hamel as a dedicated patriot and felt sorry for him. His books suddenly became "old friends" he couldn't abandon. The school, which he often wanted to escape, became a place of profound significance.

4. Understanding the text (p. 8)

1. The people in this story suddenly realise how precious their language is to them. What shows you this? Why does this happen?
Several things show their sudden realization:
  • The villagers' presence: The elders of the village sit in the classroom to participate in the last lesson, something they had never done before.
  • Franz's regret: Franz, who earlier wished to skip school, is now devastated and calls his books "old friends."
  • M. Hamel's passion: M. Hamel's emotional and patriotic speech about the French language highlights its beauty and importance.
  • Everyone's emotion: The entire class, including old Hauser who is crying, listens with rapt attention and emotion.
This sudden realization happens because the freedom to use and learn their own language is being forcibly taken away. As is often the case, they only understood the true value of their linguistic heritage when they were about to lose it. The external threat made them conscious of their identity.
2. Franz thinks, “Will they make them sing in German, even the pigeons?” What could this mean?
This poignant thought can be interpreted in a few ways:
  • The Absurdity of Oppression: It highlights the sheer absurdity and overreach of the oppressors. Franz wonders if the Germans' control is so absolute that they would try to change the very laws of nature. It's a satirical comment on the futility of trying to erase a culture completely.
  • Language as a Natural Identity: It suggests that language is as natural to a human community as cooing is to pigeons. Forcing a new language on them is an unnatural act. The pigeons' song is a symbol of natural freedom that cannot be shackled by human orders.
  • A Form of Resistance: The thought itself is a form of mental resistance. While humans can be forced to comply, the natural world remains free, symbolizing a hope that the spirit of France, like the pigeons' song, will endure.

5. Talking about the text (p. 8-9)

1. “When a people are enslaved, as long as they hold fast to their language it is as if they had the key to their prison.” Can you think of examples in history...?
This statement by M. Hamel is profoundly true. A shared language fosters unity, preserves cultural memory, and acts as a tool for resistance. Historical examples include:
  • Poland under Russian and German Rule: Throughout periods of partition and occupation in the 18th and 19th centuries, the Polish people fiercely preserved their language through underground education and literature, which was crucial in maintaining their national identity and eventually regaining independence.
  • Catalonia in Spain: During the Franco dictatorship, the Catalan language was suppressed. However, it was kept alive in homes and secret gatherings, becoming a powerful symbol of Catalan identity and resistance, and was revived after the dictatorship fell.
  • Hebrew in Israel: The revival of Hebrew as a spoken language is a remarkable example. For centuries, it was primarily a liturgical language. The Zionist movement revived it as a modern, spoken tongue, which was fundamental to the creation of the state of Israel and unifying Jewish immigrants from around the world.
2. What happens to a linguistic minority in a state? How do you think they can keep their language alive? For example: Punjabis in Bangalore.
A linguistic minority in a state often faces challenges such as marginalization, lack of access to education in their mother tongue, and pressure to assimilate into the dominant culture. They can keep their language alive through several means:
  • Community Schools and Classes: Establishing weekend schools or community centers where children can learn to read, write, and speak their mother tongue.
  • Cultural Festivals and Events: Regularly celebrating festivals, music, and theatre that use and promote their language.
  • Family and Home Environment: Actively using the language at home as the primary mode of communication between generations.
  • Media and Literature: Supporting newspapers, magazines, websites, and TV channels in their language, and encouraging the creation of literature and films.
  • Advocacy: Lobbying for the inclusion of their language in the official school curriculum as an optional subject.
3. Is it possible to carry pride in one's language too far? Do you know what ‘linguistic chauvinism’ means?
Yes, it is possible to carry pride in one's language too far. When pride turns into an aggressive belief in the superiority of one's own language and culture over others, and is used to disrespect or suppress other languages, it becomes linguistic chauvinism.

Linguistic chauvinism is an excessive and unreasonable belief that your own language is better than all others. It can lead to intolerance, conflict, and the imposition of one language upon minority groups, as seen with the German order in "The Last Lesson." True appreciation for one's own language should foster respect for the linguistic diversity of others, not disdain.

7. Noticing form (p. 10)

Pick out five sentences from the story with the past perfect tense ("had said") and say why this form has been used.
The past perfect tense (had + past participle) is used to describe an action that happened before another action in the past. The story is narrated in the simple past, so the past perfect is used to refer to events that occurred even earlier.
  1. "M. Hamel had said that he would question us on participles." (The saying happened before the story's main events of that morning began).
  2. "For the last two years all our bad news had come from there..." (The bad news arrived before Franz passed the bulletin board that day).
  3. "...now I understood why the old men of the village were sitting there... It was because they were sorry, too, that they had not gone to school more." (Their not going to school happened in their past, long before the day of the last lesson).
  4. "It was in honour of this last lesson that he had put on his fine Sunday clothes..." (He put on the clothes before Franz arrived and noticed them).
  5. "All he said seemed so easy... I think, too, that I had never listened so carefully..." (His past listening habits occurred before this final, attentive lesson).

Extra Questions & Answers

Short Answer Questions

1. Why did Franz not want to go to school that morning?
Franz did not want to go to school for two main reasons. Firstly, he was late. Secondly, and more importantly, he was terrified because his teacher, M. Hamel, was going to test the students on participles, and Franz "did not know the first word about them."
2. What was the significance of the clothes M. Hamel was wearing?
M. Hamel was wearing his beautiful green coat, his frilled shirt, and a little black silk cap, all embroidered. This was his special ceremonial attire which he wore only on inspection and prize days. Its significance on this day was to mark the solemnity and importance of the last French lesson, treating it with the dignity of a special occasion.
3. How did M. Hamel blame the parents for the neglect of the French language?
M. Hamel blamed the parents for not being anxious enough to have their children learn. He said they preferred to put their children to work on a farm or at the mills to earn a little extra money, thus prioritizing financial gain over education.
4. How did M. Hamel blame himself?
M. Hamel also took responsibility for the students' neglect. He admitted to sending them to water his flowers instead of having them learn their lessons, and giving them a holiday whenever he wanted to go fishing.
5. What was the "thunderclap" that Franz received?
The "thunderclap" was M. Hamel's announcement that this was to be their last lesson in French. The order had come from Berlin to teach only German in the schools of Alsace and Lorraine. The news was a sudden, profound, and painful shock to Franz.
6. Why were the village elders sitting at the back of the classroom?
The village elders were there for multiple reasons. They were sorry that they had not gone to school more themselves. It was also their way of thanking M. Hamel for his forty years of faithful service and of showing their respect for the country and language that was theirs no more.
7. How did Franz’s attitude towards his books change?
Initially, Franz considered his books a "nuisance" and "so heavy to carry." After the announcement, his perspective shifted dramatically. His grammar and history books became "old friends" that he couldn't bear to give up, symbolizing his newfound appreciation for his heritage.
8. What did M. Hamel write on the blackboard at the end, and what did it signify?
At the end of the lesson, M. Hamel wrote "Vive La France!" ("Long Live France!") on the blackboard. It was a final, powerful expression of his patriotism and a defiant act of resistance against the Prussian occupation, signifying that while their territory might be lost, the spirit of France would endure.

Long Answer Questions

1. Discuss the character of M. Hamel as depicted in "The Last Lesson." How does he evoke sympathy and respect in the reader?
M. Hamel's character undergoes a significant transformation in the eyes of the narrator, Franz, and the reader. Initially, he is presented as a stereotypical strict schoolmaster, a "cranky" teacher with his "terrible iron ruler." However, the events of the last lesson reveal a much deeper, more complex personality.

On this final day, M. Hamel is patient, gentle, and profoundly sad. His decision to wear his ceremonial clothes shows his immense respect for the language and the occasion. He does not scold Franz for being late, a stark contrast to his usual behavior. His character evokes sympathy as we see his forty years of faithful service coming to an abrupt, painful end. The pain of leaving the place he has known for so long—his schoolroom, his garden, the walnut trees he planted—is palpable.

He evokes respect through his unwavering patriotism and his deep love for the French language, which he describes as "the most beautiful, the clearest, the most logical" in the world. His final lesson is not just about grammar but about identity and freedom. He is honest enough to blame not just the students and parents but also himself for neglecting the language. His final, emotional act of writing "Vive La France!" on the blackboard, when he is too choked up to speak, elevates him from a mere teacher to a patriotic hero, earning him the reader's ultimate respect and admiration.
2. "The Last Lesson" is more than just a story about a language lesson; it is a powerful commentary on the human tendency to procrastinate and take freedom for granted. Elaborate.
Alphonse Daudet's "The Last Lesson" masterfully uses the context of a simple classroom to explore the universal human weaknesses of procrastination and the failure to value what one has until it is lost. The story serves as a poignant reminder that freedom, culture, and identity are precious and must be actively cherished.

The theme of procrastination is evident in nearly every character. Franz regularly puts off learning his lessons, preferring to seek birds' eggs or go sliding. His regret is sharp and immediate when he realizes he has lost the opportunity to learn his own language forever. M. Hamel points out that this is the "great trouble with Alsace," where people say, "Bah! I've plenty of time. I'll learn it tomorrow." The parents, too, are guilty of this, having prioritized sending their children to work over sending them to school. Even M. Hamel admits his own fault in giving students holidays for his personal leisure.

This collective procrastination is a metaphor for taking freedom for granted. The people of Alsace had the freedom to learn, speak, and celebrate their language, but they did not fully embrace it. It is only when an oppressive external order is imposed that the entire community—Franz, the villagers, M. Hamel—is jolted into realizing the true worth of their linguistic heritage. The story powerfully argues that freedom is not a permanent state but a responsibility. The key to preserving this freedom, as M. Hamel suggests, lies in holding "fast to their language," which symbolizes their cultural identity and unity. Thus, the story transcends its historical setting to deliver a timeless message about the importance of being vigilant and appreciative of the freedoms we possess.
