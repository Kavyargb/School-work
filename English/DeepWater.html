Theme & Sub-Theme

Theme: The Conquest of Fear through Determination and Perseverance

"Deep Water" by William Douglas is an autobiographical account that explores the psychological grip of fear and the triumphant process of overcoming it. The narrative details how a childhood misadventure of nearly drowning instilled in him a deep-seated fear of water (hydrophobia). The central theme is that fear is a debilitating handicap, but it can be conquered with strong willpower, courage, and persistent effort. The story is a testament to the idea that true peace is found not in avoiding our fears, but in facing and mastering them.

Sub-Themes:

  • The Psychology of Fear: The story provides a vivid, first-person account of the experience of terror, paralysis, and near-death, offering deep insight into how fear can physically and mentally incapacitate a person.
  • Will to Live: Despite the overwhelming terror, the author's will to survive remains strong. This intensity of the will to live is what ultimately helps him conquer his fear.
  • Facing Fears Head-On: The narrative strongly advocates for confronting fears directly rather than avoiding them. Douglas's systematic approach to learning to swim and testing himself in various bodies of water is a powerful example of this.
  • The Meaning of Courage: The story aligns with Roosevelt's famous quote, "All we have to fear is fear itself." Douglas realizes that courage is not the absence of fear, but the victory over it.

Plot Summary

  1. Early Aversion to Water: The narrator, William Douglas, develops an aversion to water at age three or four when a wave at a California beach knocks him down and terrifies him.
  2. The "Misadventure" at the Y.M.C.A. Pool: At age ten or eleven, he decides to learn to swim at the Y.M.C.A. pool. One day, an 18-year-old bully throws him into the deep end. Douglas, despite his terror, rationally plans to push off the bottom and paddle to the edge. His plan fails repeatedly. He nearly drowns, experiencing stark terror, paralysis, and finally a peaceful acceptance of death before losing consciousness.
  3. The Aftermath and Lingering Fear: He is rescued, but the experience leaves him with a deep, haunting fear of water. This "handicap" stays with him for years, ruining his enjoyment of fishing, canoeing, and boating.
  4. The Decision to Overcome Fear: Determined to conquer his phobia, Douglas hires a swimming instructor. The instructor methodically "builds a swimmer" out of him, piece by piece, over several monthsâ€”teaching him to breathe, kick, and use various strokes.
  5. Confronting the Residual Fear: Even after the instructor's job is done, Douglas is not satisfied. He tests himself by swimming alone in the pool and then in various lakes and rivers, like Lake Wentworth and Warm Lake. He confronts the "tiny vestiges" of old terror each time, challenging it and swimming on.
  6. Triumph and Deeper Meaning: By swimming across Warm Lake and back, he finally conquers his fear completely. He reflects on the experience, stating that only those who have known "stark terror and conquered it" can truly appreciate the deep meaning of life and peace. He understands that the terror is not in death itself, but in the "fear of death." His will to live has grown in intensity, and at last, he feels "released."

Key Points

  • Autobiographical Narrative: The story is a personal account from Douglas's book Of Men and Mountains, which gives it a tone of authenticity and emotional depth.
  • Two Key Incidents: The fear originates from two childhood incidents: the wave at the beach and the near-drowning at the Y.M.C.A. pool.
  • The Instructor's Method: The instructor's systematic, "piece by piece" approach is crucial. It breaks down the overwhelming task of learning to swim into manageable steps, allowing Douglas to build confidence gradually.
  • The Psychological Battle: The core of the story is the internal, psychological battle against fear. Douglas personifies his fear as "Mr. Terror" and directly confronts it, showing his mental fortitude.
  • Thematic Quote: The story's central philosophy is encapsulated in President Roosevelt's quote: "All we have to fear is fear itself." Douglas's experience gives him a profound understanding of this statement.
  • Symbolism of Water: "Deep Water" symbolizes not just a physical element but also the deep, unknown fears that reside within the human psyche. Conquering the fear of water is symbolic of conquering any major fear in life.

NCERT Textbook Solutions

Understanding the text (p. 29)

1. How does Douglas make clear to the reader the sense of panic that gripped him as he almost drowned? Describe the details that have made the description vivid.
Douglas makes the sense of panic incredibly clear through a vivid, first-person, moment-by-moment account of his experience. The details that make the description so powerful include:
  • Sensory Details: He describes the "dirty yellow tinge" of the water, the feeling of his "lungs aching" and "head throbbing," and his eyes and nose coming out of the water but not his mouth.
  • Physical Helplessness: He details his "paralysed" legs hanging like "dead weights" and his futile attempts to "flail at the surface" and scream, but "only the water heard me."
  • Psychological Terror: He describes the "sheer, stark terror" as an entity that "knows no control" and "no understanding." He personifies it as a force that took an "even deeper hold on me, like a great charge of electricity."
  • Distortion of Perception: The "nine feet" of water felt "more like ninety," conveying his child's perspective and the intensity of his fear.
2. How did Douglas overcome his fear of water?
Douglas overcame his fear through a systematic and determined process:
  1. Decision and Action: He made a firm decision to conquer his fear and hired a swimming instructor.
  2. Gradual Learning: The instructor built his confidence "piece by piece." He started with a safety belt and pulley, then learned to exhale under water and inhale above it, then to kick his legs, and finally to integrate all parts into the crawl stroke.
  3. Self-Testing: After the instructor was finished, Douglas continued to test himself. He swam alone in the pool and then challenged himself in larger bodies of water like Lake Wentworth and Warm Lake, confronting the residual fear each time.
  4. Confrontation: He directly addressed his fear, personifying it as "Mr. Terror" and mentally challenging it, thereby asserting his dominance over it.
3. Why does Douglas as an adult recount a childhood experience of terror and his conquering of it? What larger meaning does he draw from this experience?
Douglas recounts this experience to illustrate the profound psychological impact of fear and the empowering nature of overcoming it. It is not just a story about learning to swim; it's a story about conquering a deep-seated phobia that had crippled a part of his life.

The larger meaning he draws is that the experience gave him a unique perspective on life and death. He says, "In death there is peace. There is terror only in the fear of death." Having experienced both the sensation of dying and the terror it can produce, his "will to live somehow grew in intensity." Conquering his fear was a liberating experience that allowed him to "brush aside fear" and fully enjoy life. The story serves as an inspirational message that with determination, any fear can be overcome.

Extra Questions & Answers

Short Answer Questions

1. What was the "misadventure" at the Y.M.C.A. pool?
The "misadventure" was when a muscular, 18-year-old boy playfully picked up the young Douglas and tossed him into the deep end of the pool. This act of bullying led to Douglas nearly drowning and developed into a lifelong fear of water.
2. What was the narrator's immediate emotional reaction to being thrown into the pool?
Initially, though he was "frightened," he was "not yet frightened out of my wits." He was able to think rationally and planned a strategy to push off the bottom and paddle to the side. The sheer panic and terror set in only when his plan failed.
3. What was the strategy Douglas planned to save himself? Did it work?
Douglas planned that when his feet hit the bottom, he would make a big jump, come to the surface, lie flat on the water, and paddle to the edge of the pool. The strategy did not work; his jump was not powerful enough, and he came up slowly and was unable to get his mouth out of the water.
4. How did the swimming instructor "build a swimmer" out of Douglas?
The instructor built a swimmer "piece by piece." He first used a belt and an overhead pulley to give Douglas confidence. Then he taught him how to breathe, how to kick his legs, and finally how to combine all these individual parts into an "integrated whole," like the crawl stroke.
5. How did the fear of water affect Douglas's life?
The fear of water became a "handicap" that "stayed with me as the years rolled by." It ruined his fishing trips and deprived him of the joy of canoeing, boating, and swimming, preventing him from fully enjoying the natural waters he loved.
6. What did Douglas do to make sure he had conquered the old terror?
To be sure he had conquered the terror, he went to Lake Wentworth in New Hampshire and swam two miles across it to Stamp Act Island. He also went to Warm Lake and swam across to the other shore and back, imitating a feat by his friend Doug Corpron.
7. What deep meaning did Douglas's experience have for him?
The experience had a deep meaning: he realized that "In death there is peace. There is terror only in the fear of death." By facing and conquering his fear, his will to live grew stronger, and he felt a sense of release and freedom from the psychological prison his fear had created.
8. Why was Douglas determined to get over his fear of water?
Douglas was determined to get over his fear because it had deprived him of the joys he cherished, such as fishing in the lakes of Maine and New Hampshire, and canoeing and boating in the Cascades. He did not want this "handicap" to control his life any longer.

William Douglas's "Deep Water" serves as a powerful guide on how to systematically overcome a deep-seated fear. The first crucial step is the conscious decision to confront the fear. For years, Douglas let his hydrophobia ruin his recreational activities. The turning point came when he decided he would no longer be a slave to it. This initial act of will is the foundation of any conquest of fear.

Secondly, Douglas understood his limitations and sought professional help by hiring an instructor.
