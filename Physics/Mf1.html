Introduction to Magnetic Field

If a charge $q$ is placed at rest near a metallic wire carrying a current $i$, it experiences almost no force. This suggests there is no significant electric field. However, if the same charge $q$ is projected with a velocity $\vec{v}$, it experiences a force, indicating the presence of a new kind of field. This field, which acts only on moving charges, is called the magnetic field ($\vec{B}$). The force exerted by a magnetic field is called magnetic force.

The magnetic force $\vec{F}$ on a charged particle is defined by the equation:

$$ \vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B}) $$

This is part of the more general Lorentz Force law, which includes both electric and magnetic forces: $\vec{F} = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B})$.

Properties of Magnetic Force:

  • The force is proportional to the magnitude of the charge $q$.
  • The force is proportional to the speed $v$ of the particle.
  • The force is zero if the velocity $\vec{v}$ is parallel to the magnetic field $\vec{B}$.
  • The force is always perpendicular to both $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$.
  • Because the magnetic force is always perpendicular to the velocity, it does no work on the particle. Consequently, it cannot change the particle's kinetic energy or speed.

The SI unit of magnetic field is the tesla (T). Another common unit is the gauss (G), where $1 \text{ T} = 10^4 \text{ G}$.

Relation between Electric and Magnetic Fields

Electric and magnetic fields are not independent entities. They are two aspects of a single entity called the electromagnetic field. A field that appears as purely magnetic in one frame of reference may appear as a combination of electric and magnetic fields in another frame moving with respect to the first. This is a key concept in the special theory of relativity.

Motion of a Charged Particle in a Uniform Magnetic Field

Since the magnetic force does no work on a charged particle, its speed and kinetic energy remain constant. The force only changes the direction of the particle's velocity.

Case 1: Velocity perpendicular to the magnetic field

If a particle with charge $q$ and mass $m$ is projected with velocity $\vec{v}$ perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$, it experiences a force of constant magnitude $F = qvB$. This force is always perpendicular to $\vec{v}$, acting as a centripetal force, causing the particle to move in a circular path.

The radius of the circular path is found by equating the magnetic force to the centripetal force:

$$ qvB = \frac{mv^2}{r} \implies r = \frac{mv}{qB} $$

The time period of the revolution is:

$$ T = \frac{2\pi r}{v} = \frac{2\pi m}{qB} $$

The frequency of revolution, known as the cyclotron frequency, is:

$$ \nu = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{qB}{2\pi m} $$

Notably, the time period and frequency are independent of the particle's speed and the radius of the orbit.

Case 2: Velocity not perpendicular to the magnetic field

If the velocity $\vec{v}$ makes an angle with $\vec{B}$, we can resolve it into two components: $v_{||}$ (parallel to $\vec{B}$) and $v_{\perp}$ (perpendicular to $\vec{B}$).

  • The parallel component $v_{||}$ is unaffected by the magnetic field, so the particle moves with constant velocity along the field direction.
  • The perpendicular component $v_{\perp}$ causes the particle to move in a circle in the plane perpendicular to $\vec{B}$.

The combination of these two motions results in a helical path. The radius of the helix is $r = \frac{mv_{\perp}}{qB}$ and the pitch (distance moved along the field in one revolution) is $p = v_{||}T = v_{||} \frac{2\pi m}{qB}$.

Magnetic Force on a Current-carrying Wire

A current-carrying wire placed in a magnetic field experiences a force because the moving charges (electrons) inside it experience a magnetic force.

The magnetic force $d\vec{F}$ on a small current element $i\, d\vec{l}$ is given by:

$$ d\vec{F} = i (d\vec{l} \times \vec{B}) $$

For a straight wire of length $l$ carrying current $i$ in a uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$, the total force is:

$$ \vec{F} = i (\vec{l} \times \vec{B}) $$

The magnitude of the force is $F = ilB \sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the wire and the magnetic field.

Torque on a Current Loop

A current loop placed in a uniform magnetic field experiences a torque that tends to align it with the field. For a rectangular loop of sides $l$ and $b$ carrying current $i$ in a field $\vec{B}$, the forces on opposite sides cancel out, resulting in zero net force. However, these forces can produce a net torque.

The torque on the loop is given by:

$$ \vec{\tau} = \vec{\mu} \times \vec{B} $$

where $\vec{\mu}$ is the magnetic dipole moment of the loop, defined as:

$$ \vec{\mu} = i \vec{A} $$

Here, $\vec{A}$ is the area vector of the loop, with its direction perpendicular to the plane of the loop, given by the right-hand thumb rule. If a coil has $N$ turns, the magnetic moment is $\vec{\mu} = Ni\vec{A}$.

The magnitude of the torque is $\tau = \mu B \sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the magnetic moment and the magnetic field. The torque is zero when $\vec{\mu}$ is parallel ($\theta=0^\circ$) or anti-parallel ($\theta=180^\circ$) to $\vec{B}$.

Example 34.1

Question

A proton is projected with a speed of $3 \times 10^6 \text{ m s}^{-1}$ horizontally from east to west. A uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$ of strength $2.0 \times 10^{-3}$ T exists in the vertically upward direction. (a) Find the force on the proton just after it is projected. (b) What is the acceleration produced?

Solution

(a) The velocity $\vec{v}$ is horizontal from east to west. The magnetic field $\vec{B}$ is vertically upward. The angle between $\vec{v}$ and $\vec{B}$ is $90^\circ$.

The magnetic force is $\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$. The direction of $\vec{v} \times \vec{B}$ is towards the south. Since the charge on a proton is positive, the force is also towards the south.

The magnitude of the force is:

$$ F = qvB\sin\theta = (1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) (3.0 \times 10^6 \text{ m s}^{-1}) (2.0 \times 10^{-3} \text{ T}) \sin(90^\circ) $$
$$ F = 9.6 \times 10^{-16} \text{ N} $$

(b) The mass of a proton is $m_p = 1.67 \times 10^{-27}$ kg. The acceleration is:

$$ a = \frac{F}{m_p} = \frac{9.6 \times 10^{-16} \text{ N}}{1.67 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}} = 5.8 \times 10^{11} \text{ m s}^{-2} $$

Example 34.3

Question

Figure shows two long metal rails placed horizontally and parallel to each other at a separation $l$. A uniform magnetic field $B$ exists in the vertically downward direction. A wire of mass $m$ can slide on the rails. The rails are connected to a constant current source which drives a current $i$ in the circuit. The friction coefficient between the rails and the wire is $\mu$. (a) What should be the minimum value of $\mu$ which can prevent the wire from sliding on the rails? (b) Describe the motion of the wire if the value of $\mu$ is half the value found in the previous part.

Solution

(a) The magnetic force on the wire of length $l$ is $\vec{F} = i(\vec{l} \times \vec{B})$. The direction of the current is along $\vec{l}$ and $\vec{B}$ is vertically downward. The force $F = ilB$ acts horizontally, perpendicular to the wire. Let's assume it acts to the right.

To prevent the wire from sliding, the force of static friction must be equal and opposite to this magnetic force. The maximum static friction is $f_{s,max} = \mu_s N = \mu_s mg$.

For the wire to be prevented from sliding, the minimum coefficient of static friction $\mu_{min}$ must satisfy:

$$ \mu_{min} mg = ilB \implies \mu_{min} = \frac{ilB}{mg} $$

(b) If the coefficient of kinetic friction is $\mu = \frac{\mu_{min}}{2} = \frac{ilB}{2mg}$, the wire will slide. The kinetic frictional force is $f_k = \mu mg = \frac{ilB}{2}$. This force opposes the motion.

The net force on the wire is:

$$ F_{net} = F_{magnetic} - f_k = ilB - \frac{ilB}{2} = \frac{ilB}{2} $$

The acceleration of the wire is:

$$ a = \frac{F_{net}}{m} = \frac{ilB}{2m} $$

The wire will slide with a constant acceleration of $\frac{ilB}{2m}$ to the right.

Question

An alpha particle is projected vertically upward with a speed of $3.0 \times 10^4 \text{ km s}^{-1}$ in a region where a magnetic field of magnitude 1.0 T exists in the direction south to north. Find the magnetic force that acts on the $\alpha$-particle.

Solution

An alpha particle has a charge $q = +2e = 2 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C} = 3.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}$.

The velocity $\vec{v}$ is vertically upward. The speed is $v = 3.0 \times 10^4 \text{ km s}^{-1} = 3.0 \times 10^7 \text{ m s}^{-1}$.

The magnetic field $\vec{B}$ is from south to north (horizontal). The magnitude is $B = 1.0 \text{ T}$.

The angle $\theta$ between $\vec{v}$ (upward) and $\vec{B}$ (north) is $90^\circ$.

The magnetic force is given by $\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$. The magnitude is:

$$ F = qvB\sin\theta = (3.2 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}) (3.0 \times 10^7 \text{ m s}^{-1}) (1.0 \text{ T}) \sin(90^\circ) = 9.6 \times 10^{-12} \text{ N} $$

For the direction, using the right-hand rule: $\vec{v}$ is up, $\vec{B}$ is north. $\vec{v} \times \vec{B}$ points to the west. Since the charge $q$ is positive, the force $\vec{F}$ is also directed towards the west.

Exercise 34.2

Question

An electron is projected horizontally with a kinetic energy of 10 keV. A magnetic field of strength $1.0 \times 10^{-7}$ T exists in the vertically upward direction. (a) Will the electron deflect towards right or towards left of its motion? (b) Calculate the sideways deflection of the electron in travelling through 1 m. Make appropriate approximations.

Solution

(a) Let the electron be projected horizontally along the positive x-axis, so $\vec{v} = v\hat{i}$. The magnetic field is vertically upward, so $\vec{B} = B\hat{k}$.

The magnetic force is $\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$. For an electron, $q = -e$.

$$ \vec{F} = (-e)(v\hat{i} \times B\hat{k}) = -e(vB)(\hat{i} \times \hat{k}) = -e(vB)(-\hat{j}) = evB\hat{j} $$

The force is in the positive y-direction. If you are looking in the direction of motion (x-axis), the positive y-axis is to your left. So, the electron deflects towards the left.

(b) First, find the electron's speed from its kinetic energy:

$$ K.E. = 10 \text{ keV} = 10 \times 10^3 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} = 1.6 \times 10^{-15} \text{ J} $$
$$ K.E. = \frac{1}{2} m_e v^2 \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times K.E.}{m_e}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \times 1.6 \times 10^{-15}}{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}} \approx 5.93 \times 10^7 \text{ m/s} $$

The force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so the path is a circular arc. We can approximate the horizontal velocity as constant for a small deflection. The time to travel a horizontal distance $x=1$ m is $t \approx x/v$.

The sideways acceleration is $a_y = \frac{F_y}{m_e} = \frac{evB}{m_e}$. The sideways deflection $s$ (or $y$) is:

$$ s = \frac{1}{2} a_y t^2 = \frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{evB}{m_e}\right) \left(\frac{x}{v}\right)^2 = \frac{eBx^2}{2m_e v} $$

An alternative way is using the kinetic energy: $m_ev = \sqrt{2m_e K.E.}$.

$$ s = \frac{eBx^2}{2\sqrt{2m_e K.E.}} = \frac{(1.6 \times 10^{-19})(1.0 \times 10^{-7})(1)^2}{2\sqrt{2(9.1 \times 10^{-31})(1.6 \times 10^{-15})}} \approx 0.0148 \text{ m} = 1.5 \text{ cm} $$

Exercise 34.3

Question

A magnetic field of $\vec{B} = (4.0 \times 10^{-3} \hat{k})$ T exerts a force of $\vec{F} = (4.0\hat{i} + 3.0\hat{j}) \times 10^{-10}$ N on a particle having a charge of $q = 1.0 \times 10^{-9}$ C and going in the x-y plane. Find the velocity of the particle.

Solution

Let the velocity of the particle be $\vec{v} = v_x \hat{i} + v_y \hat{j}$. The magnetic force is given by $\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$.

$$ \vec{v} \times \vec{B} = (v_x \hat{i} + v_y \hat{j}) \times (B_z \hat{k}) = v_x B_z (\hat{i} \times \hat{k}) + v_y B_z (\hat{j} \times \hat{k}) = v_x B_z (-\hat{j}) + v_y B_z (\hat{i}) $$

So, the force is $\vec{F} = q(v_y B_z \hat{i} - v_x B_z \hat{j}) = F_x \hat{i} + F_y \hat{j}$.

Comparing the components: $F_x = q v_y B_z$ and $F_y = -q v_x B_z$.

We are given: $q = 1.0 \times 10^{-9} \text{ C}$, $B_z = 4.0 \times 10^{-3} \text{ T}$, $F_x = 4.0 \times 10^{-10} \text{ N}$, $F_y = 3.0 \times 10^{-10} \text{ N}$.

Solving for $v_x$ and $v_y$:

$$ v_y = \frac{F_x}{q B_z} = \frac{4.0 \times 10^{-10}}{(1.0 \times 10^{-9})(4.0 \times 10^{-3})} = 100 \text{ m/s} $$
$$ v_x = \frac{F_y}{-q B_z} = \frac{3.0 \times 10^{-10}}{-(1.0 \times 10^{-9})(4.0 \times 10^{-3})} = -75 \text{ m/s} $$

Therefore, the velocity of the particle is: $\vec{v} = (-75 \hat{i} + 100 \hat{j}) \text{ m/s}$.

Exercise 34.4

Question

An experimenter's diary reads as follows: "a charged particle is projected in a magnetic field of $(7.0\hat{i} - 3.0\hat{j}) \times 10^{-3}$ T. The acceleration of the particle is found to be $(x\hat{i} + 7.0\hat{j}) \times 10^{-6} \text{ m s}^{-2}$". The number to the left of $\hat{i}$ in the last expression was not readable. What can this number be?

Solution

The magnetic force on a charged particle is given by $\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$. A key property of this force is that it is always perpendicular to the magnetic field $\vec{B}$.

Since $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$, the acceleration vector $\vec{a}$ must also be perpendicular to the magnetic field vector $\vec{B}$.

Two vectors are perpendicular if their dot product is zero. So, $\vec{a} \cdot \vec{B} = 0$.

Given:

  • $\vec{B} = (7.0\hat{i} - 3.0\hat{j}) \times 10^{-3}$ T
  • $\vec{a} = (x\hat{i} + 7.0\hat{j}) \times 10^{-6} \text{ m s}^{-2}$

Calculating the dot product:

$$ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{B} = (x\hat{i} + 7.0\hat{j}) \cdot (7.0\hat{i} - 3.0\hat{j}) = 0 $$

(The constant factors $10^{-3}$ and $10^{-6}$ can be ignored as the product must be zero).

$$ (x)(7.0) + (7.0)(-3.0) = 0 $$
$$ 7x - 21 = 0 \implies 7x = 21 \implies x = 3 $$

The unreadable number must be 3.

Exercise 34.5

Question

A 10 g bullet having a charge of $4.00 \mu$C is fired at a speed of 270 m s⁻¹ in a horizontal direction. A vertical magnetic field of $500 \mu$T exists in the space. Find the deflection of the bullet due to the magnetic field as it travels through 100 m. Make appropriate approximations.

Solution

We need to find the deflection due to the magnetic field, so we can ignore the effect of gravity.

The magnetic force is perpendicular to both the velocity and the magnetic field. Since the velocity is horizontal and the field is vertical, the force will be horizontal and perpendicular to the direction of motion. This will cause a sideways deflection.

Given:

  • Mass, $m = 10 \text{ g} = 0.01$ kg
  • Charge, $q = 4.00 \mu\text{C} = 4.0 \times 10^{-6}$ C
  • Speed, $v = 270$ m/s
  • Magnetic Field, $B = 500 \mu\text{T} = 5.0 \times 10^{-4}$ T
  • Distance travelled, $d = 100$ m

The magnitude of the magnetic force is $F = qvB$ (since $\vec{v} \perp \vec{B}$).

$$ F = (4.0 \times 10^{-6})(270)(5.0 \times 10^{-4}) = 5.4 \times 10^{-7} \text{ N} $$

This force causes a sideways acceleration $a = F/m$.

$$ a = \frac{5.4 \times 10^{-7} \text{ N}}{0.01 \text{ kg}} = 5.4 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m/s}^2 $$

We can approximate the forward velocity as constant. The time taken to travel 100 m is:

$$ t = \frac{d}{v} = \frac{100 \text{ m}}{270 \text{ m/s}} = \frac{10}{27} \text{ s} $$

The sideways deflection $s$ is given by the equation of motion:

$$ s = \frac{1}{2}at^2 = \frac{1}{2}(5.4 \times 10^{-5}) \left(\frac{10}{27}\right)^2 \approx 3.7 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m} $$

Exercise 34.6

Question

When a proton is released from rest in a room, it starts with an initial acceleration $a_0$ towards west. When it is projected towards north with a speed $v_0$, it moves with an initial acceleration $3a_0$ towards west. Find the electric field and the minimum possible magnetic field in the room.

Solution

Let the proton have charge $e$ and mass $m_p$.

Case 1: Released from rest ($\vec{v}=0$)

The force is purely electric: $\vec{F}_E = e\vec{E}$. The acceleration is $\vec{a} = \vec{F}_E/m_p = (e/m_p)\vec{E}$.

Given that $\vec{a} = a_0$ towards west, the electric field must be directed to the west, and its magnitude is:

$$ a_0 = \frac{eE}{m_p} \implies E = \frac{m_p a_0}{e} $$

So, $\vec{E} = \frac{m_p a_0}{e}$ towards west.

Case 2: Projected north ($\vec{v} = v_0$ north)

The total force is the Lorentz force: $\vec{F}_{total} = e\vec{E} + e(\vec{v}_0 \times \vec{B})$.

The total acceleration is $\vec{a}_{total} = \vec{F}_{total}/m_p = (e\vec{E}/m_p) + (e/m_p)(\vec{v}_0 \times \vec{B}) = \vec{a}_E + \vec{a}_B$.

We know $\vec{a}_E = a_0$ west. We are given $\vec{a}_{total} = 3a_0$ west.

Therefore, the acceleration due to the magnetic field is:

$$ \vec{a}_B = \vec{a}_{total} - \vec{a}_E = (3a_0 \text{ west}) - (a_0 \text{ west}) = 2a_0 \text{ west} $$

The magnetic force causes this acceleration: $m_p \vec{a}_B = e(\vec{v}_0 \times \vec{B})$.

We have $\vec{v}_0$ (north) and the resulting force direction (west). Using the right-hand rule for a positive charge ($\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$), if $\vec{v}$ is north and $\vec{F}$ is west, the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ must be directed vertically downwards.

The magnitude of the magnetic acceleration is $a_B = (e/m_p) v_0 B \sin\theta$. For minimum $B$, we need maximum $\sin\theta$, which is 1. This occurs when $\vec{B}$ is perpendicular to $\vec{v}_0$. Our downward direction for $\vec{B}$ satisfies this.

Magnitude calculation:

$$ a_B = 2a_0 = \frac{e v_0 B}{m_p} \implies B = \frac{2m_p a_0}{e v_0} $$

The minimum magnetic field is $\frac{2m_p a_0}{e v_0}$ directed vertically downwards.

Exercise 34.8

Question

A current of 2 A enters at the corner d of a square frame abcd of side 20 cm and leaves at the opposite corner b. A magnetic field $B=0.1$ T exists in the space in a direction perpendicular to the plane of the frame. Find the magnitude and direction of the magnetic forces on the four sides of the frame.

Solution

The total current is 2 A. Due to the symmetry of the square frame, the current splits equally into two paths: `d-c-b` and `d-a-b`. So, a current of $i=1$ A flows through each path.

Let's set up a coordinate system with the square in the x-y plane, and vertices at `d(0,L)`, `c(L,L)`, `b(L,0)`, `a(0,0)`, where side length $L = 20 \text{ cm} = 0.2 \text{ m}$. Let the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ be pointing out of the page ($\vec{B} = B\hat{k}$).

The force on a wire is $\vec{F} = i(\vec{l} \times \vec{B})$. The magnitude is $F=iLB = (1 \text{ A})(0.2 \text{ m})(0.1 \text{ T}) = 0.02 \text{ N}$ for all sides since the field is perpendicular.

Now, let's find the direction for each side, assuming current enters at d and leaves at b:

  • Path d-c-b:
    • Force on side dc: Current from d to c. $\vec{l}_{dc} = L\hat{i}$. $\vec{F}_{dc} = i(\vec{l}_{dc} \times \vec{B}) = i(L\hat{i} \times B\hat{k}) = iLB(-\hat{j})$. Force is 0.02 N downward.
    • Force on side cb: Current from c to b. $\vec{l}_{cb} = -L\hat{j}$. $\vec{F}_{cb} = i(\vec{l}_{cb} \times \vec{B}) = i(-L\hat{j} \times B\hat{k}) = -iLB(\hat{i})$. Force is 0.02 N to the left.
  • Path d-a-b:
    • Force on side da: Current from d to a. $\vec{l}_{da} = -L\hat{j}$. $\vec{F}_{da} = i(\vec{l}_{da} \times \vec{B}) = i(-L\hat{j} \times B\hat{k}) = -iLB(\hat{i})$. Force is 0.02 N to the left.
    • Force on side ab: Current from a to b. $\vec{l}_{ab} = L\hat{i}$. $\vec{F}_{ab} = i(\vec{l}_{ab} \times \vec{B}) = i(L\hat{i} \times B\hat{k}) = iLB(-\hat{j})$. Force is 0.02 N downward.

In summary:

  • Force on da: 0.02 N towards left.
  • Force on cb: 0.02 N towards left.
  • Force on dc: 0.02 N downward.
  • Force on ab: 0.02 N downward.

Exercise 34.10

Question

A wire of length $l$ carries a current $i$ along the x-axis. A magnetic field exists which is given as $\vec{B} = B_0(\hat{i} + \hat{j} + \hat{k})$ T. Find the magnitude of the magnetic force acting on the wire.

Solution

The wire lies along the x-axis, so the current element vector can be written as $\vec{l} = l\hat{i}$. The current is $i$.

The magnetic field is $\vec{B} = B_0(\hat{i} + \hat{j} + \hat{k})$.

The magnetic force is given by $\vec{F} = i(\vec{l} \times \vec{B})$.

$$ \vec{F} = i(l\hat{i}) \times B_0(\hat{i} + \hat{j} + \hat{k}) $$
$$ \vec{F} = i l B_0 [\hat{i} \times (\hat{i} + \hat{j} + \hat{k})] $$

Using the properties of the cross product: $\hat{i} \times \hat{i} = 0$, $\hat{i} \times \hat{j} = \hat{k}$, and $\hat{i} \times \hat{k} = -\hat{j}$.

$$ \vec{F} = i l B_0 [(\hat{i} \times \hat{i}) + (\hat{i} \times \hat{j}) + (\hat{i} \times \hat{k})] $$
$$ \vec{F} = i l B_0 [0 + \hat{k} - \hat{j}] = i l B_0 (-\hat{j} + \hat{k}) $$

The magnitude of the force is the magnitude of this vector:

$$ |\vec{F}| = |i l B_0 (-\hat{j} + \hat{k})| = i l B_0 \sqrt{(-1)^2 + (1)^2} = \sqrt{2} i l B_0 $$

A current of 5.0 A exists in the circuit shown in the figure. The wire PQ has a length of 50 cm and the magnetic field in which it is immersed has a magnitude of 0.20 T. Find the magnetic force acting on the wire PQ.

The force on the wire PQ depends on its length, the current it carries, and the magnetic field.

Exercise 34.12

Question

A circular loop of radius $a$, carrying a current $i$, is placed in a two-dimensional magnetic field. The center of the loop coincides with the center of the field. The strength of the magnetic field at the periphery of the loop is $B$. Find the magnetic force on the wire.

Solution

The problem describes a two-dimensional, radially outward magnetic field, similar to the diagram provided (Figure 34-E4). The force on a small element of the loop $d\vec{l}$ is $d\vec{F} = i(d\vec{l} \times \vec{B})$.

Let's use a coordinate system where the loop is in the x-y plane, centered at the origin.

  • A small current element at angle $\phi$ is $d\vec{l} = a\,d\phi(-\sin\phi\hat{i} + \cos\phi\hat{j})$ (for counter-clockwise current).
  • The radial magnetic field at that point is $\vec{B} = B(\cos\phi\hat{i} + \sin\phi\hat{j})$.

Now, we compute the cross product:

$$ d\vec{l} \times \vec{B} = [a\,d\phi(-\sin\phi\hat{i} + \cos\phi\hat{j})] \times [B(\cos\phi\hat{i} + \sin\phi\hat{j})] $$
$$ = aB\,d\phi[(-\sin\phi\sin\phi)(\hat{i}\times\hat{j}) + (\cos\phi\cos\phi)(\hat{j}\times\hat{i})] $$
$$ = aB\,d\phi[-\sin^2\phi(\hat{k}) - \cos^2\phi(\hat{k})] = -aB\,d\phi(\sin^2\phi + \cos^2\phi)\hat{k} = -aB\,d\phi\,\hat{k} $$

The force element is $d\vec{F} = i(d\vec{l} \times \vec{B}) = -iaB\,d\phi\,\hat{k}$.

The force on every element is in the same direction ($-\hat{k}$, or into the page). To find the total force, we integrate around the loop:

$$ \vec{F} = \int_{0}^{2\pi} d\vec{F} = \int_{0}^{2\pi} -iaB\,\hat{k}\,d\phi = -iaB\hat{k} \int_{0}^{2\pi} d\phi = -iaB(2\pi)\hat{k} $$

The magnitude of the force is $F = 2\pi a i B$. The direction is perpendicular to the plane of the loop, pointing inwards (assuming counter-clockwise current).

Exercise 34.13

Question

A hypothetical magnetic field existing in a region is given by $\vec{B} = B_0 \vec{e}_r$, where $\vec{e}_r$ denotes the unit vector along the radial direction from the origin. A circular loop of radius $a$, carrying a current $i$, is placed with its plane parallel to the x-y plane and its centre at $(0, 0, d)$. Find the magnitude of the magnetic force acting on the loop.

Solution

The field is spherically radial from the origin: $\vec{B} = B_0 \vec{e}_r = B_0 \frac{\vec{R}}{|\vec{R}|}$, where $\vec{R}$ is the position vector from the origin.

A point on the loop can be parameterized by angle $\phi$: $\vec{R} = a\cos\phi\hat{i} + a\sin\phi\hat{j} + d\hat{k}$.

The distance from the origin to any point on the loop is constant: $|\vec{R}| = \sqrt{(a\cos\phi)^2 + (a\sin\phi)^2 + d^2} = \sqrt{a^2+d^2}$.

So the magnetic field on the loop is: $\vec{B} = \frac{B_0}{\sqrt{a^2+d^2}}(a\cos\phi\hat{i} + a\sin\phi\hat{j} + d\hat{k})$.

The current element vector is $d\vec{l} = a\,d\phi(-\sin\phi\hat{i} + \cos\phi\hat{j})$.

The force on this element is $d\vec{F} = i(d\vec{l} \times \vec{B})$. Let's compute the cross product:

$$ d\vec{l} \times \vec{B} = \frac{aB_0 d\phi}{\sqrt{a^2+d^2}} (-\sin\phi\hat{i} + \cos\phi\hat{j}) \times (a\cos\phi\hat{i} + a\sin\phi\hat{j} + d\hat{k}) $$

Breaking it down:

  • $\hat{i}$ component: $(\cos\phi\hat{j}) \times (d\hat{k}) = d\cos\phi\hat{i}$
  • $\hat{j}$ component: $(-\sin\phi\hat{i}) \times (d\hat{k}) = d\sin\phi\hat{j}$
  • $\hat{k}$ component: $(-\sin\phi\hat{i})\times(a\sin\phi\hat{j}) + (\cos\phi\hat{j})\times(a\cos\phi\hat{i}) = -a\sin^2\phi\hat{k} - a\cos^2\phi\hat{k} = -a\hat{k}$

So, $d\vec{F} = \frac{iaB_0 d\phi}{\sqrt{a^2+d^2}} (d\cos\phi\hat{i} + d\sin\phi\hat{j} - a\hat{k})$.

To find the total force, we integrate from $\phi=0$ to $2\pi$. The integrals of $\cos\phi$ and $\sin\phi$ over a full cycle are zero. So the net force in the x and y directions is zero.

$$ F_x = F_y = 0 $$

The z-component remains:

$$ F_z = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{iaB_0}{\sqrt{a^2+d^2}} (-a) d\phi = \frac{-ia^2 B_0}{\sqrt{a^2+d^2}} \int_0^{2\pi} d\phi = \frac{-2\pi i a^2 B_0}{\sqrt{a^2+d^2}} $$

The magnitude of the force is the absolute value of this z-component:

$$ F = \frac{2\pi i a^2 B_0}{\sqrt{a^2+d^2}} $$

Exercise 34.15

Question

A current loop of arbitrary shape lies in a uniform magnetic field $B$. Show that the net magnetic force acting on the loop is zero.

Solution

The magnetic force on a small element of the current loop $d\vec{l}$ is given by $d\vec{F} = i(d\vec{l} \times \vec{B})$.

To find the total force on the entire loop, we must integrate this expression over the closed path of the loop:

$$ \vec{F}_{net} = \oint i(d\vec{l} \times \vec{B}) $$

Since the current $i$ is constant around the loop and the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ is uniform (constant in space), we can take them out of the integral:

$$ \vec{F}_{net} = i \left( \left(\oint d\vec{l}\right) \times \vec{B} \right) $$

The integral $\oint d\vec{l}$ represents the vector sum of all the infinitesimal length elements along a closed path. This vector sum is always zero for any closed loop, because the path starts and ends at the same point (the net displacement is zero).

$$ \oint d\vec{l} = 0 $$

Therefore, the net force on the loop is:

$$ \vec{F}_{net} = i (0 \times \vec{B}) = 0 $$

This shows that the net magnetic force on any closed current loop in a uniform magnetic field is zero, regardless of its shape.

Question

Prove that the force acting on a current-carrying wire, joining two fixed points a and b in a uniform magnetic field, is independent of the shape of the wire.

Solution

Consider a wire of arbitrary shape carrying a current $i$ from point a to point b in a uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$.

The force on an infinitesimal element $d\vec{l}$ of the wire is $d\vec{F} = i(d\vec{l} \times \vec{B})$.

To find the total force on the wire, we integrate this expression from point a to point b:

$$ \vec{F} = \int_{a}^{b} i(d\vec{l} \times \vec{B}) $$

Since the current $i$ is constant along the wire and the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ is uniform, we can take them out of the integral:

$$ \vec{F} = i \left( \left(\int_{a}^{b} d\vec{l}\right) \times \vec{B} \right) $$

The integral $\int_{a}^{b} d\vec{l}$ is the vector sum of all the infinitesimal length elements from a to b. This is simply the net displacement vector from the start point to the end point, let's call it $\vec{L}_{ab}$.

$$ \vec{L}_{ab} = \int_{a}^{b} d\vec{l} $$

So the total force is:

$$ \vec{F} = i (\vec{L}_{ab} \times \vec{B}) $$

This final expression for the force depends only on the current $i$, the uniform magnetic field $\vec{B}$, and the vector $\vec{L}_{ab}$ connecting the fixed endpoints a and b. It does not depend on the specific path or shape of the wire between these points. Therefore, the force is independent of the shape of the wire.

  • $B = 0.020$ T
  • $r = 5.0 \text{ cm} = 0.05$ m
  • $p = 20 \text{ cm} = 0.20$ m
  • Proton properties: $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C, $m = 1.6 \times 10^{-27}$ kg

Velocity component perpendicular to B ($v_\perp$):

$$ v_\perp = \frac{(0.05 \text{ m})(1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C})(0.020 \text{ T})}{1.6 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg}} = 1.0 \times 10^5 \text{ m/s} $$

Velocity component parallel to B ($v_{||}$):

$$ v_{||} = \frac{(0.20 \text{ m})(1.6 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C})(0.020 \text{ T})}{2\pi (1.6 \times 10^{-27} \text{ kg})} = \frac{0.20 \times 0.020}{2\pi \times 10^{-8}} \text{ m/s} \approx 6.37 \times 10^4 \text{ m/s} $$

Exercise 34.53

Question

A rectangular coil of 100 turns has length 5 cm and width 4 cm. It is placed with its plane parallel to a uniform magnetic field of 0.2 T. A current of 2 A is sent through the coil. Find the magnitude of the torque acting on the coil.

Solution

The torque on a current loop in a magnetic field is given by $\vec{\tau} = \vec{\mu} \times \vec{B}$, where $\vec{\mu}$ is the magnetic dipole moment of the loop. The magnitude is $\tau = \mu B \sin\theta$.

The magnetic moment is $\vec{\mu} = Ni\vec{A}$, where $N$ is the number of turns, $i$ is the current, and $\vec{A}$ is the area vector.

Given:

  • Number of turns, $N = 100$.
  • Length = 5 cm = 0.05 m, Width = 4 cm = 0.04 m.
  • Area, $A = (0.05 \text{ m}) \times (0.04 \text{ m}) = 0.002 \text{ m}^2 = 2 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2$.
  • Current, $i = 2 \text{ A}$.
  • Magnetic field, $B = 0.2 \text{ T}$.

The coil's plane is parallel to the magnetic field. The area vector $\vec{A}$ (and thus the magnetic moment $\vec{\mu}$) is perpendicular to the plane of the coil. Therefore, the angle $\theta$ between $\vec{\mu}$ and $\vec{B}$ is $90^\circ$. This orientation gives the maximum torque.

First, calculate the magnitude of the magnetic moment:

$$ \mu = NiA = (100)(2 \text{ A})(2 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m}^2) = 0.4 \text{ A}\cdot\text{m}^2 $$

Now, calculate the magnitude of the torque:

$$ \tau = \mu B \sin\theta = (0.4 \text{ A}\cdot\text{m}^2)(0.2 \text{ T})\sin(90^\circ) = 0.08 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m} $$

Exercise 34.54

Question

A 50-turn circular coil of radius 2.0 cm carrying a current of 5.0 A is rotated in a magnetic field of strength 0.20 T. (a) What is the maximum torque that can be exerted on the coil? (b) In a particular position, the torque is half of this maximum value. What is the angle between the magnetic field and the plane of the coil?

Solution

The torque is given by $\tau = NiAB \sin\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the magnetic moment $\vec{\mu}$ and the magnetic field $\vec{B}$.

Given:

  • $N = 50$, $r = 2.0 \text{ cm} = 0.02 \text{ m}$, $i=5.0 \text{ A}$, $B=0.20 \text{ T}$.
  • Area, $A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.02)^2 = 4\pi \times 10^{-4} \text{ m}^2$.

(a) Maximum Torque

Maximum torque occurs when $\sin\theta = 1$, which means $\theta=90^\circ$. This happens when the magnetic moment is perpendicular to the field (i.e., the plane of the coil is parallel to the field).

$$ \tau_{max} = NiAB = (50)(5.0)(4\pi \times 10^{-4})(0.20) $$
$$ \tau_{max} = 200\pi \times 10^{-4} = 0.02\pi \approx 0.0628 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m} $$

(b) Angle for half maximum torque

We are given $\tau = \tau_{max} / 2$.

$$ NiAB \sin\theta = \frac{1}{2} (NiAB) $$
$$ \sin\theta = \frac{1}{2} \implies \theta = 30^\circ \text{ or } 150^\circ $$

Here, $\theta$ is the angle between the magnetic moment (normal to the coil) and the magnetic field. The question asks for the angle between the magnetic field and the plane of the coil. Let's call this angle $\alpha$.

The angle between the plane and the normal is $90^\circ$. So, $\alpha = 90^\circ - \theta$.

$$ \alpha = 90^\circ - 30^\circ = 60^\circ $$

The angle between the magnetic field and the plane of the coil is 60°.

Exercise 34.59

Question

Consider a nonconducting ring of radius $r$ and mass $m$ which has a total charge $q$ distributed uniformly on it. The ring is rotated about its axis with an angular speed $\omega$. (a) Find the equivalent electric current in the ring. (b) Find the magnetic moment $\mu$ of the ring. (c) Show that $\mu = \frac{q}{2m}l$ where $l$ is the angular momentum of the ring about its axis of rotation.

Solution

(a) Equivalent Current

The ring rotates with angular speed $\omega$. The time period of one revolution is $T = 2\pi/\omega$. In this time, the total charge $q$ passes any given point. The equivalent current $i$ is the charge passing per unit time.

$$ i = \frac{q}{T} = \frac{q}{2\pi/\omega} = \frac{q\omega}{2\pi} $$

(b) Magnetic Moment

The magnetic moment of a current loop is $\mu = iA$, where A is the area of the loop. For the ring, $A = \pi r^2$.

$$ \mu = \left(\frac{q\omega}{2\pi}\right)(\pi r^2) = \frac{q\omega r^2}{2} $$

(c) Relation with Angular Momentum

The angular momentum $l$ of the ring (treated as a point mass on the circumference) is $l = I\omega$, where $I$ is the moment of inertia. For a ring, $I = mr^2$.

$$ l = (mr^2)\omega $$

Now, let's find the ratio of the magnetic moment $\mu$ to the angular momentum $l$:

$$ \frac{\mu}{l} = \frac{q\omega r^2 / 2}{mr^2 \omega} = \frac{q}{2m} $$

This gives the relation:

$$ \mu = \frac{q}{2m} l $$

This ratio, $\gamma = \mu/l = q/2m$, is known as the gyromagnetic ratio for the orbiting charge.

Exercise 34.60

Question

Consider a nonconducting plate of radius $r$ and mass $m$ which has a charge $q$ distributed uniformly over it. The plate is rotated about its axis with an angular speed $\omega$. Show that the magnetic moment $\mu$ and the angular momentum $l$ of the plate are related as $\mu = \frac{q}{2m}l$.

Solution

We can find the total magnetic moment by integrating the moments of infinitesimal rings that make up the disc.

Consider a thin ring of radius $x$ and width $dx$.

  • Surface charge density: $\sigma = \frac{q}{\pi r^2}$.
  • Area of the ring: $dA = 2\pi x\,dx$.
  • Charge on the ring: $dq = \sigma\,dA = \frac{q}{\pi r^2}(2\pi x\,dx) = \frac{2q}{r^2}x\,dx$.

This ring rotates with angular speed $\omega$, so its equivalent current is $di = \frac{dq}{T} = \frac{dq \omega}{2\pi} = \frac{(2q/r^2)x\,dx \cdot \omega}{2\pi} = \frac{q\omega}{\pi r^2}x\,dx$.

The magnetic moment of this small ring is $d\mu = (di) \times (\text{Area of ring}) = (\frac{q\omega}{\pi r^2}x\,dx)(\pi x^2) = \frac{q\omega}{r^2}x^3\,dx$.

To get the total magnetic moment, we integrate from $x=0$ to $x=r$:

$$ \mu = \int_0^r d\mu = \int_0^r \frac{q\omega}{r^2}x^3\,dx = \frac{q\omega}{r^2} \left[\frac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^r = \frac{q\omega}{r^2} \frac{r^4}{4} = \frac{q\omega r^2}{4} $$

The angular momentum of the disc is $l = I\omega$. For a disc, $I = \frac{1}{2}mr^2$.

$$ l = \frac{1}{2}mr^2\omega $$

Now, let's find the ratio $\mu/l$:

$$ \frac{\mu}{l} = \frac{q\omega r^2 / 4}{(1/2)mr^2 \omega} = \frac{q/4}{m/2} = \frac{q}{2m} $$

Therefore, we have shown that $\mu = \frac{q}{2m}l$.

Exercise 34.61

Question

Consider a solid sphere of radius $r$ and mass $m$ which has a charge $q$ distributed uniformly over its volume. The sphere is rotated about a diameter with an angular speed $\omega$. Show that the magnetic moment $\mu$ and the angular momentum $l$ of the sphere are related as $\mu = \frac{q}{2m}l$.

Solution

We can find the total magnetic moment by integrating the moments of infinitesimal discs that make up the sphere.

Consider a thin disc of thickness $dz$ at a distance $z$ from the center. Its radius is $x = \sqrt{r^2-z^2}$.

  • Volume charge density: $\rho = \frac{q}{(4/3)\pi r^3}$.
  • Volume of the disc: $dV = (\pi x^2)dz = \pi(r^2-z^2)dz$.
  • Charge on the disc: $dq = \rho\,dV = \frac{3q}{4\pi r^3}\pi(r^2-z^2)dz = \frac{3q}{4r^3}(r^2-z^2)dz$.

From the previous problem (Ex 34.60), we know the magnetic moment of a rotating disc is $\frac{(\text{charge})\omega(\text{radius})^2}{4}$. For our elemental disc:

$$ d\mu = \frac{dq \cdot \omega \cdot x^2}{4} = \frac{\omega(r^2-z^2)}{4} \left(\frac{3q}{4r^3}(r^2-z^2)dz\right) = \frac{3q\omega}{16r^3}(r^2-z^2)^2 dz $$

To get the total magnetic moment, we integrate from $z=-r$ to $z=r$:

$$ \mu = \int_{-r}^{r} d\mu = \frac{3q\omega}{16r^3} \int_{-r}^{r} (r^4 - 2r^2z^2 + z^4) dz $$

The integral is an even function, so we can write:

$$ \int_{-r}^{r} \dots dz = 2 \int_{0}^{r} (r^4 - 2r^2z^2 + z^4) dz = 2\left[r^4z - \frac{2r^2z^3}{3} + \frac{z^5}{5}\right]_0^r = 2\left(r^5 - \frac{2r^5}{3} + \frac{r^5}{5}\right) = 2r^5\left(\frac{15-10+3}{15}\right) = \frac{16r^5}{15} $$

Substituting this back into the expression for $\mu$:

$$ \mu = \frac{3q\omega}{16r^3} \left(\frac{16r^5}{15}\right) = \frac{q\omega r^2}{5} $$

The angular momentum of a solid sphere is $l = I\omega$. For a sphere, $I = \frac{2}{5}mr^2$.

$$ l = \frac{2}{5}mr^2\omega $$

Now, let's find the ratio $\mu/l$:

$$ \frac{\mu}{l} = \frac{q\omega r^2 / 5}{(2/5)mr^2 \omega} = \frac{q/5}{2m/5} = \frac{q}{2m} $$

Therefore, we have shown that $\mu = \frac{q}{2m}l$.
