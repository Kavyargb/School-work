Introduction to Potential Energy

In mechanics (Class XI), potential energy is defined as the work done by an external force to move a body against a conservative force (like spring or gravitational force). This work gets stored as potential energy.

The Coulomb force between two stationary charges is also a conservative force. Therefore, we can define electrostatic potential energy for charges in an electrostatic field.

When an external force $F_{ext}$ moves a charge $q$ from point R to point P against an electric force $F_E$ (such that $F_{ext} = -F_E$), the work done by the external force is stored as potential energy:

$$ W_{RP} = \int_R^P \vec{F}_{ext} \cdot d\vec{r} = - \int_R^P \vec{F}_E \cdot d\vec{r} $$

This work done equals the change in potential energy:

$$ \Delta U = U_P - U_R = W_{RP} $$

Two key remarks:

  1. The work done by an electrostatic field depends only on the initial and final positions, not on the path taken. This is the fundamental characteristic of a conservative force.
  2. Potential energy is defined up to an additive constant. By convention, electrostatic potential energy is often taken as zero at infinity. With this choice, the potential energy of a charge $q$ at a point P is the work done by an external force in bringing the charge $q$ from infinity to that point P ($U_P = W_{\infty P}$).

Electrostatic Potential

Electrostatic potential (V) at a point in an electric field is defined as the work done by an external force in bringing a unit positive test charge from infinity to that point, without acceleration.

It is the potential energy per unit charge:

$$ V_P - V_R = \frac{U_P - U_R}{q} = \frac{W_{RP}}{q} $$

If we choose $V_R = 0$ at infinity, then the electrostatic potential at point P is:

$$ V_P = \frac{W_{\infty P}}{q} $$

The SI unit of electrostatic potential is Joule per Coulomb (J/C), which is called a Volt (V).

Potential Due to a Point Charge

The electrostatic potential $V(r)$ due to a point charge $Q$ at a distance $r$ from it is given by:

$$ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r} $$
  • For a positive charge $Q > 0$, the potential is positive and decreases as $r$ increases.
  • For a negative charge $Q < 0$, the potential is negative and becomes less negative (increases) as $r$ increases.
  • This equation is consistent with $V=0$ at infinity.
  • Comparison with electric field: Electric field due to a point charge falls off as $1/r^2$, while electric potential falls off as $1/r$.

Potential Due to an Electric Dipole

An electric dipole consists of two charges $+q$ and $-q$ separated by a distance $2a$. Its dipole moment is $\vec{p} = q(2\vec{a})$, pointing from $-q$ to $+q$.

The electric potential at a point with position vector $\vec{r}$ (from the centre of the dipole) and making an angle $\theta$ with the dipole moment $\vec{p}$ is:

$$ V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{\vec{p} \cdot \vec{r}}{r^3} $$

For a point P at a large distance $r \gg a$ from the dipole, the potential simplifies to:

$$ V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p \cos\theta}{r^2} $$
  • On the axial line ($\theta = 0^\circ$ or $\theta = 180^\circ$): $V = \pm \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{p}{r^2}$. Positive along $\vec{p}$, negative opposite to $\vec{p}$.
  • On the equatorial plane ($\theta = 90^\circ$): $V = 0$.
  • The potential due to a dipole depends not just on distance $r$ but also on angle $\theta$.
  • The potential due to a dipole falls off as $1/r^2$, which is faster than the $1/r$ dependence for a single point charge.

Potential Due to a System of Charges

By the superposition principle, the total electrostatic potential at any point P due to a system of $n$ point charges ($q_1, q_2, \dots, q_n$) is the algebraic sum of the potentials due to individual charges:

$$ V(\vec{r}) = V_1(\vec{r}) + V_2(\vec{r}) + \dots + V_n(\vec{r}) $$
$$ V(\vec{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \left( \frac{q_1}{r_1} + \frac{q_2}{r_2} + \dots + \frac{q_n}{r_n} \right) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{q_i}{r_i} $$

where $r_i$ is the distance of point P from the charge $q_i$.

Potential due to a Uniformly Charged Spherical Shell

  • Outside the shell ($r \ge R$): The potential is the same as if the entire charge $q$ were concentrated at the centre.
    $$ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r} $$
  • Inside the shell ($r < R$): The electric field inside is zero. This implies no work is done moving a charge inside, so the potential is constant and equal to its value at the surface.
    $$ V(r) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{R} $$

Equipotential Surfaces

An equipotential surface is a surface on which the electrostatic potential is constant at all points.

Properties:

  1. No work done: No work is required to move a test charge from one point to another on an equipotential surface, since $W = q(V_P - V_R)$ and $V_P = V_R$.
  2. Electric field is perpendicular: The electric field $\vec{E}$ at any point on an equipotential surface is always perpendicular (normal) to the surface at that point. If it were not, there would be a component of $\vec{E}$ along the surface, and work would be done in moving a charge, which contradicts the definition.
  3. Never intersect: Two equipotential surfaces can never intersect each other, as this would imply two different potentials at the same point, which is impossible.
  4. Density of lines: Equipotential surfaces are closer together in regions of strong electric field and farther apart in regions of weak electric field.

Examples:

  • For a single point charge, equipotential surfaces are concentric spheres centered at the charge.
  • For a uniform electric field, equipotential surfaces are a set of parallel planes perpendicular to the field lines.

Relation between Electric Field and Potential

The electric field $\vec{E}$ and potential $V$ are related by:

$$ E = - \frac{dV}{dl} $$

where $dV$ is the change in potential over a small displacement $dl$ along the direction of $\vec{E}$.

This relation implies two key points:

  1. The electric field is in the direction in which the potential decreases most rapidly (steepest).
  2. The magnitude of the electric field is equal to the potential gradient (rate of change of potential with distance) in that direction.

Potential Energy of a System of Charges in an External Field

1. Potential Energy of a Single Charge in an External Field

If a charge $q$ is placed at a point $\vec{r}$ in an external electric potential $V(\vec{r})$, its potential energy is:

$$ U(\vec{r}) = qV(\vec{r}) $$

This is the work done by an external agency to bring the charge $q$ from infinity to point $\vec{r}$ against the external electric field.

The unit of energy frequently used in atomic and nuclear physics is the electron volt (eV):

$$ 1 \text{ eV} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ J} $$

2. Potential Energy of a System of Two Charges in an External Field

For two charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ located at $\vec{r}_1$ and $\vec{r}_2$ in an external potential $V(\vec{r})$, the total potential energy is the sum of:

  1. Potential energy of $q_1$ in the external field: $q_1 V(\vec{r}_1)$.
  2. Potential energy of $q_2$ in the external field: $q_2 V(\vec{r}_2)$.
  3. Potential energy due to interaction between $q_1$ and $q_2$: $\frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{12}}$, where $r_{12}$ is the distance between $q_1$ and $q_2$.

So, the total potential energy is:

$$ U = q_1 V(\vec{r}_1) + q_2 V(\vec{r}_2) + \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{12}} $$

3. Potential Energy of a Dipole in a Uniform External Electric Field

When an electric dipole with dipole moment $\vec{p}$ is placed in a uniform external electric field $\vec{E}$, it experiences a torque $\vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E}$ that tends to align it with the field. The potential energy $U$ of the dipole in the field depends on its orientation $\theta$ with respect to the field:

$$ U = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E} = -pE \cos\theta $$
  • Minimum energy (stable equilibrium) when $\theta = 0^\circ$ (dipole aligned with field). $U_{min} = -pE$.
  • Maximum energy (unstable equilibrium) when $\theta = 180^\circ$ (dipole anti-parallel to field). $U_{max} = +pE$.
  • Potential energy is zero when $\theta = 90^\circ$ (perpendicular to field), by convention.

Electrostatics of Conductors

Conductors are materials that contain mobile charge carriers (e.g., free electrons in metals). Key properties in electrostatics:

  1. Inside a conductor, electrostatic field is zero: In a static situation, free charges distribute themselves such that the net electric field everywhere inside the conductor is zero.
  2. At the surface of a charged conductor, electrostatic field is normal to the surface: If there were a tangential component, free charges would move along the surface, creating a current, which contradicts the static condition.
  3. The interior of a conductor can have no excess charge in the static situation: Any net charge given to a conductor resides on its outer surface. This follows from Gauss's Law ($E=0$ inside means $q_{enc}=0$).
  4. Electrostatic potential is constant throughout the volume of the conductor and has the same value on its surface: Since $E=0$ inside, no work is done in moving a charge within the conductor, implying zero potential difference.
  5. Electric field at the surface of a charged conductor: The magnitude of the electric field $\vec{E}$ just outside the surface of a charged conductor is directly proportional to the surface charge density $\sigma$:
    $$ \vec{E} = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} \hat{n} $$
    where $\hat{n}$ is the unit vector normal to the surface in the outward direction.
  6. Electrostatic Shielding: The electric field inside a cavity within a conductor (with no charges placed inside the cavity) is zero, regardless of the external fields or charges on the conductor. This phenomenon is used to protect sensitive instruments from external electric influences.

Dielectrics and Polarisation

Dielectrics are non-conducting substances (insulators) that have no free charge carriers.

When a dielectric is placed in an external electric field $\vec{E}_0$, the field induces dipole moments within the material, a process called polarisation. Unlike conductors, dielectrics do not allow charges to move freely to cancel the external field completely; instead, they reduce the net electric field inside them.

Types of Molecules:

  • Non-polar molecules: Molecules in which the centres of positive and negative charges coincide (e.g., O₂, H₂). In an external field, an induced dipole moment is created as the charges are displaced in opposite directions.
  • Polar molecules: Molecules in which the centres of positive and negative charges do not coincide, even in the absence of an external field (e.g., H₂O, HCl). They possess a permanent dipole moment. In an external field, these dipoles tend to align with the field.

In both cases, a net dipole moment is developed in the dielectric. The dipole moment per unit volume is called Polarisation ($\vec{P}$).

For linear isotropic dielectrics, $\vec{P}$ is proportional to $\vec{E}$:

$$ \vec{P} = \epsilon_0 \chi_e \vec{E} $$

where $\chi_e$ (chi-e) is the electric susceptibility of the dielectric medium.

The collective effect of these induced dipoles or aligned permanent dipoles is the appearance of induced surface charges ($\sigma_p$) on the faces of the dielectric. These induced charges create an electric field ($\vec{E}_p$) inside the dielectric that opposes the external field $\vec{E}_0$.

The net electric field inside the dielectric is reduced:

$$ \vec{E} = \vec{E}_0 - \vec{E}_p $$

The extent of this reduction depends on the nature of the dielectric.

Capacitors and Capacitance

A capacitor is a system of two conductors separated by an insulating medium (dielectric). When charged, the conductors have equal and opposite charges, say $+Q$ and $-Q$, and a potential difference $V$ between them.

The capacitance (C) of a capacitor is defined as the ratio of the magnitude of the charge on either conductor to the potential difference between them:

$$ C = \frac{Q}{V} $$
  • Capacitance is a measure of a capacitor's ability to store electric charge.
  • It depends on the geometrical configuration (shape, size, separation) of the conductors and the nature of the dielectric medium between them. It does NOT depend on $Q$ or $V$.
  • The SI unit of capacitance is the Farad (F). $1 \text{ F} = 1 \text{ C/V}$.
  • Commonly used smaller units are microfarad ($\mu$F = 10⁻⁶ F), nanofarad (nF = 10⁻⁹ F), and picofarad (pF = 10⁻¹² F). A Farad is a very large unit.
  • Dielectric Strength: The maximum electric field that a dielectric medium can withstand without breakdown (losing its insulating property) is called its dielectric strength.

The Parallel Plate Capacitor

A parallel plate capacitor consists of two large plane parallel conducting plates of area $A$ separated by a small distance $d$.

If there is vacuum (or air) between the plates:

  • The surface charge densities are $\sigma = Q/A$ and $-\sigma = -Q/A$.
  • The electric field between the plates is uniform and given by:
    $$ E_0 = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{Q}{\epsilon_0 A} $$
  • The potential difference between the plates is $V_0 = E_0 d = \frac{Qd}{\epsilon_0 A}$.
  • The capacitance ($C_0$) is:
    $$ C_0 = \frac{Q}{V_0} = \frac{Q}{Qd/(\epsilon_0 A)} = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d} $$

Effect of Dielectric on Capacitance

When a dielectric medium is inserted between the plates of a parallel plate capacitor (fully occupying the space):

  • The external field $\vec{E}_0$ polarizes the dielectric, creating an opposing field $\vec{E}_p$ due to induced surface charges.
  • The net electric field inside the dielectric is reduced to $E = E_0/K$, where $K$ is the dielectric constant (or relative permittivity) of the medium.
    $$ E = \frac{\sigma - \sigma_p}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{E_0}{K} $$
  • The potential difference across the plates is reduced to $V = E d = \frac{V_0}{K}$.
  • The capacitance $C$ with the dielectric is:
    $$ C = \frac{Q}{V} = \frac{Q}{V_0/K} = K \frac{Q}{V_0} = KC_0 $$
  • The dielectric constant $K$ is defined as the ratio of the capacitance with dielectric ($C$) to the capacitance with vacuum ($C_0$):
    $$ K = \frac{C}{C_0} = \frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon_0} $$
    where $\epsilon$ is the permittivity of the medium. $K \ge 1$.
  • Thus, inserting a dielectric increases the capacitance by a factor of $K$. For a parallel plate capacitor, $C = \frac{K\epsilon_0 A}{d} = \frac{\epsilon A}{d}$.

Combination of Capacitors

1. Capacitors in Series

When capacitors are connected in series, they are connected end-to-end. The following properties hold:

  • The charge (Q) is the same on each capacitor.
  • The total potential difference ($V$) across the combination is the sum of the potential differences across individual capacitors: $V = V_1 + V_2 + \dots + V_n$.
  • The equivalent capacitance ($C_{eq}$) for a series combination is given by:
    $$ \frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \dots + \frac{1}{C_n} $$
  • For two capacitors in series: $C_{eq} = \frac{C_1 C_2}{C_1 + C_2}$.
  • The equivalent capacitance in series is always less than the smallest individual capacitance.

2. Capacitors in Parallel

When capacitors are connected in parallel, their corresponding plates are connected together to common points. The following properties hold:

  • The potential difference (V) is the same across each capacitor.
  • The total charge ($Q$) on the combination is the sum of the charges on individual capacitors: $Q = Q_1 + Q_2 + \dots + Q_n$.
  • The equivalent capacitance ($C_{eq}$) for a parallel combination is given by:
    $$ C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 + \dots + C_n $$
  • The equivalent capacitance in parallel is always greater than the largest individual capacitance.

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

The energy stored in a charged capacitor is equal to the work done by the external agency in charging it. This energy is stored in the electric field between the plates.

The energy stored $U$ can be expressed in several equivalent forms:

$$ U = \frac{1}{2} QV = \frac{1}{2} CV^2 = \frac{1}{2} \frac{Q^2}{C} $$

The energy density (u), which is the energy stored per unit volume of space, in an electric field $\vec{E}$ is:

$$ u = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon E^2 $$

This expression is generally true for any electric field configuration.

Summary

  1. Electrostatic force is conservative. Work done by an external force in bringing a charge $q$ from R to P is $q(V_P - V_R)$, which is the potential energy difference $U_P - U_R$.
  2. Potential at a point is the work done per unit charge in bringing it from infinity. For a point charge $Q$ at the origin, $V(r) = Q/(4\pi\epsilon_0 r)$.
  3. For an electric dipole $\vec{p}$, $V(r, \theta) = (1/(4\pi\epsilon_0)) (\vec{p} \cdot \hat{r} / r^2)$ for $r \gg a$.
  4. For a system of charges, $V(\vec{r}) = \sum_{i=1}^n V_i(\vec{r}) = \sum_{i=1}^n (1/(4\pi\epsilon_0)) (q_i/r_i)$.
  5. An equipotential surface has constant potential. Electric field $\vec{E}$ is perpendicular to equipotential surfaces and points in the direction of steepest potential decrease ($E = -dV/dl$).
  6. Potential energy of a system of two charges $q_1, q_2$ at distance $r_{12}$ is $U = (1/(4\pi\epsilon_0)) (q_1 q_2 / r_{12})$.
  7. Potential energy of a charge $q$ in an external potential $V(\vec{r})$ is $qV(\vec{r})$. Potential energy of a dipole $\vec{p}$ in a uniform external field $\vec{E}$ is $U = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E}$.
  8. Key properties of conductors: $E=0$ inside; $E$ is normal to surface; no excess charge inside; potential is constant throughout volume and on surface; $E=\sigma/\epsilon_0 \hat{n}$ at surface. Electrostatic shielding.
  9. A capacitor stores charge ($Q$) and energy ($U = (1/2)CV^2$). Capacitance $C = Q/V$. Unit is Farad (F). Depends on geometry and dielectric.
  10. For a parallel plate capacitor in vacuum, $C_0 = \epsilon_0 A/d$.
  11. Introducing a dielectric of constant $K$ increases capacitance to $C = KC_0$.
  12. Capacitors in series: $1/C_{eq} = 1/C_1 + 1/C_2 + \dots$.
  13. Capacitors in parallel: $C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 + \dots$.
  14. Energy density in an electric field: $u = (1/2)\epsilon E^2$.

Points to Ponder

  1. Electrostatics deals with forces between charges at rest. When we talk of electrostatic force, it is assumed that each charge is kept at rest by some unspecified force that opposes the net Coulomb force.
  2. A capacitor confines electric field lines to a small region. Even if the field is strong, the potential difference between the conductors can be small.
  3. Electric field is discontinuous across the surface of a charged spherical shell (zero inside, $\sigma/\epsilon_0$ just outside). Electric potential, however, is continuous across the surface.
  4. The torque $\vec{p} \times \vec{E}$ on a dipole causes it to oscillate about $\vec{E}$. Dissipation aligns the dipole with $\vec{E}$.
  5. Potential due to a charge $q$ at its own location is not defined (it is infinite).
  6. In $qV(\vec{r})$ for potential energy, $V(\vec{r})$ is the potential due to external charges, not the potential due to $q$ itself.
  7. A cavity inside a conductor is shielded from outside electrical influences. However, if charges are put inside the cavity, the exterior of the conductor is not shielded by the inside charges.

NCERT Examples Solved

Example 2.1

Question

(a) Calculate the potential at a point P due to a charge of $4 \times 10^{-7}$ C located 9 cm away.
(b) Hence obtain the work done in bringing a charge of $2 \times 10^{-9}$ C from infinity to the point P. Does the answer depend on the path along which the charge is brought?

Solution

(a) Given $Q = 4 \times 10^{-7}$ C and $r = 9 \text{ cm} = 0.09 \text{ m}$.

The potential $V$ at point P due to a point charge is given by:

$$ V = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{r} $$

Using $k = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} = 9 \times 10^9 \text{ N m}^2/\text{C}^2$:

$$ V = (9 \times 10^9 \text{ N m}^2/\text{C}^2) \times \frac{4 \times 10^{-7} \text{ C}}{0.09 \text{ m}} = \frac{36 \times 10^2}{0.09} = 4 \times 10^4 \text{ V} $$

(b) The work done $W$ in bringing a charge $q = 2 \times 10^{-9}$ C from infinity to point P is $W = qV$.

$$ W = (2 \times 10^{-9} \text{ C}) \times (4 \times 10^4 \text{ V}) = 8 \times 10^{-5} \text{ J} $$

No, the work done will be path independent. This is because the electrostatic force is a conservative force, and work done against a conservative force depends only on the initial and final positions, not on the path taken.

Example 2.2

Question

Two charges $3 \times 10^{-8}$ C and $-2 \times 10^{-8}$ C are located 15 cm apart. At what point on the line joining the two charges is the electric potential zero? Take the potential at infinity to be zero.

Solution

Let $q_1 = 3 \times 10^{-8}$ C and $q_2 = -2 \times 10^{-8}$ C. The distance between them is $d = 15 \text{ cm} = 0.15 \text{ m}$.

Let the origin be at $q_1$. Then $q_2$ is at $x = 0.15 \text{ m}$. Let P be a point at distance $x$ from $q_1$ where the potential is zero. Potential at P is $V = V_1 + V_2 = 0$.

$$ \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_1}{x} + \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_2}{(d-x)} = 0 $$
$$ \frac{q_1}{x} = -\frac{q_2}{(d-x)} $$

Substitute values: $q_1 = 3 \times 10^{-8}$ C, $q_2 = -2 \times 10^{-8}$ C, $d = 0.15 \text{ m}$.

$$ \frac{3 \times 10^{-8}}{x} = -\frac{(-2 \times 10^{-8})}{(0.15-x)} $$
$$ \frac{3}{x} = \frac{2}{0.15-x} $$
$$ 3(0.15-x) = 2x $$
$$ 0.45 - 3x = 2x $$
$$ 0.45 = 5x \implies x = \frac{0.45}{5} = 0.09 \text{ m} = 9 \text{ cm} $$

This point is between the two charges (0 < x < 0.15).

There is another possibility outside the line segment. Let P be at a distance $x$ from $q_1$ (on the side of $q_2$). So $x > 0.15 \text{ m}$. The distance from $q_2$ to P is $x-d$.

$$ \frac{q_1}{x} + \frac{q_2}{(x-d)} = 0 $$
$$ \frac{3 \times 10^{-8}}{x} + \frac{(-2 \times 10^{-8})}{(x-0.15)} = 0 $$
$$ \frac{3}{x} = \frac{2}{x-0.15} $$
$$ 3(x-0.15) = 2x $$
$$ 3x - 0.45 = 2x $$
$$ x = 0.45 \text{ m} = 45 \text{ cm} $$

So, electric potential is zero at 9 cm from the positive charge (between the charges) and at 45 cm from the positive charge (on the side of the negative charge).

Example 2.4

Question

Four charges are arranged at the corners of a square ABCD of side d, as shown in Fig. 2.15.(a) Find the work required to put together this arrangement. (b) A charge $q_0$ is brought to the centre E of the square, the four charges being held fixed at its corners. How much extra work is needed to do this?

(Diagram described: Square ABCD, side d. Charges at corners: A(+q), B(-q), C(+q), D(-q). Centre is E.)

Solution

(a) To find the work required to put together the arrangement, we calculate the total potential energy of the system. This is the sum of potential energies of all possible pairs of charges.

The distances are:

  • Side length: $d$ (AB, BC, CD, DA)
  • Diagonal length: $d\sqrt{2}$ (AC, BD)

Pairs and their potential energies:

  • $U_{AB} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{(+q)(-q)}{d} = -\frac{q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d}$
  • $U_{BC} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{(-q)(+q)}{d} = -\frac{q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d}$
  • $U_{CD} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{(+q)(-q)}{d} = -\frac{q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d}$
  • $U_{DA} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{(-q)(+q)}{d} = -\frac{q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d}$
  • $U_{AC} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{(+q)(+q)}{d\sqrt{2}} = +\frac{q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d\sqrt{2}}$
  • $U_{BD} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{(-q)(-q)}{d\sqrt{2}} = +\frac{q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d\sqrt{2}}$

Total potential energy $U = U_{AB} + U_{BC} + U_{CD} + U_{DA} + U_{AC} + U_{BD}$.

$$ U = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d} \left( -q^2 - q^2 - q^2 - q^2 + \frac{q^2}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{q^2}{\sqrt{2}} \right) $$
$$ U = \frac{q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d} \left( -4 + \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} \right) = \frac{q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d} \left( -4 + \sqrt{2} \right) $$
$$ U = -\frac{q^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 d} (4 - \sqrt{2}) $$

The work required to put together this arrangement is equal to this total potential energy. $W = U$.

(b) When a charge $q_0$ is brought to the center E, the extra work needed is $W_{extra} = q_0 V_E$, where $V_E$ is the potential at E due to the four charges at the corners.

The distance from each corner to the center E is half the diagonal length, $r_E = \frac{d\sqrt{2}}{2} = \frac{d}{\sqrt{2}}$.

Potential at E due to $q_A$ (at A) is $V_A = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{+q}{d/\sqrt{2}}$.

Potential at E due to $q_B$ (at B) is $V_B = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{-q}{d/\sqrt{2}}$.

Potential at E due to $q_C$ (at C) is $V_C = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{+q}{d/\sqrt{2}}$.

Potential at E due to $q_D$ (at D) is $V_D = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{-q}{d/\sqrt{2}}$.

Total potential at E, $V_E = V_A + V_B + V_C + V_D$:

$$ V_E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0 (d/\sqrt{2})} (+q - q + q - q) = 0 $$

Since the potential at the center E is zero, the extra work needed to bring charge $q_0$ to the center is $W_{extra} = q_0 \times 0 = \mathbf{0}$ J.

Example 2.6

Question

A molecule of a substance has a permanent electric dipole moment of magnitude $10^{-29}$ C m. A mole of this substance is polarised (at low temperature) by applying a strong electrostatic field of magnitude $10^6$ V m⁻¹. The direction of the field is suddenly changed by an angle of 60°. Estimate the heat released by the substance in aligning its dipoles along the new direction of the field. For simplicity, assume 100% polarisation of the sample.

Solution

The potential energy of a dipole in an external electric field is $U = -pE \cos\theta$.

Given:

  • Dipole moment of one molecule, $p_{mol} = 10^{-29}$ C m.
  • Electric field, $E = 10^6$ V m⁻¹.
  • Avogadro's number $N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23}$ molecules/mol.

First, calculate the total dipole moment of 1 mole of the substance:

$$ P_{total} = N_A \times p_{mol} = (6.022 \times 10^{23}) \times (10^{-29}) = 6.022 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C m} $$

Initial angle of alignment: The dipoles are initially aligned, so $\theta_1 = 0^\circ$.

Initial potential energy:

$$ U_1 = -P_{total} E \cos(0^\circ) = -(6.022 \times 10^{-6})(10^6)(1) = -6.022 \text{ J} $$

Final angle of alignment: The field direction changes by 60°, so $\theta_2 = 60^\circ$.

Final potential energy:

$$ U_2 = -P_{total} E \cos(60^\circ) = -(6.022 \times 10^{-6})(10^6)(0.5) = -3.011 \text{ J} $$

The change in potential energy is $\Delta U = U_2 - U_1$:

$$ \Delta U = (-3.011 \text{ J}) - (-6.022 \text{ J}) = 3.011 \text{ J} $$

Since $\Delta U$ is positive, the potential energy of the system has increased. This means the substance has absorbed energy. However, the problem asks for heat *released*. If the dipoles are aligning themselves (moving from higher PE to lower PE), energy is released. Let's assume initial state is where dipoles are not fully aligned with the field, e.g., random initial state where $U_1=0$, and final state is $\theta=60^\circ$. The problem implies a re-alignment *from* an aligned state. Let's follow NCERT's interpretation.

If the field was initially aligned, and then it rotates by $60^\circ$, the dipoles will *re-align* to the new field direction. This means they *end up* at $\theta_2 = 0^\circ$ relative to the *new* field direction, and *started* at $60^\circ$ relative to the new field direction (because they were aligned with the *old* field direction, which rotated by $60^\circ$).

So, the initial angle (relative to the *new* field direction) is $\theta_1 = 60^\circ$.
The final angle (relative to the *new* field direction) is $\theta_2 = 0^\circ$.

Initial potential energy (relative to new field):

$$ U_1 = -P_{total} E \cos(60^\circ) = -(6.022 \times 10^{-6})(10^6)(0.5) = -3.011 \text{ J} $$

Final potential energy (relative to new field):

$$ U_2 = -P_{total} E \cos(0^\circ) = -(6.022 \times 10^{-6})(10^6)(1) = -6.022 \text{ J} $$

Change in potential energy $\Delta U = U_2 - U_1 = (-6.022 \text{ J}) - (-3.011 \text{ J}) = -3.011 \text{ J}$.

Since the change in potential energy is negative, the potential energy of the system has decreased. This energy is released as heat.
Heat released $= |\Delta U| \approx \mathbf{3.01 \text{ J}}$.

Example 2.7

Question

(a) A comb run through one's dry hair attracts small bits of paper. What happens if the hair is wet or if it is a rainy day? (Remember, a paper does not conduct electricity.)
(b) Ordinary rubber is an insulator. But special rubber tyres of aircraft are made slightly conducting. Why is this necessary?
(c) Vehicles carrying inflammable materials usually have metallic ropes touching the ground during motion. Why?
(d) A bird perches on a bare high power line, and nothing happens to the bird. A man standing on the ground touches the same line and gets a fatal shock. Why?

Solution

(a) When a comb is run through dry hair, it gets charged by friction (triboelectric effect). This charged comb then induces charges in the paper bits, leading to attraction. If the hair is wet or on a rainy day, the moisture in the air acts as a conductor, allowing charges to leak away quickly from the comb. Thus, the comb does not get sufficiently charged to attract paper bits.

(b) When an aircraft lands or takes off, friction between its tyres and the runway causes the aircraft body to accumulate static electric charge. If the tyres were perfect insulators, this charge would build up to a very high potential, which could lead to sparks and damage to the aircraft or cause a fire. By making the tyres slightly conducting, the accumulated charge can slowly leak to the ground, preventing dangerous build-up.

(c) Inflammable materials (like petrol, LPG) are highly combustible. As vehicles carrying these materials move, friction between the tyres and the road, or between the liquid and the tank walls, can build up static electric charge on the vehicle's body. A spark from this accumulated charge could ignite the inflammable material. Metallic ropes touching the ground provide a path for this static charge to be continuously conducted to the Earth, thus preventing dangerous charge accumulation and sparks.

(d) A bird perching on a single high power line does not get a shock because its body (feet) is at the same high potential as the wire. There is no potential difference across its body, so no current flows through it. A man standing on the ground, however, is at ground (zero) potential. When he touches the high power line, a large potential difference exists between his hand (at line's potential) and his feet (at ground potential). This large potential difference causes a significant current to flow through his body to the ground, resulting in a fatal shock.

Example 2.8

Question

A slab of material of dielectric constant K has the same area as the plates of a parallel-plate capacitor but has a thickness (3/4)d, where d is the separation of the plates. How is the capacitance changed when the slab is inserted between the plates?

Solution

Let the capacitance of the parallel-plate capacitor with vacuum be $C_0 = \frac{\epsilon_0 A}{d}$.
When the capacitor is connected to a battery, let the potential difference be $V_0$. The electric field in vacuum is $E_0 = V_0/d$.

When the dielectric slab of thickness $t = (3/4)d$ and dielectric constant $K$ is inserted, there are now two regions:

  1. The region with dielectric: thickness $t = (3/4)d$. The electric field in this region is $E_d = E_0/K$.
  2. The vacuum region: thickness $d - t = d - (3/4)d = (1/4)d$. The electric field in this region is $E_v = E_0$.

The total potential difference $V$ across the plates is the sum of potential differences across these two regions:

$$ V = E_d t + E_v (d-t) $$
$$ V = \frac{E_0}{K} \left(\frac{3}{4}d\right) + E_0 \left(\frac{1}{4}d\right) $$
$$ V = E_0 d \left( \frac{3}{4K} + \frac{1}{4} \right) = V_0 \left( \frac{3 + K}{4K} \right) $$

The new capacitance $C$ is $C = Q/V$. Since the charge $Q$ on the plates remains the same (assuming it's disconnected from battery or kept charged), $Q = C_0 V_0$.

$$ C = \frac{C_0 V_0}{V} = \frac{C_0 V_0}{V_0 \left( \frac{3+K}{4K} \right)} = C_0 \frac{4K}{3+K} $$

The capacitance is changed by a factor of $\frac{4K}{3+K}$. Since $K \ge 1$, this factor is always greater than 1, so the capacitance increases.

Example 2.10

Question

(a) A 900 pF capacitor is charged by 100 V battery [Fig. 2.31(a)]. How much electrostatic energy is stored by the capacitor?
(b) The capacitor is disconnected from the battery and connected to another 900 pF capacitor [Fig. 2.31(b)]. What is the electrostatic energy stored by the system?

(Diagram described: (a) Capacitor C connected to a battery. (b) Charged C disconnected from battery and connected to an uncharged identical C.)

Solution

Given $C = 900 \text{ pF} = 900 \times 10^{-12} \text{ F}$.

(a) Energy stored in the first capacitor:
Voltage of battery $V = 100 \text{ V}$.
The charge stored in the capacitor is $Q = CV = (900 \times 10^{-12})(100) = 9 \times 10^{-8} \text{ C}$.
Energy stored $U = \frac{1}{2} CV^2$:

$$ U = \frac{1}{2} (900 \times 10^{-12} \text{ F}) (100 \text{ V})^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 900 \times 10^{-12} \times 10000 = 4.5 \times 10^{-6} \text{ J} $$

The electrostatic energy stored is $4.5 \times 10^{-6}$ J.

(b) Energy stored after connection to an uncharged capacitor:
The first capacitor (C1) has charge $Q = 9 \times 10^{-8}$ C. The second capacitor (C2) is uncharged ($Q_2=0$).
When connected in parallel, the charge redistributes until they reach a common potential $V'$.
The total charge in the system is conserved: $Q_{total} = Q_1 + Q_2 = 9 \times 10^{-8} \text{ C} + 0 = 9 \times 10^{-8} \text{ C}$.
The two capacitors are in parallel, so their equivalent capacitance is $C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 = 900 \text{ pF} + 900 \text{ pF} = 1800 \text{ pF}$.

The common potential $V'$ is:

$$ V' = \frac{Q_{total}}{C_{eq}} = \frac{9 \times 10^{-8} \text{ C}}{1800 \times 10^{-12} \text{ F}} = \frac{9 \times 10^{-8}}{1.8 \times 10^{-9}} = 50 \text{ V} $$

Now, calculate the total electrostatic energy stored by the system:

$$ U' = \frac{1}{2} C_{eq} (V')^2 = \frac{1}{2} (1800 \times 10^{-12} \text{ F}) (50 \text{ V})^2 $$
$$ U' = \frac{1}{2} \times 1800 \times 10^{-12} \times 2500 = 900 \times 2500 \times 10^{-12} = 2.25 \times 10^6 \times 10^{-12} = 2.25 \times 10^{-6} \text{ J} $$

The energy stored by the system is $2.25 \times 10^{-6}$ J.

Notice that $U' < U$. The difference in energy, $U - U' = 4.5 \times 10^{-6} - 2.25 \times 10^{-6} = 2.25 \times 10^{-6}$ J, is lost during the redistribution of charge, primarily as heat in the connecting wires or electromagnetic radiation.

$$ \frac{5 \times 10^{-8}}{x} + \frac{-3 \times 10^{-8}}{0.16-x} = 0 $$
$$ \frac{5}{x} = \frac{3}{0.16-x} $$
$$ 5(0.16-x) = 3x $$
$$ 0.8 - 5x = 3x $$
$$ 0.8 = 8x \implies x = 0.1 \text{ m} = 10 \text{ cm} $$

Let P be at distance $x$ from $q_1$. The distance from $q_2$ to P is $(x-d)$.

$$ \frac{k q_1}{x} + \frac{k q_2}{x-d} = 0 $$
$$ \frac{5 \times 10^{-8}}{x} + \frac{-3 \times 10^{-8}}{x-0.16} = 0 $$
$$ \frac{5}{x} = \frac{3}{x-0.16} $$
$$ 5(x-0.16) = 3x $$
$$ 5x - 0.8 = 3x $$
$$ 2x = 0.8 \implies x = 0.4 \text{ m} = 40 \text{ cm} $$

So, another point is 40 cm from the $5 \times 10^{-8}$ C charge (and 24 cm from the $-3 \times 10^{-8}$ C charge), lying outside the charges on the side of $-3 \times 10^{-8}$ C.

Exercise 2.2

Question

A regular hexagon of side 10 cm has a charge 5 µC at each of its vertices. Calculate the potential at the centre of the hexagon.

Solution

For a regular hexagon, the distance from the centre to each vertex is equal to the side length.

Given:

  • Side length $a = 10 \text{ cm} = 0.1 \text{ m}$.
  • Charge at each vertex $q = 5 \mu\text{C} = 5 \times 10^{-6}$ C.
  • Number of vertices $n = 6$.

Distance from center to each vertex $r = a = 0.1 \text{ m}$.

The potential at the centre due to a system of point charges is the algebraic sum of potentials due to individual charges:

$$ V = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q_i}{r_i} $$

Since all charges are identical ($q_i=q$) and all distances are identical ($r_i=r$):

$$ V = n \times \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{q}{r} $$

Using $k = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} = 9 \times 10^9 \text{ N m}^2/\text{C}^2$:

$$ V = 6 \times (9 \times 10^9) \times \frac{5 \times 10^{-6} \text{ C}}{0.1 \text{ m}} $$
$$ V = 6 \times 9 \times 5 \times 10^{9-6} \times 10 = 270 \times 10^4 = 2.7 \times 10^6 \text{ V} $$

The potential at the centre of the hexagon is $2.7 \times 10^6$ V.

Exercise 2.3

Question

Two charges $2 \mu$C and $-2 \mu$C are placed at points A and B 6 cm apart.
(a) Identify an equipotential surface of the system.
(b) What is the direction of the electric field at every point on this surface?

Solution

The system consists of two equal and opposite charges, forming an electric dipole.

(a) The equipotential surface for a dipole where the potential is zero is the equatorial plane (perpendicular bisector) of the dipole. All points on this plane are equidistant from $+q$ and $-q$. Since potential is additive, and the charges are equal and opposite, the potential contributions cancel out ($V = \frac{kq}{r} + \frac{k(-q)}{r} = 0$).

(b) Electric field lines are always perpendicular to equipotential surfaces. Since the equatorial plane is an equipotential surface, the electric field at every point on this surface will be perpendicular to the plane. For a dipole, the electric field lines point from the positive charge to the negative charge. Therefore, on the equatorial plane, the electric field lines will be directed from point A to point B (if A is positive and B is negative), i.e., parallel to the line joining the charges.

Question

A spherical conductor of radius 12 cm has a charge of $1.6 \times 10^{-7}$ C distributed uniformly on its surface. What is the electric field
(a) inside the sphere
(b) just outside the sphere
(c) at a point 18 cm from the centre of the sphere?

(b) Just outside the sphere ($r = R$): The electric field just outside a conducting sphere is given by $E = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} \frac{Q}{R^2}$.

Exercise 2.9

Question

Explain what would happen if in the capacitor given in Exercise 2.8, a 3 mm thick mica sheet (of dielectric constant = 6) were inserted between the plates,
(a) while the voltage supply remained connected.
(b) after the supply was disconnected.

From Exercise 2.8, $C_0 = 17.7 \text{ pF}$, $V_0 = 100 \text{ V}$, $Q_0 = 1.77 \times 10^{-9}$ C.

  • The potential difference across the capacitor will remain constant: $V = V_0 = \mathbf{100 \text{ V}}$. This is because it is still connected to the 100 V battery.
  • The capacitance will increase by a factor of $K$: $C = KC_0 = 6 \times 17.7 \text{ pF} = \mathbf{106.2 \text{ pF}}$.

  • The electric field between the plates will remain the same ($E = V/d$). It doesn't change because $V$ is kept constant and $d$ is constant. (b) After the supply was disconnected:

    So, the potential difference becomes approximately 16.67 V.
  • The electric field between the plates will decrease: $E = V/d = (V_0/K)/d = E_0/K$. This is because the charge is fixed, and the induced field in the dielectric reduces the net field.

Exercise 2.11

Question

A 600 pF capacitor is charged by a 200 V supply. It is then disconnected from the supply and is connected to another uncharged 600 pF capacitor. How much electrostatic energy is lost in the process?

Solution

Given: $C_1 = 600 \text{ pF} = 600 \times 10^{-12} \text{ F}$.
Supply voltage $V_1 = 200 \text{ V}$.
Second capacitor $C_2 = 600 \text{ pF}$ (uncharged).

Step 1: Energy stored in $C_1$ before disconnection.
Charge on $C_1$: $Q_1 = C_1 V_1 = (600 \times 10^{-12} \text{ F}) \times (200 \text{ V}) = 1.2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ C}$.
Initial energy stored ($U_i$):

$$ U_i = \frac{1}{2} C_1 V_1^2 = \frac{1}{2} (600 \times 10^{-12}) (200)^2 = 300 \times 10^{-12} \times 40000 = 1.2 \times 10^{-5} \text{ J} $$

Step 2: Energy stored after connecting to $C_2$.
When $C_1$ (charged) is disconnected and connected to $C_2$ (uncharged) in parallel, the total charge is conserved.
Total charge $Q_{total} = Q_1 + 0 = 1.2 \times 10^{-7}$ C.
Equivalent capacitance in parallel: $C_{eq} = C_1 + C_2 = 600 \text{ pF} + 600 \text{ pF} = 1200 \text{ pF}$.
Common potential $V'$ after connection:

$$ V' = \frac{Q_{total}}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1.2 \times 10^{-7} \text{ C}}{1200 \times 10^{-12} \text{ F}} = \frac{1.2 \times 10^{-7}}{1.2 \times 10^{-9}} = 100 \text{ V} $$

Final energy stored ($U_f$) in the system:

$$ U_f = \frac{1}{2} C_{eq} (V')^2 = \frac{1}{2} (1200 \times 10^{-12} \text{ F}) (100 \text{ V})^2 $$
$$ U_f = 600 \times 10^{-12} \times 10000 = 6 \times 10^{-6} \text{ J} $$

Step 3: Energy lost.
Energy lost $\Delta U = U_i - U_f$:

$$ \Delta U = (1.2 \times 10^{-5} \text{ J}) - (6 \times 10^{-6} \text{ J}) = (12 \times 10^{-6} \text{ J}) - (6 \times 10^{-6} \text{ J}) = 6 \times 10^{-6} \text{ J} $$

The electrostatic energy lost in the process is $6 \times 10^{-6}$ J. This energy is dissipated primarily as heat in the connecting wires during charge redistribution.

Problem 28

Find the equivalent capacitance of the infinite ladder shown in figure (31-E17) between the points A and B.

Solution

This is an infinite ladder circuit. Let the equivalent capacitance of the entire ladder between points A and B be $C_{eq}$. Since the ladder is infinite, adding one more repeating section to the front of the ladder will not change the total equivalent capacitance.

The repeating section consists of a 2 µF capacitor in series with the main line, and another 2 µF capacitor in parallel (across the line).

Let's model the circuit. The first 2 µF capacitor (let's call it $C_s$) is in series with a parallel combination. This parallel combination consists of the second 2 µF capacitor ($C_p$) and the rest of the infinite ladder, which itself has a capacitance of $C_{eq}$.

The capacitance of the parallel part is $C_{parallel} = C_p + C_{eq} = (2 + C_{eq}) \mu\text{F}$.

The total capacitance $C_{eq}$ is the series combination of $C_s$ and $C_{parallel}$.

$$ C_{eq} = \frac{C_s \times C_{parallel}}{C_s + C_{parallel}} $$
$$ C_{eq} = \frac{2 \times (2 + C_{eq})}{2 + (2 + C_{eq})} = \frac{4 + 2C_{eq}}{4 + C_{eq}} $$

Now, we solve this equation for $C_{eq}$:

$$ C_{eq}(4 + C_{eq}) = 4 + 2C_{eq} $$
$$ 4C_{eq} + C_{eq}^2 = 4 + 2C_{eq} $$
$$ C_{eq}^2 + 2C_{eq} - 4 = 0 $$

This is a quadratic equation of the form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$. We use the quadratic formula to solve for $C_{eq}$:

$$ C_{eq} = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{2^2 - 4(1)(-4)}}{2(1)} $$
$$ C_{eq} = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{4 + 16}}{2} = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{20}}{2} = \frac{-2 \pm 2\sqrt{5}}{2} = -1 \pm \sqrt{5} $$

Since capacitance must be a positive physical quantity, we take the positive root:
