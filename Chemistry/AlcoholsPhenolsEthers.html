Introduction to Alcohols, Phenols, and Ethers

Alcohols, phenols, and ethers are important classes of organic compounds with wide applications in industry and daily life. They are formed by the substitution of hydrogen atoms in hydrocarbons.

  • Alcohols: One or more hydrogen atoms in an aliphatic hydrocarbon are replaced by a hydroxyl (–OH) group. Example: Methanol ($\ce{CH3OH}$).
  • Phenols: One or more hydrogen atoms in an aromatic hydrocarbon are replaced by a hydroxyl (–OH) group. Example: Phenol ($\ce{C6H5OH}$).
  • Ethers: A hydrogen atom in a hydrocarbon is replaced by an alkoxy (R-O–) or aryloxy (Ar-O–) group. They can also be seen as compounds formed by substituting the hydrogen atom of a hydroxyl group in an alcohol or phenol with an alkyl or aryl group. Example: Dimethyl ether ($\ce{CH3OCH3}$).

Classification

Classification of Alcohols and Phenols

Based on the number of hydroxyl groups:

  • Monohydric: Contain one –OH group (e.g., Ethanol, $\ce{C2H5OH}$).
  • Dihydric: Contain two –OH groups (e.g., Ethane-1,2-diol or ethylene glycol).
  • Trihydric: Contain three –OH groups (e.g., Propane-1,2,3-triol or glycerol).

Monohydric alcohols are further classified based on the hybridization of the carbon atom to which the –OH group is attached.

Compounds containing $\ce{C_{sp^3}-OH}$ bond:
  • Primary (1°), Secondary (2°), and Tertiary (3°) Alcohols: The –OH group is attached to a primary, secondary, or tertiary carbon atom, respectively.
  • Allylic Alcohols: The –OH group is attached to an $\ce{sp^3}$-hybridized carbon next to a carbon-carbon double bond ($\ce{>C=C-C-OH}$).
  • Benzylic Alcohols: The –OH group is attached to an $\ce{sp^3}$-hybridized carbon next to an aromatic ring ($\ce{Ar-C-OH}$).
Compounds containing $\ce{C_{sp^2}-OH}$ bond:
  • Vinylic Alcohols: The –OH group is bonded directly to a carbon-carbon double bond ($\ce{>C=C-OH}$). These are generally unstable and tautomerize to aldehydes or ketones.
  • Phenols: The –OH group is bonded directly to an $\ce{sp^2}$-hybridized carbon of an aromatic ring.

Classification of Ethers

  • Symmetrical (or Simple) Ethers: The two alkyl or aryl groups attached to the oxygen atom are the same (R-O-R). Example: Diethyl ether ($\ce{C2H5OC2H5}$).
  • Unsymmetrical (or Mixed) Ethers: The two groups attached to the oxygen atom are different (R-O-R'). Example: Ethyl methyl ether ($\ce{C2H5OCH3}$).

Nomenclature

Alcohols

  • Common Name: The name of the alkyl group is followed by the word 'alcohol'. Example: $\ce{CH3OH}$ is methyl alcohol.
  • IUPAC Name: The 'e' at the end of the parent alkane name is replaced by the suffix 'ol'. The position of the –OH group and any substituents are indicated by numbers, with the –OH group getting the lowest possible number. Example: $\ce{CH3CH(OH)CH3}$ is Propan-2-ol.

Phenols

  • The simplest hydroxy derivative of benzene, $\ce{C6H5OH}$, is called phenol. This is both its common name and accepted IUPAC name.
  • Substituted phenols are named as derivatives of phenol. The positions of substituents are indicated by numbers or by the prefixes ortho (1,2-), meta (1,3-), and para (1,4-). Example: 2-Methylphenol is also known as o-cresol.

Ethers

  • Common Name: The names of the two alkyl/aryl groups are written in alphabetical order, followed by the word 'ether'. Example: $\ce{CH3OC2H5}$ is ethyl methyl ether.
  • IUPAC Name: Ethers are named as alkoxyalkanes. The smaller alkyl group along with the oxygen atom is named as an 'alkoxy' substituent, and the larger alkyl group is considered the parent alkane. Example: $\ce{CH3OC2H5}$ is Methoxyethane. For aromatic ethers, the alkoxy group is treated as a substituent on the benzene ring. Example: $\ce{C6H5OCH3}$ is Methoxybenzene (common name: Anisole).

Structures of Functional Groups

  • Alcohols: The C-O-H bond angle in methanol is about 108.9°, slightly less than the tetrahedral angle (109.5°) due to repulsion between the lone pairs on the oxygen atom. The C-O bond is a sigma bond formed by the overlap of an $\ce{sp^3}$ orbital of carbon with an $\ce{sp^3}$ orbital of oxygen.
  • Phenols: The C-O bond length (136 pm) is shorter than in methanol because the carbon is $\ce{sp^2}$-hybridized and there is partial double bond character due to resonance. The C-O-H bond angle is about 109°.
  • Ethers: The C-O-C bond angle in ethers (e.g., 111.7° in methoxymethane) is slightly larger than the tetrahedral angle. This is due to the repulsive interaction between the two bulky alkyl/aryl groups attached to the oxygen atom.

Physical Properties

Boiling Points

The boiling points of alcohols and phenols are significantly higher than those of hydrocarbons, ethers, and haloalkanes of comparable molecular masses. This is due to the presence of intermolecular hydrogen bonding between the hydroxyl groups.

  • Boiling point increases with an increase in the number of carbon atoms (due to increased van der Waals forces).
  • Among isomeric alcohols, boiling point decreases with an increase in branching (as this decreases the surface area for van der Waals forces). The order is: Primary > Secondary > Tertiary.

Solubility

Lower molecular mass alcohols are soluble in water because they can form hydrogen bonds with water molecules. However, the solubility decreases as the size of the nonpolar alkyl/aryl (hydrophobic) group increases.

Comparison of Acidity:
  • Phenols are much more acidic than alcohols. This is because the phenoxide ion formed after losing a proton is highly stabilized by resonance, where the negative charge is delocalized over the benzene ring. The alkoxide ion from an alcohol has no such resonance stabilization; in fact, the alkyl group destabilizes it via a +I effect.
  • Acidity of Alcohols: The acidic strength of alcohols decreases in the order: Primary > Secondary > Tertiary. This is because electron-releasing alkyl groups (+I effect) increase the electron density on the oxygen, making the O-H bond less polar and the release of $\ce{H+}$ more difficult.
  • Effect of Substituents on Phenol Acidity:
    • Electron-withdrawing groups (like $\ce{-NO2}$, $\ce{-CN}$, -X) increase the acidity of phenols, especially when at the ortho and para positions, as they help delocalize the negative charge of the phenoxide ion.
    • Electron-donating groups (like $\ce{-CH3}$, $\ce{-OCH3}$) decrease the acidity of phenols as they intensify the negative charge on the phenoxide ion, destabilizing it.

3. Oxidation of Alcohols

Specific Reactions of Phenols

1. Electrophilic Aromatic Substitution

The –OH group is a powerful activating group and is ortho- and para-directing.

  • Nitration:
    • With dilute $\ce{HNO3}$ at low temperature, it gives a mixture of o-nitrophenol and p-nitrophenol. (o-isomer is steam volatile due to intramolecular H-bonding).
    • With concentrated $\ce{HNO3}$, it gives 2,4,6-trinitrophenol (Picric acid).
  • Halogenation:
    • With $\ce{Br2}$ in a non-polar solvent like $\ce{CS2}$ at low temperature, it gives a mixture of o-bromophenol and p-bromophenol (major).
    • With aqueous bromine (bromine water), it gives a white precipitate of 2,4,6-tribromophenol.

2. Kolbe's Reaction

Phenol is treated with NaOH to form sodium phenoxide, which is more reactive. The phenoxide then undergoes electrophilic substitution with carbon dioxide (a weak electrophile) under pressure, followed by acidification, to form 2-hydroxybenzoic acid (salicylic acid).

3. Reimer-Tiemann Reaction

Phenol is treated with chloroform ($\ce{CHCl3}$) in the presence of aqueous NaOH. An aldehyde group (–CHO) is introduced, mainly at the ortho position. This forms 2-hydroxybenzaldehyde (salicylaldehyde).

When phenol is heated with zinc dust, it is reduced to benzene.

Physical Properties

The boiling points of ethers are much lower than those of alcohols of comparable molecular mass because ethers cannot form intermolecular hydrogen bonds. Their boiling points are comparable to those of alkanes. Ethers are sparingly soluble in water as they can form hydrogen bonds with water molecules through their oxygen atom.

Chapter Summary

Alcohols and phenols are characterized by the hydroxyl (–OH) group, while ethers contain an ether linkage (C–O–C). Their properties are dictated by these functional groups. Alcohols are prepared from alkenes, carbonyl compounds, and Grignard reagents. Phenols are synthesized from haloarenes, sulphonic acids, diazonium salts, or cumene. The hydrogen bonding in alcohols and phenols leads to high boiling points and water solubility. Phenols are significantly more acidic than alcohols due to resonance stabilization of the phenoxide ion. Alcohols undergo dehydration to form alkenes and oxidation to form aldehydes, ketones, or carboxylic acids. Phenols undergo electrophilic substitution at ortho and para positions. Ethers are prepared by alcohol dehydration or Williamson synthesis. They are cleaved by strong acids like HI and HBr. Aromatic ethers also undergo electrophilic substitution.

Comparison of Acidity: Phenol vs. Ethanol

Phenol is significantly more acidic than ethanol.

  • Ethanol ($\ce{C2H5OH}$): When ethanol loses a proton, it forms the ethoxide ion ($\ce{C2H5O-}$). The ethyl group ($\ce{-C2H5}$) is an electron-donating group (+I effect), which increases the electron density on the oxygen atom. This destabilizes the negative charge on the ethoxide ion, making it a stronger base and ethanol a weaker acid.
  • Phenol ($\ce{C6H5OH}$): When phenol loses a proton, it forms the phenoxide ion ($\ce{C6H5O-}$). The negative charge on the oxygen atom is delocalized into the benzene ring through resonance. This delocalization stabilizes the phenoxide ion, making it a weaker base and phenol a stronger acid.

Because the phenoxide ion is much more stable than the ethoxide ion, phenol is a much stronger acid than ethanol. The pKa of phenol is about 10, while the pKa of ethanol is about 16.

7.19 Write the mechanism of acid dehydration of ethanol to yield ethene.
Solution:

The acid-catalyzed dehydration of ethanol to ethene at 443 K occurs in three steps. The overall reaction is:

H2SO4, 443 K
⟶

Step 1: Protonation of alcohol
The alcohol accepts a proton from the acid in a fast, reversible step to form a protonated alcohol (oxonium ion).
$\ce{CH3CH2OH + H+ <=> CH3CH2\overset{+}{O}H2}$

Step 2: Formation of carbocation
The protonated alcohol loses a molecule of water to form a primary carbocation. This is the slowest step and is therefore the rate-determining step of the reaction.
$\ce{CH3CH2\overset{+}{O}H2 ->[slow] \overset{+}{C}H2CH3 + H2O}$

Step 3: Elimination of a proton
A base (like water or the conjugate base of the acid) removes a proton from the carbocation to form an alkene. The acid catalyst is regenerated.
$\ce{\overset{+}{C}H2CH2H + Base: -> CH2=CH2 + H-Base+}$

Mechanism ($\ce{S_N2}$ Pathway):

The mechanism involves the protonation of one alcohol molecule, followed by a nucleophilic attack from a second alcohol molecule. This is preferred for primary alcohols as they do not form stable carbocations.

Step 1: Protonation of propan-1-ol
One molecule of the alcohol is protonated by the acid catalyst.
$\ce{CH3CH2CH2OH + H+ <=> CH3CH2CH2\overset{+}{O}H2}$

Step 2: Nucleophilic attack by a second alcohol molecule
A second molecule of propan-1-ol acts as a nucleophile and attacks the protonated alcohol, displacing a molecule of water. This is a bimolecular ($\ce{S_N2}$) step.
$\ce{CH3CH2CH2OH + CH3CH2CH2\overset{+}{O}H2 -> [CH3CH2CH2-\overset{+}{O}(H)-CH2CH2CH3] + H2O}$

Step 3: Deprotonation to form the ether
The resulting protonated ether loses a proton to a base (e.g., water) to form the final ether product and regenerate the acid catalyst.
$\ce{[CH3CH2CH2-\overset{+}{O}(H)-CH2CH2CH3] + H2O <=> CH3CH2CH2-O-CH2CH2CH3 + H3O+}$
