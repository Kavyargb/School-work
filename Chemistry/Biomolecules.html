Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are a large group of naturally occurring organic compounds, primarily produced by plants. Examples include glucose, sugar, and starch. Initially defined as 'hydrates of carbon' with the general formula \(\ce{C_x(H2O)_y}\), this definition was found to be too broad (e.g., acetic acid, \(\ce{CH3COOH}\), fits the formula but is not a carbohydrate) and also too restrictive (e.g., rhamnose, \(\ce{C6H12O5}\), is a carbohydrate but doesn't fit the formula).

Modern Definition: Carbohydrates are optically active polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or compounds which produce such units on hydrolysis.

Classification of Carbohydrates

  1. Monosaccharides: The simplest carbohydrates that cannot be hydrolysed further. Examples: Glucose, Fructose, Ribose.
  2. Oligosaccharides: Yield two to ten monosaccharide units on hydrolysis.
    • Disaccharides are the most common, yielding two units. Examples: Sucrose, Maltose, Lactose.
  3. Polysaccharides: Yield a large number of monosaccharide units on hydrolysis. They are not sweet and are also called 'non-sugars'. Examples: Starch, Cellulose, Glycogen.

Monosaccharides

Structure of Glucose (\(\ce{C6H12O6}\))

Glucose is an aldohexose, also known as dextrose. Its structure was elucidated based on the following evidence:

  • On prolonged heating with HI, it forms n-hexane, suggesting a six-carbon straight chain.
  • It reacts with hydroxylamine (\(\ce{NH2OH}\)) and hydrogen cyanide (\(\ce{HCN}\)), confirming the presence of a carbonyl group (>C=O).
  • It gets oxidised by mild agents like bromine water to gluconic acid, indicating the carbonyl group is an aldehyde.
  • Acetylation with acetic anhydride gives glucose pentaacetate, confirming the presence of five -OH groups.
  • Oxidation with nitric acid yields saccharic acid (a dicarboxylic acid), indicating the presence of a primary alcohol group.

Cyclic Structure of Glucose

The open-chain structure could not explain some observations, such as glucose not giving the Schiff's test and the existence of two different crystalline forms (anomers): \(\alpha\)-glucose and \(\beta\)-glucose.

This led to the proposal of a cyclic hemiacetal structure, formed by the intramolecular reaction between the -OH group at C-5 and the -CHO group at C-1. This forms a six-membered ring, called a pyranose structure.

Anomers: Isomers that differ in the configuration of the hydroxyl group at the anomeric carbon (C-1 in glucose). In the \(\alpha\)-anomer, the -OH on C-1 is trans to the \(\ce{-CH2OH}\) group, while in the \(\beta\)-anomer, it is cis.

Structure of Fructose (\(\ce{C6H12O6}\))

Fructose is a ketohexose. It also has a cyclic structure. The intramolecular reaction occurs between the C-2 keto group and the C-5 -OH group, forming a five-membered ring called a furanose structure.

Disaccharides

In disaccharides, two monosaccharides are joined by an oxide linkage known as a glycosidic linkage.

  1. Sucrose: Formed from \(\alpha\)-D-glucose and \(\beta\)-D-fructose. The glycosidic linkage is between C1 of glucose and C2 of fructose (\(\alpha, \beta-1,2\) linkage). It is a non-reducing sugar because the aldehydic and ketonic groups are involved in the linkage.

  2. Maltose: Formed from two units of \(\alpha\)-D-glucose. The linkage is between C1 of one unit and C4 of the other (\(\alpha-1,4'\) linkage). It is a reducing sugar.

  3. Lactose: Formed from \(\beta\)-D-galactose and \(\beta\)-D-glucose. The linkage is between C1 of galactose and C4 of glucose (\(\beta-1,4'\) linkage). It is a reducing sugar.

Polysaccharides

  1. Starch: A polymer of \(\alpha\)-D-glucose. It has two components:
    • Amylose: A long, unbranched chain with \(\alpha-1,4'\) glycosidic linkages.
    • Amylopectin: A branched-chain polymer with \(\alpha-1,4'\) linkages in the chain and \(\alpha-1,6'\) linkages at the branch points.
  2. Cellulose: A straight-chain polymer of \(\beta\)-D-glucose units joined by \(\beta-1,4'\) glycosidic linkages.

Proteins

Proteins are the most abundant biomolecules in living systems. They are polymers of \(\alpha\)-amino acids.

Amino Acids

Amino acids contain both an amino group (\(\ce{-NH2}\)) and a carboxyl group (\(\ce{-COOH}\)). In aqueous solution, they exist as dipolar ions called zwitterions.

⇌

There are about 20 common amino acids. Those that cannot be synthesized by the body are called essential amino acids and must be obtained through diet.

Peptide Bond

Proteins are formed when amino acids link together via an amide linkage, called a peptide bond (\(\ce{-CO-NH-}\)). When two amino acids link, the product is a dipeptide. Many amino acids linked together form a polypeptide.

Structure of Proteins

  • Primary Structure: The specific sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain.
  • Secondary Structure: The local folding of the polypeptide chain into regular patterns like the \(\alpha\)-helix and \(\beta\)-pleated sheet, stabilized by hydrogen bonds.
  • Tertiary Structure: The overall 3D folding of the polypeptide chain, giving rise to fibrous or globular shapes. It is stabilized by hydrogen bonds, disulfide linkages, van der Waals forces, and electrostatic forces.
  • Quaternary Structure: The arrangement of multiple polypeptide chains (subunits) in a protein complex.

Nucleic Acids

Nucleic acids are polymers of nucleotides, responsible for the transmission of hereditary characteristics (heredity). There are two types: Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA) and Ribonucleic Acid (RNA).

Chemical Composition of Nucleic Acids

Complete hydrolysis of DNA or RNA yields three components:

  1. A Pentose Sugar: \(\beta\)-D-ribose in RNA and \(\beta\)-D-2-deoxyribose in DNA.
  2. Phosphoric Acid: Forms the phosphate group.
  3. Nitrogenous Bases:
    • Purines: Adenine (A) and Guanine (G).
    • Pyrimidines: Cytosine (C), Thymine (T) (in DNA only), and Uracil (U) (in RNA only).

Structure of Nucleic Acids

  • A Nucleoside is formed when a base is attached to the 1' position of the sugar.
  • A Nucleotide is formed when a phosphate group is linked to the 5' -OH group of a nucleoside.
  • Nucleotides are joined together by phosphodiester linkages between the 5' position of one sugar and the 3' position of the next, forming a polynucleotide chain.

DNA Structure

DNA consists of two polynucleotide strands coiled around each other to form a double helix. The strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between specific pairs of bases:

  • Adenine (A) always pairs with Thymine (T) with two hydrogen bonds.
  • Guanine (G) always pairs with Cytosine (C) with three hydrogen bonds.

The two strands are complementary, not identical.
