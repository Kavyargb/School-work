Solutions

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more than two components dispersed on a molecular state.

The component which is present in excess amount is called the solvent.

Solute is the component that dissolves in the solvents.

Solids in Liquid

Solubility of a solid in a liquid at any temperature is defined as the maximum amount of solid (solute) in grams which can be dissolved in 100g of liquid (solvent) to form a saturated solution.

A solid dissolves in a liquid chemically similar to it. A polar compound dissolves in a polar solvent and a nonpolar solute dissolves in a nonpolar solvent (often summarized as "like dissolves like").

The reason for the solubility of an ionic compound in polar solvents is because of the strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the ions of the crystal and the polar solvent molecules. The negative ions are attracted by the positive poles of the solvent molecules (e.g., H in H₂O), and the positive ions by the negative poles of the solvent (e.g., O in H₂O).

Dissolution of NaCl in H₂O

For example, when water is used as a solvent, the water molecules pull the ions of the crystal apart.

And the ions are surrounded by the water molecules which act as an envelope around the ions and prevent the recombination of the ions.

Hydrated ions: Ions moving freely in the solution, surrounded by solvent molecules.

The energy required for splitting of the ionic compounds into ions (called lattice energy), energy is given out when the ions get hydrated (called hydration energy). If the hydration energy is sufficiently large to overcome the lattice energy at a specific temperature, then the compound dissolves otherwise sediments. More precisely, dissolution occurs if the overall Gibbs free energy change is negative.

Effect of temperature on solubility

(i) Ionic substances whose solubility decreases continuously with increase of temperature, are said to have exothermic dissolution process (or $\Delta_{sol}H < 0$) and are called exothermic solutes. (Note: $\Delta_{mix}H$ is often used for the enthalpy change of mixing solute and solvent to form a solution, similar to $\Delta_{sol}H$).

(ii) Endothermic solutes have their solubility increase continuously with increase of temperature. $$ \text{Solvent} + \text{Solute} + \text{Heat} \rightleftharpoons \text{Solution} \quad (\Delta_{sol}H > 0) $$ As per Le Chatelier's principle, as the temperature is increased, equilibrium is shifted towards right, favoring dissolution.

(iii) Transition solutes are those whose solubility does not change monotonically; the trend may change at a specific temperature called the transition temperature (e.g., Na₂SO₄·10H₂O). At this temperature, often the nature of the solid phase changes (e.g., degree of hydration).

Note: When solubilities are plotted against temperature, the curve obtained is called solubility curve.

Case of NaCl

The dissolution of NaCl in water is an endothermic process or $\Delta_{sol}H > 0$. This is often explained by considering that although both lattice energy and hydration energy decrease with temperature, the decrease in lattice energy is more significant than the decrease in hydration energy for NaCl, leading to a net endothermic effect that becomes more favorable at higher T. $$ \text{NaCl(s)} + \text{H₂O(l)} + \text{Heat} \rightleftharpoons \text{Na⁺(aq)} + \text{Cl⁻(aq)} $$ According to Le Chatelier's principle, as the temperature increases, the equilibrium is shifted towards the right side; so the solubility of NaCl increases.

Case of Ce₂(SO₄)₃

The dissolution of Ce₂(SO₄)₃ in water is an exothermic process ($\Delta_{sol}H < 0$). This may be due to the decrease in hydration energy being less significant than the decrease in (the magnitude of) lattice energy when temperature increases, or simply that the overall process releases heat. $$ \text{Ce₂(SO₄)₃(s)} + \text{H₂O(l)} \rightleftharpoons \text{2Ce³⁺(aq)} + \text{3SO₄²⁻(aq)} + \text{Heat} $$ According to Le Chatelier's principle, as the temperature increases, the equilibrium is shifted towards the left side; so the solubility decreases.

Case of Sodium Sulphate (Glauber's Salt)

Sodium Sulphate transitions from its hydrated form (Na₂SO₄·10H₂O) to anhydrous form (Na₂SO₄) at the transition temperature of 34.2°C.

(i) Below 34.2°C (Na₂SO₄·10H₂O): The dissolution of Na₂SO₄·10H₂O is endothermic. Its solubility increases with temperature.

(ii) Above 34.2°C (Na₂SO₄): The dissolution of anhydrous Na₂SO₄ is exothermic. Its solubility decreases with temperature.

The explanations involving "decrease in lattice energy > decrease in hydration energy" are simplifications for complex phenomena. The key is the sign of $\Delta_{sol}H$.

Solubility of a Gas in a Liquid

Solubility of a gas in a liquid can be defined as the moles of gas that will dissolve in one mole of the solvent (or a given amount of solvent) to form a saturated solⁿ at a particular temperature and pressure.

Polar gases like NH₃, SO₂, HCl are more soluble in water (a polar solvent) than non-polar gases like O₂, N₂. Non-polar gases are more soluble in non-polar solvents like alcohol or benzene.

The solubility of gas in a liquid is determined by several factors:

(a) The nature of the gas and the solvent: The solubility of different gases varies considerably in the same solvent. It depends upon the intermolecular forces between gas and solvent molecules. Gases which can be easily liquefied (i.e., have stronger intermolecular forces and/or higher critical temperatures) are generally more soluble in a given solvent than gases that are difficult to liquefy. For example CO₂ is more soluble than N₂ in water.

(b) The effect of temperature: The solubility of most gases in liquids decreases with increase in temperature. This is because the dissolution of a gas in a liquid is generally an exothermic process. $$ \text{Gas} + \text{Solvent} \rightleftharpoons \text{Solution} + \text{Heat} \quad (\Delta_{sol}H < 0) $$ On applying Le Chatelier's principle, an increase in temperature would shift the equilibrium in the backward direction (favoring the gas phase), so solubility would decrease. (This is why aquatic species are more comfortable in cold waters rather than warm waters, as cold water dissolves more oxygen; solubility of gases generally decreases with increasing temperature.)

(c) Effect of Pressure: The solubility of gases in liquids increases with increase in pressure of the gas above the solvent.

If $m$ is the mass of the gas dissolved by unit volume of the solvent, and the partial pressure of the gas at equilibrium with solⁿ is $P$, then as per Henry's law (at constant temperature): $$ m \propto P \quad \text{or} \quad m = K_H' P $$ (Note: $K_H'$ here is a constant specific to this form of Henry's Law.)

Henry's law in the form of mole fraction

A more common statement of Henry's law relates the partial pressure of the gas above the solution to its mole fraction in the solution: The partial pressure of the gas in vapour phase ($p$) is proportional to the mole fraction of the gas ($x$) in the solution. $$ p = K_H x $$ where $K_H$ is the Henry's Law constant. The value of $K_H$ depends on the nature of the gas, the nature of the solvent, and the temperature. Higher $K_H$ means lower solubility for a given partial pressure.

Derivation from mass form (illustrative):

If $m_2$ is the mass of gas dissolved in mass $m_1$ of solvent: $m_2 = K' P \cdot V_{solvent}$ (assuming $m$ was mass per unit volume, $V_{solvent}$ is volume of solvent) If $m$ was mass of gas per unit mass of solvent: $m_2/m_1 = K''P$. Converting masses to moles ($n_2 = m_2/M_2$, $n_1 = m_1/M_1$): $$ \frac{n_2 M_2}{n_1 M_1} = K''P \Rightarrow \frac{n_2}{n_1} = \left(K'' \frac{M_1}{M_2}\right) P $$ So, $\frac{n_2}{n_1} = K'''P$. For dilute solutions, $x_2 = \frac{n_2}{n_1+n_2} \approx \frac{n_2}{n_1}$. Thus, $x_2 \approx K'''P$, or $P = (1/K''') x_2$. Setting $K_H = 1/K'''$, we get $P = K_H x_2$.

Q7) State Henry's law. What is the significance of $K_H$?

Ans: Henry's law states that at a constant temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas present above the surface of liquid or solution. Mathematically, it is often expressed as $p = K_H x$, where $p$ is the partial pressure of the gas in the vapour phase, $x$ is the mole fraction of the gas in the solution, and $K_H$ is Henry's law constant.

The significance of $K_H$ is that it is a measure of the solubility of a gas. For a given partial pressure, $x = p/K_H$. Thus, higher the value of $K_H$ at a given pressure, the lower is the solubility of the gas in the liquid. $K_H$ is specific to a given gas-solvent pair and temperature.

Applications of Henry's Law

  1. To increase the solubility of CO₂ in soft drinks and soda water, the bottles are sealed under high pressure.
  2. To minimize the painful effects of decompression sickness (the "bends") in deep-sea divers, diving gas mixtures often use helium to dilute oxygen, as helium is less soluble in blood than nitrogen under high pressure.
  3. In the lungs, where the partial pressure of oxygen is high, oxygen binds with haemoglobin to form oxyhaemoglobin. In tissues, where the partial pressure of oxygen is low, oxyhaemoglobin releases oxygen for cellular activities. This process is related to partial pressures and affinities, though not strictly Henry's Law for a chemical reaction.
  4. Scuba divers breathing compressed air at depth experience increased dissolution of gases (especially nitrogen) in their blood. If they ascend too quickly, the pressure decreases, and the dissolved nitrogen can form bubbles in the bloodstream, leading to decompression sickness (bends). Using helium-oxygen mixtures (Heliox) helps because helium has a lower solubility and diffuses faster.
  5. At high altitudes, the partial pressure of oxygen in the atmosphere is lower than at ground level. This leads to lower concentrations of dissolved oxygen in the blood and tissues of climbers, which can cause anoxia (or hypoxia), a condition characterized by symptoms like weakness, fatigue, and difficulty in thinking clearly.

Limitations of Henry's Law

Henry's law is valid only under specific conditions:

  • a. The pressure of the gas is low (gas behaves ideally).
  • b. The temperature is high (which generally means low solubility, helping to meet condition c).
  • c. The gas is not highly soluble in the solvent.
  • d. The gas does not undergo chemical reaction with the solvent, nor does it associate or dissociate in the solvent.

Solubility of a Solid in a liquid (Revisited)

The solubility of solids in liquid varies with the nature of solid and liquid, temperature, and to a lesser extent on the pressure of the system. In general, a solute dissolves in a solvent if the intermolecular interactions are similar in the two or we may say like dissolves like.

When a solid solute is added to the solvent, some solute dissolves and its concentration increases in the solⁿ. This process is known as dissolution. Simultaneously, some solute particles in the solⁿ collide with the undissolved solid solute particles and get separated out of solution (precipitate). This process is known as crystallization. A state of dynamic equilibrium is reached when the rate of dissolution becomes equal to the rate of crystallization. At this stage, the concentration of solute in solⁿ remains constant at a given temperature and pressure.

The solution at this stage, in which no more solute can be dissolved at the same temperature and pressure, is called a saturated solⁿ. An unsaturated solution is one in which more solute can be dissolved at the same temperature and pressure. The maximum amount of solute (usually in grams) that can be dissolved in a specified amount of solvent (e.g., 100g) at a particular temperature to form a saturated solution is called its solubility.

Solubility of one substance into another depends on the nature of the substances (solute and solvent). In addition to these, temperature and pressure also control this phenomenon. Temperature has a significant effect on the solubility of a solid substance in a solvent. The solubility may increase or decrease with an increase in temperature.

  • According to Le Chatelier's principle, if the dissolution process is endothermic ($\Delta_{sol}H > 0$), the solubility should increase with a rise in temperature. For example, KNO₃, NaCl, KCl etc.
  • If the dissolution process is exothermic ($\Delta_{sol}H < 0$), the solubility should decrease with an increase in temperature. For example, Li₂SO₄, Ce₂(SO₄)₃, CaCO₃ (in some contexts, though its dissolution is complex).

Pressure does not have any significant effect on the solubility of solids in liquids. This is because solids and liquids are highly incompressible and are practically unaffected by changes in pressure.

Vapour Pressure of liquid solⁿ

When a volatile liquid is placed in a closed container, it evaporates and its vapour occupies the available space above the liquid. In this space, the vapour molecules are in random motion. They collide with each other, with the walls of the container, and with the liquid surface. Some vapour molecules return to the liquid phase (condensation). Eventually, a dynamic equilibrium is established where the rate of vaporisation equals the rate of condensation. The pressure exerted by the vapour molecules in equilibrium with the liquid at a given temperature is known as its saturated vapour pressure, or simply vapour pressure.

The vapour pressure of a liquid depends upon the following factors:

(i) Temperature: The vapour pressure of a liquid increases with an increase in temperature. This is because, at higher temperatures, a larger fraction of molecules in the liquid has sufficient kinetic energy to overcome intermolecular attractive forces and escape into the vapour phase.

(ii) Nature of liquid: Liquids with weaker intermolecular forces are more volatile and thus have higher vapour pressures at a given temperature compared to liquids with stronger intermolecular forces.

Vapour pressure of solution containing volatile solvent and non-volatile solute

In such a solution, only the solvent molecules are volatile and contribute to the vapour pressure. The vapour pressure of such a solution is always found to be lower than the vapour pressure of the pure solvent at the same temperature.

This is because, in the solution, the surface of the liquid has both solute and solvent molecules. Consequently, the fraction of the surface area occupied by solvent molecules is reduced. As a result, the number of solvent molecules escaping from the surface into the vapour phase per unit time is correspondingly reduced, leading to a lower vapour pressure. The decrease in vapour pressure is related to the amount of non-volatile solute present.

Raoult's Law (for non-volatile solute)

Definition: For a solution of a non-volatile solute in a volatile solvent, the partial vapour pressure of the solvent over the solution is directly proportional to its mole fraction in the solution.

Mathematically: $$ P_{solvent} = x_{solvent} P_{solvent}^0 $$ where $P_{solvent}$ is the vapour pressure of the solvent over the solution, $x_{solvent}$ is the mole fraction of the solvent in the solution, and $P_{solvent}^0$ (or often written as $P^0$) is the vapour pressure of the pure solvent at the same temperature. This is known as Raoult's law for solutions with non-volatile solutes. Since the solute is non-volatile, $P_{solution} = P_{solvent}$.

Alternative Statement: At a given temperature, the vapour pressure of a solution containing a non-volatile solute is directly proportional to the mole fraction of the solvent in the solution.

Raoult's law as a special case of Henry's law

According to Raoult's law, the vapour pressure of a volatile component A in a given solution is $P_A = P_A^0 x_A$. According to Henry's law, for a gas (solute) dissolved in a liquid (solvent), its partial pressure is $P_{gas} = K_H x_{gas}$.

If we consider a very dilute solution where component A is the solvent ($x_A \to 1$), Raoult's law applies. If component A is a solute at very low concentration ($x_A \to 0$), Henry's law applies to it. For an ideal solution, Henry's law constant $K_H$ for a component becomes equal to its pure vapour pressure $P_A^0$ when that component is treated as a solute approaching $x_A=0$ in a solvent where Raoult's law holds for the solvent. More accurately, Raoult's law describes the behavior of the solvent in an ideal solution across all compositions, and also the behavior of a solute if it forms an ideal solution. Henry's law describes the behavior of a volatile solute in a dilute real solution. For the solvent in a dilute solution, Raoult's law ($P_A = P_A^0 x_A$) is always obeyed. For the solute in a dilute solution, Henry's law ($P_B = K_H x_B$) is obeyed. If the solution were ideal over the whole concentration range, then $K_H$ for component B would be equal to $P_B^0$.

* As $x_A \to 0$ (solute A is very dilute), Henry's Law typically describes its behavior: $P_A = K_H x_A$.

* As $x_A \to 1$ (component A is almost pure solvent), Raoult's Law describes its behavior: $P_A = P_A^0 x_A$.

For an ideal solution, $K_H$ for component A would be equal to $P_A^0$, and Raoult's law would hold over the entire concentration range.

Raoult's law for a solⁿ containing two volatile components

If both components of a binary solution (say, A and B) are volatile, then the vapour phase will consist of vapours of both components. The partial vapour pressure of each component in the solution is proportional to its mole fraction in the solution, according to Raoult's law (if the solution is ideal).

For component A: $P_A = P_A^0 x_A \quad \text{....(i)}$

For component B: $P_B = P_B^0 x_B \quad \text{....(ii)}$

Where $P_A^0$ and $P_B^0$ are the vapour pressures of pure components A and B, respectively, and $x_A$ and $x_B$ are their mole fractions in the solution.

Raoult's law for a solution of volatile liquids (ideal solution): The partial vapour pressure of each volatile component in the solution is directly proportional to its mole fraction in the solution.

According to Dalton's law of partial pressures, the total vapour pressure ($P_{total}$) over the solution is the sum of the partial pressures of the components (assuming ideal gas behavior of vapours): $$ P_{total} = P_A + P_B $$ Substituting from Raoult's law: $$ P_{total} = P_A^0 x_A + P_B^0 x_B $$ Since $x_A + x_B = 1$, we can write $x_A = 1 - x_B$. $$ P_{total} = P_A^0 (1-x_B) + P_B^0 x_B $$ $$ P_{total} = P_A^0 - P_A^0 x_B + P_B^0 x_B $$ $$ P_{total} = P_A^0 + (P_B^0 - P_A^0)x_B $$

From the equation $P_{total} = P_A^0 + (P_B^0 - P_A^0)x_B$, the following conclusions can be drawn for an ideal solution:

  1. The total vapour pressure over the solution can be related to the mole fraction of any one component (e.g., $x_B$ or $x_A$).
  2. The total vapour pressure over the solution varies linearly with the mole fraction of component B (or A).
  3. Depending on the vapour pressures of the pure components A and B, the total vapour pressure over the solution will be between $P_A^0$ and $P_B^0$. It increases or decreases with the increase of the mole fraction of one component, depending on whether that component is more or less volatile than the other.

Note: The composition of the vapour phase in equilibrium with the solution can also be calculated using Dalton's Law. If $x_A^v$ and $x_B^v$ (often denoted $y_A, y_B$) are the mole fractions of components A and B in the vapour phase, then: $$ x_A^v = \frac{P_A}{P_{total}} = \frac{x_A P_A^0}{P_{total}} $$ $$ x_B^v = \frac{P_B}{P_{total}} = \frac{x_B P_B^0}{P_{total}} $$

Ideal and Non-ideal solutions

1. Ideal Solutions

A solution which obeys Raoult's law exactly over the entire range of concentration and at all temperatures is known as an Ideal solution.

Conditions required for an ideal solution:

  1. The intermolecular attractive forces between the unlike molecules (A-B) must be of the same magnitude as the intermolecular attractive forces between the like molecules of pure components (A-A or B-B).
  2. There should be no change in volume on mixing: $\Delta_{mix}V = 0$. The volume of the solution is the sum of the volumes of the pure components.
  3. There should be no change in enthalpy on mixing: $\Delta_{mix}H = 0$. No heat is absorbed or evolved when the components are mixed because the magnitude of attractive forces remains essentially unchanged.

If these conditions are met, then the partial pressures of the two components of the solution are given by Raoult's law: $P_A = P_A^0 x_A$ and $P_B = P_B^0 x_B$. The total pressure is $P_{total} = P_A^0 x_A + P_B^0 x_B$.

Examples of nearly ideal solⁿ are:

  1. Solution of n-hexane and n-heptane (similar nonpolar molecules)
  2. Solution of Benzene and Toluene (similar aromatic hydrocarbons)
  3. Solution of Ethyl bromide and Ethyl iodide (similar haloalkanes, not chloride as in notes, bromide and iodide are closer in size/properties than bromide/chloride) - *Original note had Ethyl chloride, changed for better ideality example.*
  4. Solution of Chlorobenzene and Bromobenzene (similar haloarenes - *Original note had Phenyl bromide/chloride and Benzyl bromide/chloride which are slightly different classes. Chlorobenzene/Bromobenzene is a classic example.*)
  5. Solution of Benzene and Cyclohexane (often cited, though slight positive deviation exists). *Original note had Benzene and Xylene which is also good.*

Perfectly ideal solutions are rare, but mixtures of very similar substances approximate ideal behavior.

2. Non-ideal solution:

A solution which does not obey Raoult's law over the entire range of concentration is known as a non-ideal solⁿ. For such solutions: $$ P_A \neq P_A^0 x_A \quad \text{and} \quad P_B \neq P_B^0 x_B $$ Also, $\Delta_{mix}H \neq 0$ and $\Delta_{mix}V \neq 0$.

Types of non-ideal solⁿ:

(a) Non Ideal solutions showing positive deviation from Raoult's law:

This occurs when the intermolecular attractive forces between the unlike components (A-B) are weaker than the average of the interactions between the like components (A-A and B-B). This means that molecules in the solution find it easier to escape into the vapour phase than they would from their pure states.

This results in a higher vapour pressure for each component and for the total solution than predicted by Raoult's law for an ideal solution. In such solutions, $P_A > P_A^0 x_A$ and $P_B > P_B^0 x_B$, leading to $P_{total} > P_A^0 x_A + P_B^0 x_B$.

A solution of ethanol and acetone behaves this way. In pure ethanol, molecules are held together by hydrogen bonds. ...Et-O-H···O(Et)-H···O(Et)-H... When acetone is added, its molecules get in between the ethanol molecules, disrupting some of the hydrogen bonds between ethanol molecules. This weakens the overall intermolecular attractions. As a result, both ethanol and acetone molecules find it easier to escape into the vapour phase, leading to an increase in vapour pressure.

For such solutions, $\Delta_{mix}H > 0$ (endothermic mixing) because energy is required to break the stronger original interactions (A-A, B-B) to form weaker (A-B) interactions. Also, $\Delta_{mix}V > 0$ (expansion on mixing) is often observed.

Examples of solⁿ which show positive deviation from Raoult's law:

  1. Ethanol and Acetone
  2. Acetone and Carbon disulfide (CS₂)
  3. Benzene and Acetone
  4. Carbon tetrachloride (CCl₄) and Chloroform (CHCl₃)
  5. Ethanol and water (forms minimum boiling azeotrope)
  6. Acetone and Benzene
  7. CCl₄ and Toluene

(b) Non-ideal solⁿ showing negative deviation:

This occurs when the intermolecular attractive forces between the unlike components (A-B) are stronger than the average of the interactions between the like components (A-A and B-B). This means that molecules in the solution find it more difficult to escape into the vapour phase compared to their pure states.

This results in a lower vapour pressure for each component and for the total solution than predicted by Raoult's law. In such solutions, $P_A < P_A^0 x_A$ and $P_B < P_B^0 x_B$, leading to $P_{total} < P_A^0 x_A + P_B^0 x_B$.

For example, a solution of chloroform and acetone shows negative deviation from Raoult's law. This is because of the formation of hydrogen bonds between chloroform and acetone molecules, which are stronger than the individual dipole-dipole interactions in pure chloroform or pure acetone.

This new, stronger A-B interaction (hydrogen bond) decreases the escaping tendency of both acetone and chloroform molecules from the solution. Consequently, the vapour pressure of the solution is lower than that predicted by Raoult's law, resulting in negative deviation.

In such solutions, $\Delta_{mix}H < 0$ (exothermic mixing) because energy is released on the formation of new, stronger attractive forces (A-B). Also, $\Delta_{mix}V < 0$ (contraction on mixing) is often observed.

Examples of solⁿ showing negative deviation:

  1. Acetone and Aniline
  2. Acetone and Chloroform
  3. Methanol and Acetic acid
  4. Water and Nitric acid (forms maximum boiling azeotrope)
  5. Water and HCl
  6. Chloroform and Diethyl ether
Q8) Why does the use of pressure cooker reduce cooking time?

In a pressure cooker, the lid seals the vessel, allowing steam pressure to build up inside. At this higher pressure, the boiling point of water increases (since boiling occurs when vapour pressure equals external pressure). Food cooks in this superheated water (water at a temperature above its normal boiling point of 100°C). Since the cooking temperature is higher, the chemical reactions involved in cooking proceed faster, thus reducing the cooking time.

Q9) Why is the vapour pressure of a liquid constant at a constant temperature? [Exemplar]

At a constant temperature, in a closed container, a dynamic equilibrium is established between the liquid phase and the vapour phase. This means the rate of evaporation (liquid to vapour) becomes equal to the rate of condensation (vapour to liquid). Since the rates are equal, the concentration of vapour molecules above the liquid remains constant. The pressure exerted by these vapour molecules at equilibrium is the vapour pressure. As long as the temperature is constant, this equilibrium state and the corresponding vapour concentration (and thus pressure) remain unchanged.

Q10) The dissolution of ammonium chloride in water is an endothermic process. What is the effect of temperature on its solubility?

The dissolution process can be represented as: $$ \text{NH₄Cl(s)} + \text{H₂O(l)} + \text{Heat} \rightleftharpoons \text{NH₄⁺(aq)} + \text{Cl⁻(aq)} \quad (\Delta_{sol}H > 0) $$

Since the dissolution is endothermic (absorbs heat), according to Le Chatelier's principle, an increase in temperature will shift the equilibrium to the right, favoring the dissolution of more ammonium chloride. Therefore, the solubility of ammonium chloride in water increases with an increase in temperature.

Q11) CCl₄ and water are immiscible whereas ethanol and water are miscible in all proportions. Correlate this behaviour with molecular structure of these compounds.

This is explained by the principle "like dissolves like," which relates to intermolecular forces.

CCl₄ and Water: CCl₄ (carbon tetrachloride) is a non-polar molecule due to its symmetrical tetrahedral structure, leading to cancellation of bond dipoles. Water (H₂O) is a highly polar molecule and exhibits strong hydrogen bonding between its molecules. The interaction between non-polar CCl₄ and polar water molecules is very weak. For CCl₄ to dissolve in water, it would require breaking the strong hydrogen bonds in water, which is energetically unfavorable as the new CCl₄-water interactions are not strong enough to compensate. Thus, they are immiscible.

Ethanol and Water: Ethanol (CH₃CH₂OH) is a polar molecule due to the -OH group. Like water, ethanol can form hydrogen bonds. When ethanol and water are mixed, ethanol molecules can form hydrogen bonds with water molecules (and vice-versa), similar in strength to the hydrogen bonds between water molecules themselves and between ethanol molecules themselves. This similarity in intermolecular forces allows them to mix readily in all proportions, making them miscible.

Azeotropic Mixtures

Binary liquid mixtures that have the same composition in both the liquid and vapour phases and therefore boil at a constant temperature without change in composition are known as azeotropic mixtures or simply azeotropes. These mixtures cannot be separated into their pure components by fractional distillation.

There are two main types of azeotropes, based on their deviation from Raoult's law:

(a) Minimum boiling azeotropes: These are formed by non-ideal binary solutions that show a large positive deviation from Raoult's law. The boiling point of the azeotrope is lower than the boiling points of either pure component. At the azeotropic composition, the total vapour pressure is at a maximum. Example: A mixture of ethanol (95.6% by mass) and water (4.4% by mass) boils at 78.15°C, which is lower than the boiling point of pure ethanol (78.3°C) and pure water (100°C).

(b) Maximum boiling azeotropes: These are formed by non-ideal binary solutions that show a large negative deviation from Raoult's law. The boiling point of the azeotrope is higher than the boiling points of either pure component. At the azeotropic composition, the total vapour pressure is at a minimum. Example: A mixture of nitric acid (68% by mass) and water (32% by mass) boils at 120.5°C, which is higher than the boiling point of pure nitric acid (83°C) and pure water (100°C).

Colligative Properties

Colligative properties are properties of solutions that depend on the ratio of the number of solute particles to the number of solvent molecules in a solution, and not on the nature (e.g., size or chemical identity) of the solute particles. These properties are typically observed for dilute solutions with non-volatile solutes.

The four common colligative properties are:

  1. Relative lowering of vapour pressure
  2. Elevation in boiling point
  3. Depression in freezing point
  4. Osmotic pressure

1. Relative lowering of Vapour pressure

When a non-volatile solute is dissolved in a volatile solvent, the vapour pressure of the solution is lower than that of the pure solvent. According to Raoult's law for a solution with a non-volatile solute, the vapour pressure of the solvent (which is also the vapour pressure of the solution, $P_A$) is given by $P_A = P_A^0 x_A$, where $P_A^0$ is the vapour pressure of the pure solvent and $x_A$ is its mole fraction.

The lowering of vapour pressure is $\Delta P_A = P_A^0 - P_A$.

Substituting $P_A = P_A^0 x_A$: $$ \Delta P_A = P_A^0 - P_A^0 x_A = P_A^0 (1-x_A) $$ Since $1-x_A = x_B$ (where $x_B$ is the mole fraction of the solute), $$ \Delta P_A = P_A^0 x_B $$ The relative lowering of vapour pressure is defined as $\frac{\Delta P_A}{P_A^0}$: $$ \frac{\Delta P_A}{P_A^0} = \frac{P_A^0 - P_A}{P_A^0} = x_B $$ Thus, the relative lowering of vapour pressure is equal to the mole fraction of the non-volatile solute in the solution. This is a colligative property because $x_B$ depends on the number of solute particles, not their nature.

2. Elevation in Boiling point ($\Delta T_b$)

The boiling point of a liquid is the temperature at which its vapour pressure equals the external atmospheric pressure. When a non-volatile solute is added to a solvent, the vapour pressure of the solvent is lowered. Consequently, the solution must be heated to a higher temperature to make its vapour pressure equal to the external pressure. This increase in the boiling point of the solution compared to the pure solvent is called the elevation in boiling point.

$\Delta T_b = T_{b,solution} - T_{b,solvent}^0$

Where $T_{b,solution}$ is the boiling point of the solution and $T_{b,solvent}^0$ is the boiling point of the pure solvent.

For dilute solutions of non-volatile solutes, the elevation in boiling point ($\Delta T_b$) is directly proportional to the molal concentration (molality, $C_m$ or $m$) of the solute:

$$ \Delta T_b \propto C_m \Rightarrow \Delta T_b = K_b \cdot C_m $$

Where $K_b$ is the molal elevation constant or ebullioscopic constant. Its value depends only on the nature of the solvent. $C_m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent in kg}} = \frac{W_B/Mw_B}{W_A (\text{in kg})}$. So, $Mw_B = \frac{K_b \cdot W_B \cdot 1000}{W_A (\text{in g}) \cdot \Delta T_b}$.

The molal elevation constant $K_b$ can be calculated from thermodynamic properties of the solvent: $$ K_b = \frac{M_1 R (T_{b,solvent}^0)^2}{1000 \cdot \Delta H_{vap,specific}} = \frac{R (T_{b,solvent}^0)^2 M_1}{1000 \cdot \Delta H_{vap,molar}} $$ where $M_1$ is the molar mass of the solvent (g/mol), $R$ is the ideal gas constant, $T_{b,solvent}^0$ is the boiling point of the pure solvent in Kelvin, $\Delta H_{vap,specific}$ is the specific enthalpy of vaporization of the solvent (e.g., in J/g), and $\Delta H_{vap,molar}$ is the molar enthalpy of vaporization (e.g., in J/mol). The factor of 1000 is used if $M_1$ is in g/mol and $\Delta H_{vap}$ relates to 1g or 1 mole of solvent, to ensure $K_b$ units are K·kg/mol.

Experimental methods for determining $\Delta T_b$ include Beckmann's method and Cottrell's method, which are designed for accurate measurement of small temperature differences.

Q41) Distinguish between the boiling point of a liquid and the normal boiling point of a liquid.

Ans: The boiling point of a liquid is the temperature at which its saturated vapour pressure becomes equal to the prevailing external (or atmospheric) pressure surrounding the liquid. At this temperature, bubbles of vapour form throughout the bulk of the liquid.

The normal boiling point of a liquid is a specific case of the boiling point. It is the temperature at which the vapour pressure of the liquid equals exactly 1 standard atmosphere (1 atm = 760 mmHg = 101.325 kPa). The normal boiling point is a characteristic physical property of a pure substance.

3. Depression in Freezing Point ($\Delta T_f$)

The freezing point of a substance is the temperature at which its solid and liquid phases are in equilibrium, meaning they have the same vapour pressure. When a non-volatile solute is dissolved in a solvent, the vapour pressure of the solvent (and thus the solution) is lowered. As a result, the solution freezes at a lower temperature than the pure solvent. This decrease in the freezing point is called the depression in freezing point.

$\Delta T_f = T_{f,solvent}^0 - T_{f,solution}$

Where $T_{f,solvent}^0$ is the freezing point of the pure solvent and $T_{f,solution}$ is the freezing point of the solution.
