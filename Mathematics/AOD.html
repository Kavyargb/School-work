APPLICATION OF DERIVATIVES

6.1 Introduction

In Chapter 5, we have learnt how to find derivative of composite functions, inverse trigonometric functions, implicit functions, exponential functions and logarithmic functions. In this chapter, we will study applications of the derivative in various disciplines, e.g., in engineering, science, social science, and many other fields. For instance, we will learn how the derivative can be used (i) to determine rate of change of quantities, (ii) to find the equations of tangent and normal to a curve at a point, (iii) to find turning points on the graph of a function which in turn will help us to locate points at which largest or smallest value (locally) of a function occurs. We will also use derivative to find intervals on which a function is increasing or decreasing. Finally, we use the derivative to find approximate value of certain quantities.

6.2 Rate of Change of Quantities

Recall that by the derivative $\frac{ds}{dt}$ we mean the rate of change of distance $s$ with respect to the time $t$. In a similar fashion, whenever one quantity $y$ varies with another quantity $x$, satisfying some rule $y = f(x)$, then $\frac{dy}{dx}$ (or $f'(x)$) represents the rate of change of $y$ with respect to $x$ and $\frac{dy}{dx}\Big|_{x=x_0}$ (or $f'(x_0)$) represents the rate of change of $y$ with respect to $x$ at $x = x_0$.

Further, if two variables $x$ and $y$ are varying with respect to another variable $t$, i.e., if $x = f(t)$ and $y = g(t)$, then by Chain Rule

$$ \frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{dy}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} \text{, if } \frac{dx}{dt} \neq 0 $$

Example 1

Find the rate of change of the area of a circle per second with respect to its radius $r$ when $r = 5$ cm.

The area $A$ of a circle with radius $r$ is given by $A = \pi r^2$. Therefore, the rate of change of the area $A$ with respect to its radius $r$ is given by $\frac{dA}{dr} = \frac{d}{dr}(\pi r^2) = 2\pi r$.

When $r = 5$ cm, $\frac{dA}{dr}\Big|_{r=5cm} = 2\pi(5) = 10\pi$. Thus, the area of the circle is changing at the rate of $10\pi$ cm²/s.

Example 2

The volume of a cube is increasing at a rate of 9 cubic centimetres per second. How fast is the surface area increasing when the length of an edge is 10 centimetres ?

Let $x$ be the length of a side, $V$ be the volume and $S$ be the surface area of the cube. Then, $V = x^3$ and $S = 6x^2$, where $x$ is a function of time $t$.

Now $\frac{dV}{dt} = 9 \text{ cm}^3/s$ (Given)

Therefore $9 = \frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{d}{dx}(x^3)\cdot\frac{dx}{dt}$ (By Chain Rule)

$ = 3x^2 \frac{dx}{dt}$

or $\frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{9}{3x^2} = \frac{3}{x^2}$ (1)

Now $\frac{dS}{dt} = \frac{d}{dx}(6x^2)\cdot\frac{dx}{dt}$ (By Chain Rule)

$ = 12x \cdot \frac{3}{x^2}$ (Using (1))

$ = \frac{36}{x}$

Hence, when $x = 10$ cm, $\frac{dS}{dt}\Big|_{x=10cm} = \frac{36}{10} = 3.6$ cm²/s

Example 3

A stone is dropped into a quiet lake and waves move in circles at a speed of 4cm per second. At the instant, when the radius of the circular wave is 10 cm, how fast is the enclosed area increasing?

The area $A$ of a circle with radius $r$ is given by $A = \pi r^2$. Therefore, the rate of change of area $A$ with respect to time $t$ is

$$ \frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{d}{dt}(\pi r^2) = \frac{d}{dr}(\pi r^2) \cdot \frac{dr}{dt} = 2\pi r \frac{dr}{dt} $$

It is given that $\frac{dr}{dt} = 4\text{cm/s}$

Therefore, when $r = 10$ cm, $\frac{dA}{dt} = 2\pi (10) (4) = 80\pi$

Thus, the enclosed area is increasing at the rate of $80\pi \text{ cm}^2/s$, when $r = 10 \text{ cm}$.

Note $\frac{dy}{dx}$ is positive if $y$ increases as $x$ increases and is negative if $y$ decreases as $x$ increases.

Example 4

The length $x$ of a rectangle is decreasing at the rate of 3 cm/minute and the width $y$ is increasing at the rate of 2cm/minute. When $x = 10$cm and $y = 6$cm, find the rates of change of (a) the perimeter and (b) the area of the rectangle.

Since the length $x$ is decreasing and the width $y$ is increasing with respect to time, we have

$\frac{dx}{dt} = -3\text{ cm/min}$ and $\frac{dy}{dt} = 2\text{ cm/min}$

(a) The perimeter $P$ of a rectangle is given by

$P = 2 (x + y)$

Therefore $\frac{dP}{dt} = 2\left(\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{dy}{dt}\right) = 2 (-3+2)=-2\text{ cm/min}$

(b) The area $A$ of the rectangle is given by

$A=x\cdot y$

Therefore $\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{dx}{dt} \cdot y + x \cdot \frac{dy}{dt}$

$ = -3(6) + 10(2)$ (as $x = 10$ cm and $y = 6$ cm)

$ = -18 + 20 = 2\text{ cm}^2/\text{min}$

Example 5

The total cost $C(x)$ in Rupees, associated with the production of $x$ units of an item is given by

$C(x) = 0.005 x^3 – 0.02 x^2 + 30x + 5000$

Find the marginal cost when 3 units are produced, where by marginal cost we mean the instantaneous rate of change of total cost at any level of output.

Since marginal cost is the rate of change of total cost with respect to the output, we have

Marginal cost (MC) = $\frac{dC}{dx} = 0.005(3x^2) - 0.02(2x) +30$

When $x = 3$, MC = $0.015(3^2) - 0.04(3) +30$

$ = 0.015(9) - 0.12 + 30 = 0.135 - 0.12 + 30 = 30.015$

Hence, the required marginal cost is ₹30.02 (nearly).

Example 6

The total revenue in Rupees received from the sale of $x$ units of a product is given by $R(x) = 3x^2 + 36x + 5$. Find the marginal revenue, when $x = 5$, where by marginal revenue we mean the rate of change of total revenue with respect to the number of items sold at an instant.

Since marginal revenue is the rate of change of total revenue with respect to the number of units sold, we have

Marginal Revenue (MR) = $\frac{dR}{dx} = 6x + 36$

When $x = 5$, MR = $6(5) + 36 = 30 + 36 = 66$

Hence, the required marginal revenue is ₹66.

1. Find the rate of change of the area of a circle with respect to its radius r when (a) r = 3 cm (b) r = 4 cm

Let $A$ be the area of the circle with radius $r$. Then $A = \pi r^2$.

The rate of change of the area with respect to the radius $r$ is $\frac{dA}{dr} = \frac{d}{dr}(\pi r^2) = 2\pi r$.

(a) When $r = 3$ cm, $\frac{dA}{dr} = 2\pi (3) = 6\pi \text{ cm}^2/\text{cm}$.

(b) When $r = 4$ cm, $\frac{dA}{dr} = 2\pi (4) = 8\pi \text{ cm}^2/\text{cm}$.

2. The volume of a cube is increasing at the rate of 8 cm³/s. How fast is the surface area increasing when the length of an edge is 12 cm?

Let $x$ be the length of the edge of the cube. Let $V$ be its volume and $S$ be its surface area.

Given $\frac{dV}{dt} = 8 \text{ cm}^3/\text{s}$. We need to find $\frac{dS}{dt}$ when $x=12$ cm.

$V = x^3 \implies \frac{dV}{dt} = 3x^2 \frac{dx}{dt}$

$8 = 3x^2 \frac{dx}{dt} \implies \frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{8}{3x^2}$

$S = 6x^2 \implies \frac{dS}{dt} = 12x \frac{dx}{dt}$

Substitute $\frac{dx}{dt}$:

$\frac{dS}{dt} = 12x \left(\frac{8}{3x^2}\right) = \frac{96x}{3x^2} = \frac{32}{x}$

When $x = 12$ cm:

$\frac{dS}{dt}\Big|_{x=12} = \frac{32}{12} = \frac{8}{3} \text{ cm}^2/\text{s}$.

3. The radius of a circle is increasing uniformly at the rate of 3 cm/s. Find the rate at which the area of the circle is increasing when the radius is 10 cm.

Let $r$ be the radius of the circle and $A$ be its area.

Given $\frac{dr}{dt} = 3 \text{ cm/s}$. We need to find $\frac{dA}{dt}$ when $r=10$ cm.

$A = \pi r^2$

$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{d}{dr}(\pi r^2) \cdot \frac{dr}{dt} = 2\pi r \frac{dr}{dt}$

Substitute the given values:

$\frac{dA}{dt} = 2\pi (10) (3) = 60\pi \text{ cm}^2/\text{s}$.

4. An edge of a variable cube is increasing at the rate of 3 cm/s. How fast is the volume of the cube increasing when the edge is 10 cm long?

Let $x$ be the length of an edge of the cube and $V$ be its volume.

Given $\frac{dx}{dt} = 3 \text{ cm/s}$. We need to find $\frac{dV}{dt}$ when $x=10$ cm.

$V = x^3$

$\frac{dV}{dt} = 3x^2 \frac{dx}{dt}$

Substitute the given values:

$\frac{dV}{dt} = 3 (10)^2 (3) = 3 (100) (3) = 900 \text{ cm}^3/\text{s}$.

5. A stone is dropped into a quiet lake and waves move in circles at the speed of 5 cm/s. At the instant when the radius of the circular wave is 8 cm, how fast is the enclosed area increasing?

Let $r$ be the radius of the circular wave and $A$ be its area.

Given $\frac{dr}{dt} = 5 \text{ cm/s}$. We need to find $\frac{dA}{dt}$ when $r=8$ cm.

$A = \pi r^2$

$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{d}{dr}(\pi r^2) \cdot \frac{dr}{dt} = 2\pi r \frac{dr}{dt}$

Substitute the given values:

$\frac{dA}{dt} = 2\pi (8) (5) = 80\pi \text{ cm}^2/\text{s}$.

6. The radius of a circle is increasing at the rate of 0.7 cm/s. What is the rate of increase of its circumference?

Let $r$ be the radius of the circle and $C$ be its circumference.

Given $\frac{dr}{dt} = 0.7 \text{ cm/s}$. We need to find $\frac{dC}{dt}$.

$C = 2\pi r$

$\frac{dC}{dt} = \frac{d}{dr}(2\pi r) \cdot \frac{dr}{dt} = 2\pi \frac{dr}{dt}$

Substitute the given value:

$\frac{dC}{dt} = 2\pi (0.7) = 1.4\pi \text{ cm/s}$.

7. The length x of a rectangle is decreasing at the rate of 5 cm/minute and the width y is increasing at the rate of 4 cm/minute. When x = 8cm and y = 6cm, find the rates of change of (a) the perimeter, and (b) the area of the rectangle.

Given $\frac{dx}{dt} = -5 \text{ cm/min}$ and $\frac{dy}{dt} = 4 \text{ cm/min}$.

We are given $x = 8$ cm and $y = 6$ cm.

(a) Perimeter $P = 2(x+y)$

$\frac{dP}{dt} = 2\left(\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{dy}{dt}\right) = 2(-5 + 4) = 2(-1) = -2 \text{ cm/min}$.

(b) Area $A = xy$

$\frac{dA}{dt} = x\frac{dy}{dt} + y\frac{dx}{dt}$ (Product Rule)

$\frac{dA}{dt} = (8)(4) + (6)(-5) = 32 - 30 = 2 \text{ cm}^2/\text{min}$.

8. A balloon, which always remains spherical on inflation, is being inflated by pumping in 900 cubic centimetres of gas per second. Find the rate at which the radius of the balloon increases when the radius is 15 cm.

Let $V$ be the volume and $r$ be the radius of the spherical balloon.

Given $\frac{dV}{dt} = 900 \text{ cm}^3/\text{s}$. We need to find $\frac{dr}{dt}$ when $r=15$ cm.

$V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$

$\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{4}{3}\pi (3r^2) \frac{dr}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \frac{dr}{dt}$

Substitute the given values:

$900 = 4\pi (15)^2 \frac{dr}{dt}$

$900 = 4\pi (225) \frac{dr}{dt}$

$900 = 900\pi \frac{dr}{dt}$

$\frac{dr}{dt} = \frac{900}{900\pi} = \frac{1}{\pi} \text{ cm/s}$.

9. A balloon, which always remains spherical has a variable radius. Find the rate at which its volume is increasing with the radius when the later is 10 cm.

Let $V$ be the volume and $r$ be the radius of the spherical balloon.

We need to find $\frac{dV}{dr}$ when $r=10$ cm.

$V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$

$\frac{dV}{dr} = \frac{4}{3}\pi (3r^2) = 4\pi r^2$

When $r = 10$ cm:

$\frac{dV}{dr}\Big|_{r=10} = 4\pi (10)^2 = 4\pi (100) = 400\pi \text{ cm}^3/\text{cm}$.

10. A ladder 5 m long is leaning against a wall. The bottom of the ladder is pulled along the ground, away from the wall, at the rate of 2cm/s. How fast is its height on the wall decreasing when the foot of the ladder is 4 m away from the wall ?

Let $x$ be the distance of the foot of the ladder from the wall and $y$ be the height of the ladder on the wall. The length of the ladder is $L=5$ m.

By Pythagorean theorem: $x^2 + y^2 = L^2 \implies x^2 + y^2 = 5^2 = 25$.

Given $\frac{dx}{dt} = 2 \text{ cm/s} = 0.02 \text{ m/s}$. We need to find $\frac{dy}{dt}$ when $x=4$ m.

Differentiate $x^2 + y^2 = 25$ with respect to $t$:

$2x \frac{dx}{dt} + 2y \frac{dy}{dt} = 0$

$x \frac{dx}{dt} + y \frac{dy}{dt} = 0$

When $x = 4$ m, we can find $y$ using $x^2 + y^2 = 25$:

$4^2 + y^2 = 25 \implies 16 + y^2 = 25 \implies y^2 = 9 \implies y = 3$ m (since height must be positive).

Now substitute $x=4$, $y=3$, and $\frac{dx}{dt}=0.02$ into the differentiated equation:

$4 (0.02) + 3 \frac{dy}{dt} = 0$

$0.08 + 3 \frac{dy}{dt} = 0$

$3 \frac{dy}{dt} = -0.08$

$\frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{0.08}{3} = -\frac{8}{300} = -\frac{2}{75} \text{ m/s}$.

The height on the wall is decreasing at the rate of $\frac{2}{75} \text{ m/s}$.

11. A particle moves along the curve $6y = x^3 + 2$. Find the points on the curve at which the y-coordinate is changing 8 times as fast as the x-coordinate.

Given the curve $6y = x^3 + 2$.

Differentiate with respect to time $t$:

$6 \frac{dy}{dt} = 3x^2 \frac{dx}{dt}$

Given that the $y$-coordinate is changing 8 times as fast as the $x$-coordinate, so $\frac{dy}{dt} = 8 \frac{dx}{dt}$.

Substitute this into the differentiated equation:

$6 \left(8 \frac{dx}{dt}\right) = 3x^2 \frac{dx}{dt}$

$48 \frac{dx}{dt} = 3x^2 \frac{dx}{dt}$

Since the particle is moving, $\frac{dx}{dt} \neq 0$. We can divide by $\frac{dx}{dt}$:

$48 = 3x^2$

$x^2 = \frac{48}{3} = 16$

$x = \pm 4$

Now find the corresponding $y$-coordinates using $6y = x^3 + 2$:

If $x = 4$: $6y = (4)^3 + 2 = 64 + 2 = 66 \implies y = 11$. So, point is $(4, 11)$.

If $x = -4$: $6y = (-4)^3 + 2 = -64 + 2 = -62 \implies y = -\frac{62}{6} = -\frac{31}{3}$. So, point is $(-4, -\frac{31}{3})$.

The points on the curve are $(4, 11)$ and $(-4, -\frac{31}{3})$.

12. The radius of an air bubble is increasing at the rate of $\frac{1}{2}$ cm/s. At what rate is the volume of the bubble increasing when the radius is 1 cm?

Let $V$ be the volume and $r$ be the radius of the air bubble (spherical).

$\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{4}{3}\pi (3r^2) \frac{dr}{dt} = 4\pi r^2 \frac{dr}{dt}$

Substitute the given values:

$\frac{dV}{dt} = 4\pi (1)^2 \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 4\pi (1) \left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = 2\pi \text{ cm}^3/\text{s}$.

13. A balloon, which always remains spherical, has a variable diameter $\frac{3}{2}(2x+1)$. Find the rate of change of its volume with respect to x.

Let $V$ be the volume of the spherical balloon. Given diameter $D = \frac{3}{2}(2x+1)$.

Radius $r = \frac{D}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{3}{2}(2x+1) = \frac{3}{4}(2x+1)$.

Volume $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 = \frac{4}{3}\pi \left(\frac{3}{4}(2x+1)\right)^3$

$V = \frac{4}{3}\pi \left(\frac{27}{64}\right)(2x+1)^3 = \frac{9\pi}{16}(2x+1)^3$

We need to find the rate of change of its volume with respect to $x$, i.e., $\frac{dV}{dx}$.

$\frac{dV}{dx} = \frac{9\pi}{16} \cdot 3(2x+1)^2 \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(2x+1)$

$\frac{dV}{dx} = \frac{9\pi}{16} \cdot 3(2x+1)^2 \cdot 2$

$\frac{dV}{dx} = \frac{27\pi}{8}(2x+1)^2$.

$V = \frac{1}{3}\pi (6h)^2 h = \frac{1}{3}\pi (36h^2) h = 12\pi h^3$

$\frac{dV}{dt} = 12\pi (3h^2) \frac{dh}{dt} = 36\pi h^2 \frac{dh}{dt}$

$12 = 36\pi (4)^2 \frac{dh}{dt}$

$12 = 36\pi (16) \frac{dh}{dt}$

$12 = 576\pi \frac{dh}{dt}$

$\frac{dh}{dt} = \frac{12}{576\pi} = \frac{1}{48\pi} \text{ cm/s}$.

$ = 20.967$

The marginal cost when 17 units are produced is ₹20.967.

6.3 Increasing and Decreasing Functions

In this section, we will use differentiation to find out whether a function is increasing or decreasing or none.

Consider the function $f$ given by $f(x) = x^2, x \in R$. The graph of this function is a parabola (See Figure 6.1 in textbook).

Definition 1

Let $I$ be an interval contained in the domain of a real valued function $f$. Then $f$ is said to be

(i) increasing on $I$ if $x_1 < x_2$ in $I \rightarrow f(x_1) \leq f(x_2)$ for all $x_1, x_2 \in I$.

(ii) decreasing on $I$, if $x_1 < x_2$ in $I \rightarrow f(x_1) \geq f(x_2)$ for all $x_1, x_2 \in I$.

(iii) constant on $I$, if $f(x) = c$ for all $x \in I$, where $c$ is a constant.

(iv) strictly increasing on $I$ if $x_1 < x_2$ in $I \rightarrow f(x_1) < f(x_2)$ for all $x_1, x_2 \in I$.

(v) strictly decreasing on $I$ if $x_1 < x_2$ in $I \rightarrow f(x_1) > f(x_2)$ for all $x_1, x_2 \in I$.

For graphical representation of such functions see Figure 6.2 in textbook.

Definition 2

Let $x_0$ be a point in the domain of definition of a real valued function $f$. Then $f$ is said to be increasing, decreasing at $x_0$ if there exists an open interval $I$ containing $x_0$ such that $f$ is increasing, decreasing, respectively, in $I$.

Example 7

Show that the function given by $f(x) = 7x – 3$ is increasing on R.

Let $x_1$ and $x_2$ be any two numbers in R.

Then $x_1 < x_2 \rightarrow 7x_1 < 7x_2 \rightarrow 7x_1 – 3 < 7x_2 – 3 \rightarrow f(x_1) < f(x_2)$

Thus, by Definition 1, it follows that $f$ is strictly increasing on R.

Theorem 1 (First Derivative Test)

Let $f$ be continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on the open interval $(a,b)$. Then

(a) $f$ is increasing in $[a,b]$ if $f'(x) \geq 0$ for each $x \in (a, b)$

(b) $f$ is decreasing in $[a,b]$ if $f'(x) \leq 0$ for each $x \in (a, b)$

(c) $f$ is a constant function in $[a,b]$ if $f'(x) = 0$ for each $x \in (a, b)$

Proof (a): Let $x_1, x_2 \in [a, b]$ be such that $x_1 < x_2$. Then, by Mean Value Theorem (Theorem 8 in Chapter 5), there exists a point $c$ between $x_1$ and $x_2$ such that

$$ f(x_2) – f(x_1) = f'(c) (x_2 – x_1) $$

i.e. $f(x_2) - f(x_1) > 0$ (as $f'(c) > 0$ (given))

i.e. $f(x_2) > f(x_1)$

Thus, we have $x_1 < x_2 \rightarrow f(x_1) < f(x_2)$, for all $x_1,x_2 \in[a,b]$

Hence, $f$ is an increasing function in $[a,b]$.

The proofs of part (b) and (c) are similar. It is left as an exercise to the reader.

Remarks

There is a more generalised theorem, which states that if $f'(x) > 0$ for $x$ in an interval excluding the end points and $f$ is continuous in the interval, then $f$ is increasing. Similarly, if $f'(x) < 0$ for $x$ in an interval excluding the end points and $f$ is continuous in the interval, then $f$ is decreasing.

Example 8

Show that the function given by $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 4x$, $x \in R$ is increasing on R.

Note that $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x + 4$

$ = 3(x^2 – 2x + 1) + 1$

$ = 3(x - 1)^2 + 1$

Since $(x - 1)^2 \geq 0$ for all $x \in R$, $3(x - 1)^2 \geq 0$, so $3(x - 1)^2 + 1 \geq 1 > 0$.

Therefore, $f'(x) > 0$ in every interval of R.

Therefore, the function $f$ is increasing on R.

Example 9

Prove that the function given by $f(x) = \cos x$ is
(a) decreasing in $(0, \pi)$
(b) increasing in $(\pi, 2\pi)$, and
(c) neither increasing nor decreasing in $(0, 2\pi)$.

Note that $f'(x) = - \sin x$

(a) Since for each $x \in (0, \pi)$, $\sin x > 0$, we have $f'(x) = -\sin x < 0$ and so $f$ is decreasing in $(0, \pi)$.

(b) Since for each $x \in (\pi, 2\pi)$, $\sin x < 0$, we have $f'(x) = -\sin x > 0$ and so $f$ is increasing in $(\pi, 2\pi)$.

6.4 Maxima and Minima

In this section, we will use the concept of derivatives to calculate the maximum or minimum values of various functions. In fact, we will find the 'turning points' of the graph of a function and thus find points at which the graph reaches its highest (or lowest) locally. The knowledge of such points is very useful in sketching the graph of a given function. Further, we will also find the absolute maximum and absolute minimum of a function that are necessary for the solution of many applied problems.

Let us consider the following problems that arise in day to day life.

(i) The profit from a grove of orange trees is given by $P(x) = ax + bx^2$, where $a,b$ are constants and $x$ is the number of orange trees per acre. How many trees per acre will maximise the profit?

(ii) A ball, thrown into the air from a building 60 metres high, travels along a path given by $h(x) = 60+x-\frac{x^2}{60}$, where $x$ is the horizontal distance from the building and $h(x)$ is the height of the ball. What is the maximum height the ball will reach?

(iii) An Apache helicopter of enemy is flying along the path given by the curve $f(x) = x^2 + 7$. A soldier, placed at the point (1, 2), wants to shoot the helicopter when it is nearest to him. What is the nearest distance?

Definition 3

Let $f$ be a function defined on an interval $I$. Then

(a) $f$ is said to have a maximum value in $I$, if there exists a point $c$ in $I$ such that $f(c) \geq f(x)$, for all $x \in I$. The number $f(c)$ is called the maximum value of $f$ in $I$ and the point $c$ is called a point of maximum value of $f$ in $I$.

(b) $f$ is said to have a minimum value in $I$, if there exists a point $c$ in $I$ such that $f(c) \leq f(x)$, for all $x \in I$. The number $f(c)$, in this case, is called the minimum value of $f$ in $I$ and the point $c$, in this case, is called a point of minimum value of $f$ in $I$.

(c) $f$ is said to have an extreme value in $I$ if there exists a point $c$ in $I$ such that $f(c)$ is either a maximum value or a minimum value of $f$ in $I$. The number $f(c)$, in this case, is called an extreme value of $f$ in $I$ and the point $c$ is called an extreme point.

Remark

In Fig 6.7(a), (b) and (c), we have exhibited that graphs of certain particular functions help us to find maximum value and minimum value at a point. Infact, through graphs, we can even find maximum/minimum value of a function at a point at which it is not even differentiable (Example 15).

The reader may observe that in Example 16, if we include the points 0 and 1 in the domain of $f$, i.e., if we extend the domain of $f$ to $[0,1]$, then the function $f$ has minimum value 0 at $x = 0$ and maximum value 1 at $x = 1$. Infact, we have the following results (The proof of these results are beyond the scope of the present text)

Every monotonic function assumes its maximum/minimum value at the end points of the domain of definition of the function.

A more general result is

Every continuous function on a closed interval has a maximum and a minimum value.

Note By a monotonic function $f$ in an interval $I$, we mean that $f$ is either increasing in $I$ or decreasing in $I$.

Definition 4

Let $f$ be a real valued function and let $c$ be an interior point in the domain of $f$. Then

(a) $c$ is called a point of local maxima if there is an $h > 0$ such that $f(c) \geq f(x)$, for all $x$ in $(c – h, c + h)$, $x \neq c$. The value $f(c)$ is called the local maximum value of $f$.

(b) $c$ is called a point of local minima if there is an $h > 0$ such that $f(c) \leq f(x)$, for all $x$ in $(c – h, c + h)$. The value $f(c)$ is called the local minimum value of $f$.

Geometrically, the above definition states that if $x = c$ is a point of local maxima of $f$, then the graph of $f$ around $c$ will be as shown in Fig 6.12(a). Note that the function $f$ is increasing (i.e., $f'(x) > 0$) in the interval $(c – h, c)$ and decreasing (i.e., $f'(x) < 0$) in the interval $(c, c + h)$.

This suggests that $f'(c)$ must be zero. (See Figure 6.12 in textbook)

Theorem 2

Let $f$ be a function defined on an open interval $I$. Suppose $c \in I$ be any point. If $f$ has a local maxima or a local minima at $x = c$, then either $f'(c) = 0$ or $f$ is not differentiable at $c$.

Remark

The converse of above theorem need not be true, that is, a point at which the derivative vanishes need not be a point of local maxima or local minima. For example, if $f(x) = x^3$, then $f'(x) = 3x^2$ and so $f'(0) = 0$. But 0 is neither a point of local maxima nor a point of local minima (Fig 6.13).

Note A point $c$ in the domain of a function $f$ at which either $f'(c) = 0$ or $f$ is not differentiable is called a critical point of $f$. Note that if $f$ is continuous at $c$ and $f'(c) = 0$, then there exists an $h > 0$ such that $f$ is differentiable in the interval $(c – h, c + h)$.

Theorem 3 (First Derivative Test)

Let $f$ be a function defined on an open interval $I$. Let $f$ be continuous at a critical point $c$ in $I$. Then

(i) If $f'(x)$ changes sign from positive to negative as $x$ increases through $c$, i.e., if $f'(x) > 0$ at every point sufficiently close to and to the left of $c$, and $f'(x) < 0$ at every point sufficiently close to and to the right of $c$, then $c$ is a point of local maxima.

(ii) If $f'(x)$ changes sign from negative to positive as $x$ increases through $c$, i.e., if $f'(x) < 0$ at every point sufficiently close to and to the left of $c$, and $f'(x) > 0$ at every point sufficiently close to and to the right of $c$, then $c$ is a point of local minima.

(iii) If $f'(x)$ does not change sign as $x$ increases through $c$, then $c$ is neither a point of local maxima nor a point of local minima. Infact, such a point is called point of inflection (Fig 6.13).

Note If $c$ is a point of local maxima of $f$, then $f(c)$ is a local maximum value of $f$. Similarly, if $c$ is a point of local minima of $f$, then $f(c)$ is a local minimum value of $f$. Figures 6.13 and 6.14, geometrically explain Theorem 3.

We shall now give another test to examine local maxima and local minima of a given function. This test is often easier to apply than the first derivative test.

Theorem 4 (Second Derivative Test)

Let $f$ be a function defined on an interval $I$ and $c \in I$. Let $f$ be twice differentiable at $c$. Then

(i) $x = c$ is a point of local maxima if $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c) < 0$. The value $f(c)$ is local maximum value of $f$.

(ii) $x = c$ is a point of local minima if $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c) > 0$. In this case, $f(c)$ is local minimum value of $f$.

(iii) The test fails if $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c) = 0$. In this case, we go back to the first derivative test and find whether $c$ is a point of local maxima, local minima or a point of inflexion.

Note As $f$ is twice differentiable at $c$, we mean second order derivative of $f$ exists at $c$.

As $x$ increases through $0$, $f'(x)$ changes sign from negative to positive.

6.4.1 Maximum and Minimum Values of a Function in a Closed Interval

Let us consider a function $f$ given by $f(x) = x+2, x \in (0, 1)$.

Observe that the function is continuous on $(0, 1)$ and neither has a maximum value nor has a minimum value. Further, we may note that the function even has neither a local maximum value nor a local minimum value.

However, if we extend the domain of $f$ to the closed interval $[0, 1]$, then $f$ still may not have a local maximum (minimum) values but it certainly does have maximum value $3 = f(1)$ and minimum value $2 = f(0)$. The maximum value 3 of $f$ at $x = 1$ is called absolute maximum value (global maximum or greatest value) of $f$ on the interval $[0, 1]$. Similarly, the minimum value 2 of $f$ at $x = 0$ is called the absolute minimum value (global minimum or least value) of $f$ on $[0, 1]$.

Consider the graph given in Fig 6.19 of a continuous function defined on a closed interval $[a, d]$. (See Figure 6.19 in textbook)

Theorem 5

Let $f$ be a continuous function on an interval $I = [a, b]$. Then $f$ has the absolute maximum value and $f$ attains it at least once in $I$. Also, $f$ has the absolute minimum value and $f$ attains it at least once in $I$.

Theorem 6

Let $f$ be a differentiable function on a closed interval $I$ and let $c$ be any interior point of $I$. Then

(i) $f'(c) = 0$ if $f$ attains its absolute maximum value at $c$.

(ii) $f'(c) = 0$ if $f$ attains its absolute minimum value at $c$.

In view of the above results, we have the following working rule for finding absolute maximum and/or absolute minimum values of a function in a given closed interval $[a, b]$.

Working Rule

Step 1: Find all critical points of $f$ in the interval, i.e., find points $x$ where either $f'(x) = 0$ or $f$ is not differentiable.

Step 2: Take the end points of the interval.

Step 3: At all these points (listed in Step 1 and 2), calculate the values of $f$.

Step 4: Identify the maximum and minimum values of $f$ out of the values calculated in Step 3. This maximum value will be the absolute maximum (greatest) value of $f$ and the minimum value will be the absolute minimum (least) value of $f$.

The domain of $\log x$ is $(0, \infty)$. For all $x \in (0, \infty)$, $g'(x) = \frac{1}{x} > 0$. This means $g(x)$ is strictly increasing on its domain.

A strictly increasing function does not have any local maxima or minima.

Since the leading coefficient of $h'(x)$ ($3x^2+2x+1$) is positive (3 > 0) and $\Delta < 0$, $h'(x)$ is always positive ($h'(x) > 0$ for all $x \in R$).

A strictly increasing function does not have any local maxima or minima.

23. Prove that the volume of the largest cone that can be inscribed in a sphere of radius R is 8/27 of the volume of the sphere.

Let the sphere be centered at the origin, with equation $x^2+y^2=R^2$. Let the cone be inscribed with its vertex at $(0, R)$ and its base perpendicular to the y-axis.

Let the height of the cone be $h$ and the radius of its base be $r$.

The center of the cone's base will be at some point $(0, y_0)$ where $y_0 = R-h$.

The radius of the cone base, $r$, is the x-coordinate on the sphere at height $y_0$. $r^2 + y_0^2 = R^2 \implies r^2 = R^2 - (R-h)^2 = R^2 - (R^2 - 2Rh + h^2) = 2Rh - h^2$.

1. Show that the function $f(x) = \frac{\log x}{x}$ has maximum at $x=e$.

Given the function $f(x) = \frac{\log x}{x}$. The domain is $x > 0$.

To find the maximum, we first find the derivative using the quotient rule:

$f'(x) = \frac{x \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(\log x) - \log x \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(x)}{x^2}$

$f'(x) = \frac{x \cdot \frac{1}{x} - \log x \cdot 1}{x^2} = \frac{1 - \log x}{x^2}$.

Set the derivative to zero to find critical points:

$f'(x) = 0 \implies \frac{1 - \log x}{x^2} = 0$.

Since $x > 0$, we only need $1 - \log x = 0 \implies \log x = 1$.

Using the base $e$ for the natural logarithm, this gives $x = e$.

Now, we use the second derivative test to confirm it's a maximum. First, find $f''(x)$:

$f''(x) = \frac{x^2 \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(1 - \log x) - (1 - \log x) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(x^2)}{(x^2)^2}$

$f''(x) = \frac{x^2 \cdot (-\frac{1}{x}) - (1 - \log x) \cdot (2x)}{x^4} = \frac{-x - 2x(1 - \log x)}{x^4} = \frac{-1 - 2(1 - \log x)}{x^3} = \frac{-1 - 2 + 2\log x}{x^3} = \frac{2\log x - 3}{x^3}$.

Evaluate $f''(x)$ at the critical point $x=e$:

$f''(e) = \frac{2\log e - 3}{e^3} = \frac{2(1) - 3}{e^3} = \frac{-1}{e^3}$.

Since $f'(e) = 0$ and $f''(e) = -\frac{1}{e^3} < 0$, the function $f(x)$ has a local maximum at $x=e$. As this is the only critical point in the domain, it is the point of absolute maximum. Hence proved.

2. The two equal sides of an isosceles triangle with fixed base $b$ are decreasing at the rate of 3 cm per second. How fast is the area decreasing when the two equal sides are equal to the base?

Let the isosceles triangle be ABC with base BC = $b$ (fixed). Let the equal sides be AB = AC = $a$.

Given: $\frac{da}{dt} = -3$ cm/s.

The area of the triangle can be found by first finding the height. Let AD be the altitude from A to BC. Then D is the midpoint of BC, so BD = $b/2$.

Height $h = AD = \sqrt{AB^2 - BD^2} = \sqrt{a^2 - (b/2)^2} = \sqrt{a^2 - \frac{b^2}{4}}$.

Area of the triangle, $A = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} b h = \frac{b}{2}\sqrt{a^2 - \frac{b^2}{4}}$.

We need to find $\frac{dA}{dt}$ when $a=b$. Differentiate $A$ with respect to time $t$:

$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{b}{2} \cdot \frac{d}{dt}\left(a^2 - \frac{b^2}{4}\right)^{1/2}$

Using the chain rule: $\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{b}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\left(a^2 - \frac{b^2}{4}\right)^{-1/2} \cdot \frac{d}{dt}\left(a^2 - \frac{b^2}{4}\right)$

$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{b}{4\sqrt{a^2 - b^2/4}} \cdot (2a \frac{da}{dt} - 0)$ (since $b$ is fixed).

$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{2ab}{4\sqrt{a^2 - b^2/4}} \frac{da}{dt} = \frac{ab}{2\sqrt{a^2 - b^2/4}} \frac{da}{dt}$.

Now, substitute the given values: $a=b$ and $\frac{da}{dt} = -3$.

$\frac{dA}{dt}\bigg|_{a=b} = \frac{b \cdot b}{2\sqrt{b^2 - b^2/4}} (-3) = \frac{-3b^2}{2\sqrt{3b^2/4}} = \frac{-3b^2}{2 \cdot (\sqrt{3}b/2)} = \frac{-3b^2}{\sqrt{3}b} = -\sqrt{3}b$.

The area is decreasing at a rate of $\sqrt{3}b$ cm²/s.

3. Find the intervals in which the function $f$ given by $f(x) = \frac{4\sin x - 2x - x\cos x}{2+\cos x}$ is (i) increasing (ii) decreasing.

$f(x) = \frac{4\sin x}{2+\cos x} - \frac{2x + x\cos x}{2+\cos x} = \frac{4\sin x}{2+\cos x} - \frac{x(2 + \cos x)}{2+\cos x}$.

$f(x) = \frac{4\sin x}{2+\cos x} - x$.

Now, we find the derivative $f'(x)$. Let $g(x) = \frac{4\sin x}{2+\cos x}$. Then $f(x) = g(x) - x$.

$g'(x) = \frac{(2+\cos x)(4\cos x) - (4\sin x)(-\sin x)}{(2+\cos x)^2} = \frac{8\cos x + 4\cos^2 x + 4\sin^2 x}{(2+\cos x)^2} = \frac{8\cos x + 4}{(2+\cos x)^2}$.

So, $f'(x) = g'(x) - 1 = \frac{8\cos x + 4}{(2+\cos x)^2} - 1$.

$f'(x) = \frac{8\cos x + 4 - (2+\cos x)^2}{(2+\cos x)^2} = \frac{8\cos x + 4 - (4 + 4\cos x + \cos^2 x)}{(2+\cos x)^2}$.

$f'(x) = \frac{4\cos x - \cos^2 x}{(2+\cos x)^2} = \frac{\cos x(4 - \cos x)}{(2+\cos x)^2}$.

To determine the intervals of increase/decrease, we analyze the sign of $f'(x)$.

  • The denominator $(2+\cos x)^2$ is always positive since $-1 \le \cos x \le 1$, so $1 \le 2+\cos x \le 3$.
  • The term $(4 - \cos x)$ is always positive since the maximum value of $\cos x$ is 1.

Therefore, the sign of $f'(x)$ is determined solely by the sign of $\cos x$. Let's analyze over the interval $[0, 2\pi]$.

(i) Increasing function:

$f'(x) > 0 \implies \cos x > 0$.

This occurs when $x$ is in the first and fourth quadrants. So, $f(x)$ is increasing on $(0, \frac{\pi}{2}) \cup (\frac{3\pi}{2}, 2\pi)$.

(ii) Decreasing function:

$f'(x) < 0 \implies \cos x < 0$.

This occurs when $x$ is in the second and third quadrants. So, $f(x)$ is decreasing on $(\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{3\pi}{2})$.

4. Find the intervals in which the function $f(x)=x^3 + \frac{1}{x^3}$, $x \ne 0$ is (i) increasing (ii) decreasing.

Given $f(x) = x^3 + \frac{1}{x^3} = x^3 + x^{-3}$.

First, find the derivative:

$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3x^{-4} = 3x^2 - \frac{3}{x^4} = \frac{3x^6 - 3}{x^4} = \frac{3(x^6 - 1)}{x^4}$.

The sign of $f'(x)$ depends on the numerator, since the denominator $x^4$ is always positive for $x \ne 0$.

(i) Increasing function:

For $f(x)$ to be increasing, $f'(x) > 0$.

This requires $x^6 - 1 > 0 \implies x^6 > 1$.

Taking the sixth root, we get $|x| > 1$, which means $x > 1$ or $x < -1$.

So, $f(x)$ is increasing on $(-\infty, -1) \cup (1, \infty)$.

(ii) Decreasing function:

For $f(x)$ to be decreasing, $f'(x) < 0$.

This requires $x^6 - 1 < 0 \implies x^6 < 1$.

Taking the sixth root, we get $|x| < 1$. Since $x \ne 0$, this means $-1 < x < 0$ or $0 < x < 1$.

So, $f(x)$ is decreasing on $(-1, 0) \cup (0, 1)$.

5. Find the maximum area of an isosceles triangle inscribed in the ellipse $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ with its vertex at one end of the major axis.

Let the major axis be along the x-axis. The vertices of the ellipse are at $(\pm a, 0)$ and $(0, \pm b)$.

Let the vertex of the isosceles triangle be at one end of the major axis, say $A=(a, 0)$.

The base of the triangle will be a vertical chord of the ellipse, at some $x$-coordinate, say $x_0$. The other two vertices of the triangle are $B=(x_0, y_0)$ and $C=(x_0, -y_0)$, where $(x_0, y_0)$ is a point on the ellipse.

The length of the base BC is $2y_0$.

The height of the triangle is the perpendicular distance from vertex A to the base BC, which is $a - x_0$.

The area of the triangle is $\mathcal{A} = \frac{1}{2} \times (\text{base}) \times (\text{height}) = \frac{1}{2}(2y_0)(a - x_0) = y_0(a - x_0)$.

Since $(x_0, y_0)$ is on the ellipse, $\frac{x_0^2}{a^2} + \frac{y_0^2}{b^2} = 1 \implies y_0 = b\sqrt{1 - \frac{x_0^2}{a^2}} = \frac{b}{a}\sqrt{a^2 - x_0^2}$.

So, the area as a function of $x_0$ is $\mathcal{A}(x_0) = \frac{b}{a}\sqrt{a^2 - x_0^2}(a - x_0)$. Here $x_0 \in (-a, a)$.

To maximize the area, it is easier to maximize its square, $S(x_0) = \mathcal{A}(x_0)^2$.

$S(x_0) = \frac{b^2}{a^2}(a^2 - x_0^2)(a - x_0)^2 = \frac{b^2}{a^2}(a - x_0)(a + x_0)(a - x_0)^2 = \frac{b^2}{a^2}(a - x_0)^3(a + x_0)$.

Differentiating $S$ with respect to $x_0$ using the product rule:

$S'(x_0) = \frac{b^2}{a^2} \left[ 3(a - x_0)^2(-1)(a + x_0) + (a - x_0)^3(1) \right]$.

$S'(x_0) = \frac{b^2}{a^2}(a - x_0)^2 \left[ -3(a + x_0) + (a - x_0) \right]$.

$S'(x_0) = \frac{b^2}{a^2}(a - x_0)^2 [-3a - 3x_0 + a - x_0] = \frac{b^2}{a^2}(a - x_0)^2 (-2a - 4x_0)$.

Set $S'(x_0) = 0$. Since $x_0 \ne a$, we must have $-2a - 4x_0 = 0 \implies 4x_0 = -2a \implies x_0 = -\frac{a}{2}$.

(The second derivative is complicated, but a sign check on $S'(x_0)$ confirms this is a maximum).

Now, we find the maximum area using $x_0 = -a/2$.

Height = $a - x_0 = a - (-\frac{a}{2}) = \frac{3a}{2}$.

Base = $2y_0 = 2 \cdot \frac{b}{a}\sqrt{a^2 - (-\frac{a}{2})^2} = \frac{2b}{a}\sqrt{a^2 - \frac{a^2}{4}} = \frac{2b}{a}\sqrt{\frac{3a^2}{4}} = \frac{2b}{a} \frac{\sqrt{3}a}{2} = b\sqrt{3}$.

Maximum Area = $\frac{1}{2} \times (b\sqrt{3}) \times (\frac{3a}{2}) = \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{4}ab$.

6. A tank with rectangular base and rectangular sides, open at the top is to be constructed so that its depth is 2 m and volume is 8 m³. If building of tank costs Rs 70 per sq metres for the base and Rs 45 per square metre for sides. What is the cost of least expensive tank?

7. The sum of the perimeter of a circle and square is $k$, where $k$ is some constant. Prove that the sum of their areas is least when the side of square is double the radius of the circle.

Let $r$ be the radius of the circle and $a$ be the side of the square.

Perimeter of circle = $2\pi r$. Perimeter of square = $4a$.

Given: $2\pi r + 4a = k$ (where $k$ is a constant).

We can express $a$ in terms of $r$: $4a = k - 2\pi r \implies a = \frac{k - 2\pi r}{4}$.

The sum of their areas, $S$, is:

$S = (\text{Area of circle}) + (\text{Area of square}) = \pi r^2 + a^2$.

Substitute $a$ to get $S$ as a function of $r$:

$S(r) = \pi r^2 + \left(\frac{k - 2\pi r}{4}\right)^2 = \pi r^2 + \frac{1}{16}(k^2 - 4k\pi r + 4\pi^2 r^2)$.

To find the minimum sum of areas, we differentiate $S$ with respect to $r$:

$S'(r) = 2\pi r + \frac{1}{16}(0 - 4k\pi + 8\pi^2 r) = 2\pi r - \frac{4k\pi}{16} + \frac{8\pi^2 r}{16} = 2\pi r - \frac{k\pi}{4} + \frac{\pi^2 r}{2}$.

Set $S'(r) = 0$ to find the critical point:

$2\pi r + \frac{\pi^2 r}{2} = \frac{k\pi}{4}$.

Factor out $r$: $r\left(2\pi + \frac{\pi^2}{2}\right) = \frac{k\pi}{4}$.

$r\left(\frac{4\pi + \pi^2}{2}\right) = \frac{k\pi}{4} \implies r = \frac{k\pi}{4} \cdot \frac{2}{4\pi + \pi^2} = \frac{k\pi}{2\pi(4+\pi)} = \frac{k}{2(4+\pi)}$.

Check the second derivative:

$S''(r) = 2\pi + \frac{\pi^2}{2} > 0$ (since it's a sum of positive constants). This confirms a minimum.

Now we need to prove that the side of the square ($a$) is double the radius ($r$).

We have $2\pi r + 4a = k \implies 4a = k - 2\pi r$.

Substitute the value of $r$ we found: $4a = k - 2\pi\left(\frac{k}{2(4+\pi)}\right) = k - \frac{k\pi}{4+\pi} = \frac{k(4+\pi) - k\pi}{4+\pi} = \frac{4k + k\pi - k\pi}{4+\pi} = \frac{4k}{4+\pi}$.

$a = \frac{k}{4+\pi}$.

Now, let's check the relationship between $a$ and $r$:

$a = \frac{k}{4+\pi}$ and $r = \frac{k}{2(4+\pi)}$.

We can see that $a = 2 \cdot \frac{k}{2(4+\pi)} = 2r$.

Thus, the sum of the areas is least when the side of the square is double the radius of the circle. Hence proved.

9. A point on the hypotenuse of a triangle is at distance $a$ and $b$ from the sides of the triangle. Show that the minimum length of the hypotenuse is $(a^{2/3} + b^{2/3})^{3/2}$.

Let the right-angled triangle be ABC, with the right angle at B. Let the sides be along the axes, with B at the origin, A on the y-axis and C on the x-axis. So $A=(0, y_c)$ and $C=(x_c, 0)$.

Let $P(x,y)$ be the point on the hypotenuse AC. The distances from P to the sides are $x=a$ and $y=b$. So $P = (a, b)$.

Let $\theta$ be the angle the hypotenuse makes with the x-axis (angle BCA). So $\tan \theta = \frac{y_c}{x_c}$.

The length of the hypotenuse is $L = AC$. We can express $L$ in terms of segments. Let P divide AC into two segments. Draw perpendiculars from P to the axes. $P=(a,b)$.

From similar triangles, we can relate the parts. Let the hypotenuse be divided into two parts by the point P. Let these parts be $L_1$ and $L_2$. So $L = L_1 + L_2$.

The segment from C to P is $L_1$. We have $\sin\theta = \frac{b}{L_1} \implies L_1 = \frac{b}{\sin\theta}$.

The segment from P to A is $L_2$. We have $\cos\theta = \frac{a}{L_2} \implies L_2 = \frac{a}{\cos\theta}$.

The length of the hypotenuse is $L(\theta) = L_1 + L_2 = \frac{b}{\sin\theta} + \frac{a}{\cos\theta} = b\csc\theta + a\sec\theta$.

To minimize $L$, we find $L'(\theta)$ and set it to 0.

$L'(\theta) = -b\csc\theta\cot\theta + a\sec\theta\tan\theta = -\frac{b\cos\theta}{\sin^2\theta} + \frac{a\sin\theta}{\cos^2\theta}$.

Set $L'(\theta) = 0 \implies \frac{a\sin\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = \frac{b\cos\theta}{\sin^2\theta} \implies a\sin^3\theta = b\cos^3\theta \implies \tan^3\theta = \frac{b}{a} \implies \tan\theta = \left(\frac{b}{a}\right)^{1/3}$.

Now we find $\sin\theta$ and $\cos\theta$ from this value of $\tan\theta$. Let $\tan\theta = \frac{b^{1/3}}{a^{1/3}}$. Opposite side = $b^{1/3}$, Adjacent side = $a^{1/3}$. Hypotenuse of this construction triangle is $\sqrt{(a^{1/3})^2 + (b^{1/3})^2} = \sqrt{a^{2/3} + b^{2/3}}$.

So, $\sin\theta = \frac{b^{1/3}}{\sqrt{a^{2/3} + b^{2/3}}}$ and $\cos\theta = \frac{a^{1/3}}{\sqrt{a^{2/3} + b^{2/3}}}$.

Substitute these into the expression for $L$ to find the minimum length:

$L_{min} = \frac{b}{\sin\theta} + \frac{a}{\cos\theta} = \frac{b}{\frac{b^{1/3}}{\sqrt{a^{2/3} + b^{2/3}}}} + \frac{a}{\frac{a^{1/3}}{\sqrt{a^{2/3} + b^{2/3}}}}$.

$L_{min} = b^{2/3}\sqrt{a^{2/3} + b^{2/3}} + a^{2/3}\sqrt{a^{2/3} + b^{2/3}}$.

$L_{min} = (a^{2/3} + b^{2/3})\sqrt{a^{2/3} + b^{2/3}} = (a^{2/3} + b^{2/3})^{1} (a^{2/3} + b^{2/3})^{1/2} = (a^{2/3} + b^{2/3})^{3/2}$.

Checking the second derivative confirms this is a minimum. Hence proved.

12. Show that the altitude of the right circular cone of maximum volume that can be inscribed in a sphere of radius $r$ is $\frac{4r}{3}$.

This is a rephrasing of Exercise 6.3, Q23, with the sphere's radius denoted by $r$ instead of $R$. The setup and calculation are identical.

Let the sphere be centered at the origin. Let the cone be inscribed with its vertex at $(0, r)$ and base on a plane $y=y_0$.

The altitude of the cone is $h = r - y_0$. So $y_0 = r - h$.

The radius of the cone's base, let's call it $R_{cone}$, is the $x$-coordinate on the sphere at height $y_0$. $R_{cone}^2 + y_0^2 = r^2 \implies R_{cone}^2 = r^2 - (r-h)^2 = r^2 - (r^2 - 2rh + h^2) = 2rh - h^2$.

The volume of the cone is $V = \frac{1}{3}\pi R_{cone}^2 h$.

$V(h) = \frac{1}{3}\pi (2rh - h^2)h = \frac{\pi}{3}(2rh^2 - h^3)$.

$V'(h) = \frac{\pi}{3}(4rh - 3h^2) = \frac{\pi h}{3}(4r - 3h)$.

Set $V'(h)=0$. Since $h \ne 0$, we have $4r - 3h = 0 \implies h = \frac{4r}{3}$.

The second derivative $V''(h) = \frac{\pi}{3}(4r - 6h)$. At $h=\frac{4r}{3}$, $V''(h) < 0$, confirming a maximum.

Thus, the altitude of the cone of maximum volume is $\frac{4r}{3}$. Hence proved.

13. Let $f$ be a function defined on $[a, b]$ such that $f'(x) > 0$, for all $x \in (a, b)$. Then prove that $f$ is an increasing function on $(a, b)$.

This is a direct application of the Mean Value Theorem (MVT) and the definition of an increasing function.

Let $x_1, x_2$ be any two points in the interval $(a, b)$ such that $x_1 < x_2$.

Since $f$ is differentiable on $(a, b)$, it is also continuous on $(a, b)$. Therefore, we can apply the Mean Value Theorem to the function $f$ on the closed interval $[x_1, x_2]$.

According to MVT, there exists some point $c$ in the open interval $(x_1, x_2)$ such that:

$f'(c) = \frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1}$.

We can rewrite this as: $f(x_2) - f(x_1) = f'(c)(x_2 - x_1)$.

We are given that $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x \in (a, b)$. Since $c \in (x_1, x_2) \subset (a, b)$, we have $f'(c) > 0$.

Also, since we chose $x_1 < x_2$, we have $(x_2 - x_1) > 0$.

The product of two positive numbers is positive, so $f'(c)(x_2 - x_1) > 0$.

This implies $f(x_2) - f(x_1) > 0$, which means $f(x_2) > f(x_1)$.

So, we have shown that for any $x_1, x_2 \in (a, b)$ with $x_1 < x_2$, we have $f(x_1) < f(x_2)$.

This is the definition of a strictly increasing function. Thus, $f$ is an increasing function on $(a,b)$. Hence proved.

14. Show that the height of the cylinder of maximum volume that can be inscribed in a sphere of radius $R$ is $\frac{2R}{\sqrt{3}}$. Also find the maximum volume.

Let a cylinder with radius $r$ and height $h$ be inscribed in a sphere of radius $R$.

Let the center of the sphere be at the origin. By Pythagoras' theorem on a cross-section, the relationship between the dimensions is:

$r^2 + \left(\frac{h}{2}\right)^2 = R^2$.

The volume of the cylinder is $V = \pi r^2 h$.

From the Pythagorean relation, $r^2 = R^2 - \frac{h^2}{4}$. Substitute this into the volume formula to get $V$ as a function of $h$.

$V(h) = \pi\left(R^2 - \frac{h^2}{4}\right)h = \pi\left(R^2h - \frac{h^3}{4}\right)$. The height $h \in (0, 2R)$.

To find the maximum volume, differentiate with respect to $h$:

$V'(h) = \pi\left(R^2 - \frac{3h^2}{4}\right)$.

Set $V'(h) = 0 \implies R^2 - \frac{3h^2}{4} = 0 \implies \frac{3h^2}{4} = R^2 \implies h^2 = \frac{4R^2}{3} \implies h = \frac{2R}{\sqrt{3}}$ (since height is positive).

Check the second derivative: $V''(h) = \pi\left(-\frac{6h}{4}\right) = -\frac{3\pi h}{2}$. Since $h>0$, $V''(h) < 0$, which confirms a maximum.

Thus, the height of the cylinder of maximum volume is $h = \frac{2R}{\sqrt{3}}$.

Now, find the maximum volume by substituting this value of $h$ back into $V(h)$:

$V_{max} = \pi\left(R^2\left(\frac{2R}{\sqrt{3}}\right) - \frac{1}{4}\left(\frac{2R}{\sqrt{3}}\right)^3\right) = \pi\left(\frac{2R^3}{\sqrt{3}} - \frac{1}{4}\frac{8R^3}{3\sqrt{3}}\right)$.

$V_{max} = \pi\left(\frac{2R^3}{\sqrt{3}} - \frac{2R^3}{3\sqrt{3}}\right) = \pi\left(\frac{6R^3 - 2R^3}{3\sqrt{3}}\right) = \frac{4\pi R^3}{3\sqrt{3}}$.

The maximum volume is $\frac{4\pi R^3}{3\sqrt{3}}$.

15. Show that height of the cylinder of greatest volume which can be inscribed in a right circular cone of height $h$ and semi-vertical angle $\alpha$ is one-third that of the cone and the greatest volume of cylinder is $\frac{4}{27}\pi h^3 \tan^2\alpha$.

Let the cone have vertex at the origin, height $h$ along the y-axis, and radius $R$. We have $\tan\alpha = \frac{R}{h}$, so $R = h\tan\alpha$.

Let a cylinder be inscribed in the cone with radius $r_{cyl}$ and height $h_{cyl}$.

Consider the cross-section. By similar triangles (the large cone and the small cone above the cylinder):

The small cone above the cylinder has height $h - h_{cyl}$ and radius $r_{cyl}$.

The ratio of radius to height is constant: $\frac{r_{cyl}}{h - h_{cyl}} = \frac{R}{h} = \tan\alpha$.

From this, $r_{cyl} = (h - h_{cyl})\tan\alpha$.

The volume of the cylinder is $V = \pi r_{cyl}^2 h_{cyl}$.

Substitute $r_{cyl}$ to get $V$ as a function of $h_{cyl}$ (let's denote it by $H$ for simplicity):

$V(H) = \pi [(h - H)\tan\alpha]^2 H = \pi \tan^2\alpha (h - H)^2 H$.

$V(H) = \pi \tan^2\alpha (h^2 - 2hH + H^2)H = \pi \tan^2\alpha (h^2H - 2hH^2 + H^3)$. Here $H \in (0, h)$.

To maximize, find the derivative with respect to $H$:

$V'(H) = \pi \tan^2\alpha (h^2 - 4hH + 3H^2)$.

Set $V'(H)=0 \implies h^2 - 4hH + 3H^2 = 0$. This is a quadratic in $H$.

$(3H - h)(H - h) = 0$.

Solutions are $H = \frac{h}{3}$ or $H=h$. Since $H \in (0,h)$, we must have $H = \frac{h}{3}$.

Check the second derivative: $V''(H) = \pi \tan^2\alpha (-4h + 6H)$.

$V''(\frac{h}{3}) = \pi \tan^2\alpha (-4h + 6(\frac{h}{3})) = \pi \tan^2\alpha (-4h + 2h) = -2h\pi\tan^2\alpha < 0$, which confirms a maximum.

So, the height of the cylinder of greatest volume is one-third the height of the cone. First part proved.

Now find the greatest volume. Substitute $H = \frac{h}{3}$ back into the volume formula:

$V_{max} = \pi \tan^2\alpha \left(h - \frac{h}{3}\right)^2 \left(\frac{h}{3}\right) = \pi \tan^2\alpha \left(\frac{2h}{3}\right)^2 \left(\frac{h}{3}\right)$.

$V_{max} = \pi \tan^2\alpha \left(\frac{4h^2}{9}\right) \left(\frac{h}{3}\right) = \frac{4\pi h^3 \tan^2\alpha}{27}$.

The greatest volume of the cylinder is $\frac{4}{27}\pi h^3 \tan^2\alpha$. Second part proved.
