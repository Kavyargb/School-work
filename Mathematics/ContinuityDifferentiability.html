1. Prove that the function \(f(x) = 5x - 3\) is continuous at \(x = 0\), at \(x = -3\) and at \(x = 5\).

A function \(f(x)\) is continuous at a point \(c\) if \( \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c) \).

At \(x = 0\):

  • Function value: \(f(0) = 5(0) - 3 = -3\)
  • Limit: \( \lim_{x \to 0} (5x - 3) = 5(0) - 3 = -3 \)

Since \( \lim_{x \to 0} f(x) = f(0) \), the function is continuous at \(x = 0\).

At \(x = -3\):

  • Function value: \(f(-3) = 5(-3) - 3 = -15 - 3 = -18\)
  • Limit: \( \lim_{x \to -3} (5x - 3) = 5(-3) - 3 = -18 \)

Since \( \lim_{x \to -3} f(x) = f(-3) \), the function is continuous at \(x = -3\).

At \(x = 5\):

  • Function value: \(f(5) = 5(5) - 3 = 25 - 3 = 22\)
  • Limit: \( \lim_{x \to 5} (5x - 3) = 5(5) - 3 = 22 \)

Since \( \lim_{x \to 5} f(x) = f(5) \), the function is continuous at \(x = 5\).

3. Examine the following functions for continuity.

(a) \(f(x) = x - 5\)

This is a polynomial function. Polynomial functions are continuous everywhere.

Let \(c\) be any real number.

  • Function value: \(f(c) = c - 5\)
  • Limit: \( \lim_{x \to c} (x - 5) = c - 5 \)

Since \( \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c) \), the function is continuous for all real numbers.

(b) \(f(x) = \frac{1}{x-5}, x \neq 5\)

This is a rational function. Rational functions are continuous at all points in their domain.

The domain of \(f(x)\) is all real numbers except \(x = 5\).

Let \(c\) be any real number such that \(c \neq 5\).

  • Function value: \(f(c) = \frac{1}{c-5}\)
  • Limit: \( \lim_{x \to c} \frac{1}{x-5} = \frac{1}{c-5} \)

Since \( \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c) \) for all \(c \neq 5\), the function is continuous throughout its domain.

(c) \(f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 25}{x+5}, x \neq -5\)

This function can be simplified:

$$f(x) = \frac{(x-5)(x+5)}{x+5} = x-5 \quad \text{for } x \neq -5$$

Since \(f(x)\) is defined as \(x-5\) for \(x \neq -5\), it is a polynomial function within its domain. Polynomial functions are continuous everywhere.

The domain of \(f(x)\) is all real numbers except \(x = -5\).

Let \(c\) be any real number such that \(c \neq -5\).

  • Function value: \(f(c) = c-5\)
  • Limit: \( \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = \lim_{x \to c} (x-5) = c-5 \)

Since \( \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c) \) for all \(c \neq -5\), the function is continuous throughout its domain.

(d) \(f(x) = |x - 5|\)

This is a modulus function, which can be written as a piecewise function:

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} -(x-5), & \text{if } x < 5 \\ x-5, & \text{if } x \geq 5 \end{cases}$$

For \(x < 5\), \(f(x) = -(x-5) = -x+5\), which is a polynomial and thus continuous.

For \(x > 5\), \(f(x) = x-5\), which is a polynomial and thus continuous.

We need to check continuity at the point where the definition changes, i.e., at \(x = 5\).

At \(x = 5\):

  • Function value: \(f(5) = 5-5 = 0\)
  • Left-hand limit: \( \lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 5^-} -(x-5) = -(5-5) = 0 \)
  • Right-hand limit: \( \lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 5^+} (x-5) = 5-5 = 0 \)

Since \( \lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x) = f(5) = 0 \), the function is continuous at \(x = 5\).

Thus, \(f(x) = |x-5|\) is continuous for all real numbers.

For \(h < 0\), \(1+h < 1\), so \(f(1+h) = -(1+h-1) = -h\).

\(f(1) = |1-1| = 0\).

$$ LHD = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{-h - 0}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^-} (-1) = -1 $$

$$ \frac{d}{dx}(u \cdot v \cdot w) = \frac{du}{dx} (v \cdot w) + u \left(\frac{dv}{dx} w + v \frac{dw}{dx}\right) $$ $$ \frac{d}{dx}(u \cdot v \cdot w) = \frac{du}{dx} v w + u \frac{dv}{dx} w + u v \frac{dw}{dx} $$

This proves the formula by repeated application of the product rule.

(ii) By logarithmic differentiation:

Let \(y = u \cdot v \cdot w\).

Take the natural logarithm of both sides:

$$ \log y = \log (u \cdot v \cdot w) $$ $$ \log y = \log u + \log v + \log w $$

Now, differentiate implicitly with respect to \(x\):

$$ \frac{1}{y} \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{u} \frac{du}{dx} + \frac{1}{v} \frac{dv}{dx} + \frac{1}{w} \frac{dw}{dx} $$

Multiply both sides by \(y\):

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = y \left( \frac{1}{u} \frac{du}{dx} + \frac{1}{v} \frac{dv}{dx} + \frac{1}{w} \frac{dw}{dx} \right) $$

Substitute \(y = u \cdot v \cdot w\):

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = (u \cdot v \cdot w) \left( \frac{1}{u} \frac{du}{dx} + \frac{1}{v} \frac{dv}{dx} + \frac{1}{w} \frac{dw}{dx} \right) $$

Distribute \((u \cdot v \cdot w)\) to each term inside the parenthesis:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{u v w}{u} \frac{du}{dx} + \frac{u v w}{v} \frac{dv}{dx} + \frac{u v w}{w} \frac{dw}{dx} $$ $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = v w \frac{du}{dx} + u w \frac{dv}{dx} + u v \frac{dw}{dx} $$

Rearranging the terms:

$$ \frac{d}{dx}(u \cdot v \cdot w) = \frac{du}{dx} v w + u \frac{dv}{dx} w + u v \frac{dw}{dx} $$

This proves the formula using logarithmic differentiation.

Differentiate the equation of the circle with respect to \(x\):

$$ 2(x-a) + 2(y-b)y' = 0 \implies (y-b)y' = -(x-a) \implies y' = -\frac{x-a}{y-b} $$

Differentiate again with respect to \(x\) using the quotient rule:

$$ y'' = -\frac{(1)(y-b) - (x-a)(y')}{(y-b)^2} $$ Substitute \(y'\): $$ y'' = -\frac{y-b - (x-a)(-\frac{x-a}{y-b})}{(y-b)^2} = -\frac{y-b + \frac{(x-a)^2}{y-b}}{(y-b)^2} $$ $$ y'' = -\frac{\frac{(y-b)^2+(x-a)^2}{y-b}}{(y-b)^2} = -\frac{(y-b)^2+(x-a)^2}{(y-b)^3} $$

Since \((x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = c^2\):

$$ y'' = -\frac{c^2}{(y-b)^3} $$

$$ f'(x) = \begin{cases} 3x^2, & \text{if } x > 0 \\ -3x^2, & \text{if } x < 0 \end{cases} $$

Check for differentiability at \(x=0\): LHD at 0: \(\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{f(h)-f(0)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{-h^3}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^-}-h^2=0\). RHD at 0: \(\lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{f(h)-f(0)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^+}\frac{h^3}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^+}h^2=0\). Since LHD=RHD=0, \(f'(0)=0\). So, \(f'(x)\) exists for all \(x\) and can be written as \(f'(x)=3x|x|\).

Find the second derivative, \(f''(x)\):

$$ f''(x) = \begin{cases} 6x, & \text{if } x > 0 \\ -6x, & \text{if } x < 0 \end{cases} $$

Check for differentiability of \(f'(x)\) at \(x=0\): LHD of \(f'\) at 0: \(\lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{f'(h)-f'(0)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^-}\frac{-3h^2-0}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^-}-3h=0\). 19. Using the fact that \(\sin(A+B) = \sin A \cos B + \cos A \sin B\) and differentiation, obtain the sum formula for cosines.

Treat \(A\) as a variable, say \(x\), and \(B\) as a constant.

$$ \sin(x+B) = \sin x \cos B + \cos x \sin B $$

Differentiate both sides with respect to \(x\):

$$ \frac{d}{dx}(\sin(x+B)) = \frac{d}{dx}(\sin x \cos B + \cos x \sin B) $$

Using the chain rule on the left side:

$$ \cos(x+B) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(x+B) = \cos(x+B) \cdot 1 = \cos(x+B) $$

Differentiating the right side (where \(\cos B\) and \(\sin B\) are constants):

$$ \cos x \cos B - \sin x \sin B $$

Equating the results:

$$ \cos(x+B) = \cos x \cos B - \sin x \sin B $$

Replacing \(x\) with \(A\) gives the sum formula for cosine:

$$ \cos(A+B) = \cos A \cos B - \sin A \sin B $$

20. Does there exist a function which is continuous everywhere but not differentiable at exactly two points? Justify your answer.

Yes, such a function exists.

Justification: Consider the function \(f(x) = |x-1| + |x+1|\).

Continuity: The function \(g(x)=|x|\) is continuous everywhere. The functions \(h(x)=x-1\) and \(k(x)=x+1\) are polynomials and are continuous everywhere. Therefore, the composite functions \(|x-1|\) and \(|x+1|\) are continuous everywhere. The sum of two continuous functions is continuous, so \(f(x)\) is continuous for all real numbers.

Differentiability: Let's analyze the function in a piecewise manner:

  • For \(x < -1\): \(f(x) = -(x-1) - (x+1) = -2x\). So, \(f'(x) = -2\).
  • For \(-1 < x < 1\): \(f(x) = -(x-1) + (x+1) = 2\). So, \(f'(x) = 0\).
  • For \(x > 1\): \(f(x) = (x-1) + (x+1) = 2x\). So, \(f'(x) = 2\).

At \(x=-1\), the left-hand derivative is -2 and the right-hand derivative is 0. Since they are not equal, \(f(x)\) is not differentiable at \(x=-1\).

At \(x=1\), the left-hand derivative is 0 and the right-hand derivative is 2. Since they are not equal, \(f(x)\) is not differentiable at \(x=1\).

For all other points, the function is defined by a simple polynomial and is differentiable.

Thus, \(f(x)=|x-1|+|x+1|\) is a function that is continuous everywhere but not differentiable at exactly two points, \(x=-1\) and \(x=1\).

21. If \(y = \begin{vmatrix} f(x) & g(x) & h(x) \\ l & m & n \\ a & b & c \end{vmatrix}\), prove that \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \begin{vmatrix} f'(x) & g'(x) & h'(x) \\ l & m & n \\ a & b & c \end{vmatrix}\).

Given that \(l, m, n, a, b, c\) are constants, we can expand the determinant for \(y\):

$$ y = f(x)(mc - nb) - g(x)(lc - na) + h(x)(lb - ma) $$

This is an expression where \(f(x), g(x), h(x)\) are multiplied by constants. The coefficients are: \(C_1 = mc - nb\), \(C_2 = -(lc-na) = na - lc\), \(C_3 = lb - ma\).

$$ y = C_1 f(x) + C_2 g(x) + C_3 h(x) $$

Differentiate \(y\) with respect to \(x\):

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = C_1 f'(x) + C_2 g'(x) + C_3 h'(x) $$ $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = (mc - nb)f'(x) - (lc - na)g'(x) + (lb - ma)h'(x) $$

This expression is the expansion of the determinant:

$$ \begin{vmatrix} f'(x) & g'(x) & h'(x) \\ l & m & n \\ a & b & c \end{vmatrix} $$

Hence, the result is proven.

22. If \(y = e^{a\cos^{-1}x}, -1 \leq x \leq 1\), show that \((1-x^2)\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - x\frac{dy}{dx} - a^2y = 0\).

Given: \(y = e^{a\cos^{-1}x}\).

First derivative:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = e^{a\cos^{-1}x} \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(a\cos^{-1}x) = y \cdot a\left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\right) = -\frac{ay}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} $$

Rearrange the equation:

$$ \sqrt{1-x^2} \frac{dy}{dx} = -ay $$

Square both sides:

$$ (1-x^2)\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2 = a^2y^2 $$

Differentiate again with respect to \(x\) using the product rule:

$$ \frac{d}{dx}\left[(1-x^2)\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2\right] = \frac{d}{dx}(a^2y^2) $$ $$ (-2x)\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2 + (1-x^2) \cdot 2\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = a^2 \cdot 2y \frac{dy}{dx} $$

Since \(y=e^{a\cos^{-1}x}\) is never zero, \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) is not identically zero. We can divide by \(2\frac{dy}{dx}\):

$$ -x\frac{dy}{dx} + (1-x^2)\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = a^2y $$

Rearranging the terms gives the required differential equation:

$$ (1-x^2)\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} - x\frac{dy}{dx} - a^2y = 0 $$
